# City riding arena

*Börsenplatz 4*

![Bird's-eye view drawing of the municipal riding arena](../../images/a4236eeadb7d30760ac04be2108e7bb9_original.jpg)
*1835 — Author: Carl Theodor Reiffenstein | License: HMF R158 | Description: Bird's-eye view drawing of the municipal riding arena*

The citizens of Frankfurt did not immediately take to the barricades in the first days of the 1848 revolutions. Initially, reforms were achieved through political petitions. To begin with on 3 March 1848, a popular assembly of 2000 people at Frankfurt’s riding arena once on this site drafted a petition by acclamation with eight demands. Over the following days in March, the Senate then granted the freedom of the press and assembly for the free city of Frankfurt, released political opponents from prison, modernised the legal system with jury courts, and improved the position of farmers and smallholders in the surrounding villages. However, it rejected the call for the equality of all citizens irrespective of their religion. The Senate also instructed the legislative assembly to reform the current municipal constitution. This process of reform continued in the city until January 1850.

On 14 May 1848, the riding arena was also the venue of another gathering with over 1000 people. At this meeting, the Frankfurt Workers’ Association was founded. The chair was taken by Christian Esselen, a former officer from Hamm, while Eduard Pelz, a bookseller and publisher from Saxony, was appointed as Secretary. Frankfurt’s bourgeoisie and the craft masters were highly suspicious of the Workers’ Association’s activities. In response to a petition with 631 signatures, the Senate ordered Esselen and Pelz to leave Frankfurt on 24 May 1848, severely limiting the association’s sphere of influence in the city.
