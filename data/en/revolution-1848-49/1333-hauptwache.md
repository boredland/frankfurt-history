# Hauptwache

*An der Hauptwache 15*

![Color lithograph depicting the exchange of fire between attackers and defenders of the Hauptwache](../../images/7bcd953cacd1ceb07826af576e1e6d59_original.jpg)
*ca. 1833 — Author: Francois Georgin | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7Z Nr. 1833-2 | Description: Color lithograph depicting the exchange of fire between attackers and defenders of the Hauptwache*

The Main Guardhouse, the Hauptwache, dates from 1729–1730. In the 19th century, it served as a municipal police station and a prison. On 3 April 1833, in the pre-March period preceding the 1848 revolutions, the guard houses here and on the Konstablerwache square were attacked by around 100 radical liberals and students. In this action, now known as the ‘Charge of the Frankfurt Guardhouses’ – the Frankfurter Wachensturm – the radicals and students even managed the occupy the buildings for a time. They had hoped this would trigger an armed revolution across the German states, but without mass support the uprising was crushed in an hour.

The German Confederation, an organisation of 39 German states, reacted with a series of harsh measures. As a demonstration of its power, it stationed 3,000 soldiers in Frankfurt. By 1842, it had also charged 2,000 people with allegedly taking part in the attacks. Some of those arrested also received long prison sentences, including the notary Friedrich Siegmund Jucho, later Frankfurt’s representative in the National Assembly. He was held in prison until 1838 for the distribution of revolutionary writings. Yet despite such repressive measures, the liberal movement remained active until 1848.
