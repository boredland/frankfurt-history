# Field hospital in the Bürgerhospital

*Stiftstraße 30*

![Colored lithograph: Barricade at the Bornheim Gate](../../images/0f665d670f936bcd1daa804d65209abb_original.jpg)
*1848 — Author: Carl Friedrich Fay | License: HMF C 10164c | Description: Colored lithograph: Barricade at the Bornheim Gate*

In September 1848, Frankfurt had just two hospitals to care for the many wounded in the uprising – the Hospital of the Holy Spirit in Lange Strasse and the Bürgerhospital, originally close to the Eschenheim Gate. The wounded from the fierce battles at the Bleichstrasse barricade were taken to the Bürgerhospital, founded under an endowment from Johann Christian Senckenberg. All the other wounded were treated in the Hospital of the Holy Spirit.

When the troops started to storm the Bleichstrasse barricade on 18 September 1848, an administrative member of the Dr. Senckenberg Foundation hurried to the scene to ensure the wounded on both sides could be taken to the Bürgerhospital.

After exchanging fire for hours in Bleichstrasse, the sad outcome was seven insurgents with gunshot wounds and 32 wounded soldiers. Five severely injured soldiers and two civilians died of their wounds. The revolutionaries taken to hospital included a day labourer, who was shot in the stomach and soon died from internal bleeding, a rag and bone man, three craft apprentices, a master slater, and a maidservant with a slight hand wound from a grazing shot.

On the days after the uprising, the soldiers from Prussia, Austria and Electoral Hesse in the Bürgerhospital had some eminent visitors: the imperial regent Archduke John, minister of war Eduard von Peucker and high-ranking members of the army paid a flying visit to those in hospital and ‘attested to their satisfaction with the medical and surgical care and the good nursing and food’.
