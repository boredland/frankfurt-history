# Deutscher Hof (centre-left)

*Goethestraße 10*

![Lithograph depicting the "Deutscher Hof" building](../../images/6a01c10a2689e4d1cac20b94aa0b356d_original.jpg)
*1863 — Author: W. Loeillot | License: HMF C68280 | Description: Lithograph depicting the "Deutscher Hof" building*

The National Assembly’s first session on 18 May 1848 saw the formation of a democratic centre-left faction. Under the leadership of Robert Blum, the group numbered around 100 people and met in the Deutscher Hof Hotel. According to reports, the meetings were held in a convivial pub-like atmosphere, without a specific agenda and, at times, with very intensive discussions.

Apart from Robert Blum, the best-known parliamentarians in the group included the Mainz politician Johann Adam von Itzstein; Königsberg physician Johann Jacoby; Speyer publisher Georg Friedrich Kolb; Cologne journalist Franz Raveaux; Bavarian lawyer Friedrich Schüler; Giessen natural scientist Carl Vogt; and the Dresden physician Franz Jacob Wigard.

The members of the Deutscher Hof faction called for a democratic republic with a directly elected unicameral parliament based on the sovereignty of the people and universal and equal suffrage. They argued for a strong parliament and anchoring fundamental rights in the constitution.

Yet in terms of its political agenda, the Deutsche Hof brought together contradictory centre-left positions and views. As a result, on 27 May 1848 the more radical and left-wing delegates formed the Donnersberg group. In August 1848, the Westenhall faction was the product of a second split. These delegates, derided as ‘Linke im Frack’ – ‘frock-coated left wingers’ – found the Deutscher Hof positions too radical, and they left to create a new faction with parliamentarians from the Württemberger Hof group. The remaining Deutscher Hof faction underwent a third split in October that same year. In this case, the disapproval over Robert Blum’s interference in Austria’s political affairs led some members to join the Nürnberger Hof faction. After this series of splits, the Deutscher Hof group was reduced to around 45 members, including eleven Austrian and six Prussian parliamentarians. The majority were moderate democrats from Germany’s middling states such as Baden, Saxony, Württemberg and Bavaria.

In October 1848, in reaction to these splits, the Deutscher Hof group revised its political aims. From then on, the faction supported the right of the single sovereign states to agree on their own constitutions. At the same time, it also advocated limiting their authority in favour of a centralised power, and rejected a hereditary Prussian emperor. The members envisaged a greater German solution, but without the non-German parts of Prussia and Austria. In principle, the faction planned to achieve the implementation of the constitution solely through parliamentary means.
