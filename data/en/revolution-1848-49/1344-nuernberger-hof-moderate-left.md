# Nürnberger Hof (moderate left)

*Hinter dem Lämmchen 8*

![Drawing of the "Nürnberger Hof" (Nuremberg Court)](../../images/62d767cb915daa5443439815d96a1c19_original.jpg)
*1849 — Author: Carl Theodor Reiffenstein | License: HMF R0404 | Description: Drawing of the "Nürnberger Hof" (Nuremberg Court)*

In October 1848 revolutionary fighting broke out in Vienna and Robert Blum, one of the leading figures in the National Assembly, led a delegation to the city to mediate. Some parliamentarians disapproving of this move split from the right wing of the Deutscher Hof group to form the moderate liberal Nürnberger Hof faction. Despite the split, the Nürnberger Hof group still largely followed the same political programme. The leading moderates in the group included the Saxony factory owner Bernhard Eisenstuck; Speyer publisher and writer Georg Friedrich Kolb; Magdeburg physician Wilhelm Loewe; and Leipzig natural scientist Emil Adolf Roßmäßler.

The Nürnberger Hof was also the name of another centre left parliamentary group. This faction formed in May 1849 with former members from the Weidenbusch group, which advocated a hereditary German emperor, and from the Württemberger Hof, Westendhall, Landsberg and Augsburger Hof groups. This second Nürnberger Hof faction included such figures as the Leipzig philosopher Karl Biedermann and Hamburg lawyer Gabriel Riesser, later the first Jew to be named a judge in Germany.

The Nürnberger Hof was a courtyard and complex of buildings originally named after the Nuremberg merchants staying there during trade fairs or using it for their stores and trading offices. Today, only traces of the Nürnberger Hof remain in Frankfurt’s cityscape. Part of it was demolished to make way for Braubachstrasse in 1904 to 1906. Other parts were badly damaged in the bombing raids in 1944 and then torn down or cleared for the building programme from 1952 to 1955.
