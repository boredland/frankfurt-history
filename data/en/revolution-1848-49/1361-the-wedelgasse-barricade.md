# The Wedelgasse barricade

*Römerberg 27*

![Chalk lithography: Everyone pitches in – building barricades on Wedelgasse](../../images/d1c3444293b9062c8483e850266e79e6_original.jpg)
*1848 — Author: Ludwig von Elliot | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. W 1-9 ZGS Nr. 36-250 | Description: Chalk lithography: Everyone pitches in – building barricades on Wedelgasse*

The first barricade went up at the junction of Wedelgasse (today: Bethmannstrasse), Neuer Kräme and the Römerberg. Wooden booths were set up on the Römerberg square for the autumn fair, and they served the revolutionaries as material for the barricades, along with anything else they could lay their hands on. The scene in the lithograph not only shows citizens in tails and top hats helping out, but some street urchins as well. Evidently the work is being coordinated by a radical with a broad felt hat and a sabre and a worker in a ‘barricade cap’ with a pickaxe in his hand.

As Carl Vogt, a left-wing delegate in the National Assembly, recalled: ‘Those building the barricades, mostly street urchins or apprentices between 16 and 18 years old, tore down the booths, ripped up the cobbles, and piled up the boxes for the fair by emptying some of them and filling them with stones. The people there for the fair contented themselves with bundling up their wares; the rest of the general public watched the goings-on as if it were a play and, with one or two exceptions, neither joined in nor tried to stop it.’

The aim was to turn the eastern historic city into a fortress with total of 50 barricades, but the smaller roadblocks were soon abandoned again. Only the main barricades were bitterly defended.
