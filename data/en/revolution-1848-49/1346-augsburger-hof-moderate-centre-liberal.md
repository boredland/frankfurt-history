# Augsburger Hof (moderate centre-liberal)

*Hasengasse 5-7 (Kleinmarkthalle)*

![Photo of Vogelgesangsgasse with a view of the Augsburger Hof.](../../images/e8b7cb9dbd224b45b76f701c0c348a8d_original.jpg)
*1915 — Author: Carl Andreas Abt | License: HMF C23217 | Description: Photo of Vogelgesangsgasse with a view of the Augsburger Hof.*

Due to differences over the Truce of Malmö, the Augsburger Hof group split from the Württemberger Hof in September 1848. This new group of moderate liberal delegates regularly met in the wine tavern close to the Trierischen Plätzchen square, south of the Töngesgasse.

In his memoirs, Robert von Mohl offered an insight into the course of the group’s meetings. This faction had around 40 members and met in the back room of the smoky and rather modest tavern. After dining together, the members conferred on the items on the agenda and the approach they should adopt for the following day’s session. They then agreed on the group’s stance on issues and the selection of speakers in the National Assembly.

The Augsburger Hof faction included Leipzig philosopher Karl Biedermann; Hesse judge August Emmerling; Stuttgart theologian and librarian August Friedrich Gfrörer; Heidelberg legal scholar Carl Joseph Anton Mittermaier, the preliminary parliament president; Tübingen constitutional expert Robert von Mohl, minister of justice from 1848–1849; Hamburg lawyer Gabriel Riesser, later the first Jew to be named a judge in Germany; Württemberg educationalist and university vice-chancellor Gustav von Rümelin; Bavarian lawyer Gustav von Schlör; Oppenheim vintner Philipp Wilhelm Wernher; and Adolf von Zerzog, a large landowner from Regensburg.

On constitutional issues, the Augsburger Hof group adopted much of the Württemberger Hof’s policies. In addition, its members advocated a parliamentary democracy with a strong centralised power, as well as the lesser German solution with an imperial Germany and hereditary succession.
