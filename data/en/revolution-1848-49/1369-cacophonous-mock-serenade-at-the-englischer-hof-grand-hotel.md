# ‘Cacophonous mock serenade’ at the Englischer Hof Grand Hotel

*Roßmarkt 13*

![Photo: The English Court at Roßmarkt](../../images/6e022d1f52b09bc98238b7c477a34780_original.jpg)
*1878 (or 1890) — Author: Karl Hertel | License: gemeinfrei | Description: Photo: The English Court at Roßmarkt*

After the National Assembly narrowly voted to accept the Truce of Malmö on Saturday, 16 September 1848, the first riots in Frankfurt began that same evening. In front of the Englischer Hof Grand Hotel on Rossmarkt, an angry mob gave a cacophonous mock serenade, hooting and howling, leading to violent scuffles and damage to property. Thinking Johann Gustav Heckscher, the National Assembly’s foreign minister, was staying in the hotel, the mob threatened to kill him. The situation only calmed down when the citizens’ militia cleared the square.

The unrest was sparked by the Schleswig-Holstein question. The duchies of Schleswig and Holstein both belonged in personal union to the Danish kings. Holstein, though, was part of the German Confederation while Schleswig, whose population was both German and Danish, legally remained a Danish fief. The movement for German national unification was calling for the duchy of Schleswig to join the Confederation. When Schleswig-Holstein’s German majority declared its independence from Denmark with a self-appointed government in Kiel, the conflict turned into an open uprising against Denmark. In April 1848, the Confederation asked Prussia to intervene and send troops to support the rebels.

Under international pressure, Prussia withdrew its troops. On 26 August 1848, making considerable concessions, it signed the Truce of Malmö with Denmark – much to the displeasure of the National Assembly’s Provisional Central Power in Frankfurt. The concluding negotiations in St Paul’s Church on the Truce openly showed parliament’s frustration at not having the same military force at its disposal as Prussia. On 16 September 1848, after an exceptionally controversial debate, a narrow majority of delegates voted to accept the ceasefire – the spark that fuelled the riots in Frankfurt.
