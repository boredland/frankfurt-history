# Casino (liberal centre-right)

*Roßmarkt 10*

![Photo of the casino company building on Roßmarkt](../../images/bbfc76a84646c1ecfb879d436f0ce91e_original.jpg)
*1884 — Author: Carl Friedrich Mylius | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7Z Nr. 1848-72 | Description: Photo of the casino company building on Roßmarkt*

The Casino group proved to be the most popular parliamentary faction, with around 200 members at its height. They met in the rooms of the Casino society and social club founded on the Rossmarkt in 1802. After the group was founded on 25 June 1848, these liberal centre-right parliamentarians initially met in the Großer Hirschgraben.

The Casino faction attracted numerous liberal politicians and the most prominent members of the National Assembly. These included the Prussian Major Hans von Auerswald, murdered in Frankfurt on 18 September; Krefeld banker Hermann von Beckerath; Prussian lawyer Georg Beseler, a leading member of the Constitutional Committee; Wismar historian Friedrich Christoph Dahlmann, one of the ‘Göttingen Seven’ group of professors; Prussian historian Johann Gustav Droysen; Hesse professor and historian Georg Gottfried Gervinus; lawyer and politician August Hergenhahn from the Duchy of Nassau; Prince Felix Lichnowsky, murdered in Frankfurt on 18 September, who came from a family with large estates in Silesia; Baden journalist Karl Mathy; Rhineland entrepreneur Gustav von Mevissen; Prussian historian Georg Waitz; and Carl Theodor Welckerd, a legal scholar from Hesse. The German philologist and literary scholar Jakob Grimm was given an honorary place in the group.

The faction comprised delegates from south-west Germany, the Rhineland’s middle classes and professors from northern Germany. Numerous members held key parliamentary positions, such as the Baden lawyer Alexander von Soiron, who was elected First Vice-President of the National Assembly, and part of the Constitutional Committee and the Imperial Deputation; Mannheim entrepreneur Friedrich Daniel Bassermann, who took over as Constitutional Committee chair; and Prussian judge Eduard von Simson, elected National Assembly President in December 1848. Heinrich von Gagern’s policies largely relied on the support of the Casino group even though, in his role as National Assembly President until December 1848, he could not officially join them.

The Casino members called for a constitutional monarchy, a strong executive, and a parliament limited purely to legislative issues. Although they recognised the National Assembly’s task of drafting a constitution for all the German Confederation states, they believed each state ought to have a right to decide on the particular features relevant to their own situation. While a majority of members called for the lesser German solution with a hereditary Prussian emperor, the group also included supporters of a greater German solution.

Rather than having a fixed political programme as in other parliamentary factions, the Casino group took a pragmatic approach in its policies. The statement issued in the wake of the September crisis after the Truce of Malmö also remained vague in its details. In September 1848, owing to disagreements over the power of parliament, various Casino members split off to establish the Landsberg faction with parliamentarians from the Württemberger Hof who also favoured expanding the right to vote. In December 1848, a second splinter group left to become the Pariser Hof faction since they supported ratifying the constitution in agreement with the Confederation’s individual states.

With the exponents of the greater German solution leaving the Casino group in December, it developed into a faction supporting a hereditary imperial sovereign as envisaged in Heinrich von Gagern’s draft constitution. From then on, the Casino members sought to apply their approach of political realism to establish a constitutional monarchy and severely restrict the right to vote. While the Casino faction desired agreement on the constitution with the governments of the individual states, it did not want to grant all states in the Confederation a right of veto.

On the issue of a lesser or greater German solution, which dominated the political scene in 1849, most Casino members supported von Gagern’s proposal of a Prussian hereditary emperor. Those advocating the greater German solution of including Austria and setting up a council of rulers then left and found a new home in the Mainlust group.
