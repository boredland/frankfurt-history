# Residence of Friedrich Siegmund Jucho

*Schöne Aussicht 16*

![](../../images/0ff70fa5c61b1fcb546bfb9cf0e789a2_original.jpg)
*ca. 1860 — Author: Reproaufnahme von Gottfried Vömel einer Fotografie von Carl Friedrich Mylius | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7Voe Nr. 155*

In 1848, Friedrich Siegmund Jucho, one of the leading figures in the National Assembly, lived in a house on the banks of the River Main. As a participant at the meeting of liberal and democratic leaders in Heidelberg in early March 1848, Jucho substantially contributed to preparing the preliminary parliament and inviting it to Frankfurt. During the provisional parliament, four delegates also stayed with him – Adam von Itzstein, Robert Blum from Leipzig, Wilhelm Rauschenplat from Hannover and Karl Todt from Saxony. Moreover, Jucho’s organisational talent in the background also played an important part in ensuring the smooth convention of the National Assembly.

He was the only Frankfurt citizen to be a member of the provisional parliament as well as a delegate to the National Assembly and part of the city’s constituent assembly. There, he belonged to a liberal minority, but in the National Assembly he joined kindred parliamentarians in the Westendhall faction. In 1849, Jucho decided against moving to Stuttgart with the rump parliament, which led some fellow delegates to accuse him of a dereliction of duty. As the National Assembly’s Secretary, Jucho not only became the executor of the archives, files and documents, but also secured the survival of the original document of the constitution against reactionary forces.
