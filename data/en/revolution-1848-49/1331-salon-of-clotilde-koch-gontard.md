# Salon of Clotilde Koch-Gontard

*Am Salzhaus 1*

![Photo of the Clotilde Koch-Gontard salon](../../images/86ee131a203a2b7868bf1ea8487ce2c0_original.jpg)
*ca. 1908 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 2348 | Description: Photo of the Clotilde Koch-Gontard salon*

Clotilde Koch-Gontard – wife of British consul Robert Koch – welcomed envoys and emissaries to her house in the city centre. The regular guests at her luncheons also included parliamentarians who were friends. In this way, her house on Grosser Hirschgraben became a political salon, a place where supporters of the liberal movement met and exchanged views on political issues with the host of this salon. Clotilde Koch-Gontard’s relationship to Heinrich von Gagern, President of the National Assembly, was especially frank and honest.

Since women in the 19th century were not granted equal rights under the law, the National Assembly had no women delegates. Nonetheless, women of all classes were very interested in the debates in St Paul’s Church and the 200 gallery seats reserved for them were usually overcrowded. The delegates’ wives played an important role in liaising with the constituencies, and as advisers and confidants. Moves to emancipation at this time included the Women’s College established in Hamburg in 1850 and Frankfurt’s women’s gymnastics associations founded in late 1848.
