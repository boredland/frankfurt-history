# Café Milani (Conservative)

*Roßmarkt 17*

![Lithograph depicting Café Milani on Roßmarkt](../../images/4c54e11b723d3365accddbb6e796d931_original.jpg)
*ca. 1840 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF C12058 | Description: Lithograph depicting Café Milani on Roßmarkt*

The centre-right conservative parliamentarians gathering in the Stone House from June 1848 began meeting in Café Milani on Rossmarkt in September 1848. The Café Milani coffee house, named after its Italian owner, had opened in June 1848. Six years later, it moved into new premises. The meetings in the Café Milani resembled a distinguished gentlemen’s get-together in the evening, except the participants were not allowed to smoke or drink alcohol.

The group comprised around 50 members, most from the upper middle classes, aristocratic circles, or the higher ranks of the civil service. At least half the members came from Prussia, and around one third from Austria and Bavaria. The leading figures were East Prussian judge Eduard von Flottwell, former governor of the province of Posen; Hamburg entrepreneur Ernst Merck; Prussian Catholic diplomat and politician Joseph von Radowitz; legal scholar Hermann von Rotenhan, a confidante of Bavarian King Ludwig I; lawyer Karl von Schrenck, later Minister-President of Bavaria; and Georg von Vincke, a lawyer from an old Westphalian aristocratic family with a manorial estate.

Since the Café Milani members only wanted to reform the existing German Confederation, they argued for a greater federal confederation of German states and sought to preserve monarchical rights, sovereign rulers’ armies, and the institutions particular to the individual states. They advocated strengthening the influence of the territorial states of Prussia, Bavaria, Austria and Hannover in any future nation-state. In addition, in their view, the National Assembly’s work should be limited to drafting a constitution. Parliament, they argued, merely had legislative authority over the Central Power it had created. Thus, it should not become involved in the executive affairs of the individual sovereign states, and any constitution drafted by the National Assembly would require the agreement of those states.
