# Braunfels (kleindeutsch and constitutional monarchy)

*Neue Kräme 29*

![Photo of a row of buildings including the house and restaurant "Zum Braunfels"](../../images/52a7bfa8a6123e8b477133fc36e12920_original.jpg)
*1883 — Author: Carl Friedrich Mylius | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-4054 | Description: Photo of a row of buildings including the house and restaurant "Zum Braunfels"*

In 1849, the delegates meeting at the Braunfels Hotel were committed to political aims somewhere between the Weidenbusch’s lesser German solution with a Prussian hereditary emperor and the Mainlust group’s federal greater German solution. The stately patrician Braunfels House was a stone Gothic building on Liebfrauenplatz. In its long and chequered history, it had also provided the quarters for Holy Roman emperors and was the home of the Frankfurt stock exchange until 1843. The Braunfels group were open to the lesser German solution, but sought to anchor an equal and direct right to vote in the constitution and would only grant the emperor a delaying veto.

Since Heinrich von Gagern needed the Braunfels group’s support for a majority in favour of a hereditary Prussian emperor, he came to an agreement with Heinrich Simon, the Braunfel’s leader. In return for their votes, von Gagern incorporated their demand for a democratic right to vote and a delaying veto into the draft constitution. This Simon-Gagern Pact created the necessary majority to pass the imperial constitution at the second reading from 23 to 27 March 1849. With that, the National Assembly had fulfilled its task and passed a constitution for a future German nation-state.
