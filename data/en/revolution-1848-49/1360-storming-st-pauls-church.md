# Storming St. Paul’s Church

*Paulsplatz*

![Colored lithograph: St. Paul's Church surrounded by the Austrian infantry regiment "Erzherzog Rainer" and the Prussian infantry regiment No. 38 on the morning of September 18, 1848. In the foreground, insurgents break up the pavement.](../../images/657e57f172a905a037159a97c955b875_original.jpg)

On the morning of 18 September 1848, St Paul’s Church had a martial air with soldiers surrounding the building. During the night, concerned for the safety of the parliamentarians, the city of Frankfurt and the provisional government’s ministry of the interior had already requested assistance from troops stationed in Mainz. Two battalions, one Prussian and one Austrian, were then dispatched to Frankfurt.

Around 11 o’clock, a delegation from the non-parliamentary opposition arrived at the main entrance of St Paul’s Church. They presented the Pfingstweide petition from the day before denouncing the majority of delegates as traitors. As the delegation left, the situation behind the church was coming to a head.

Up to 300 members of the gymnasts movement, craft apprentices, workers and farm hands had gathered at the delegates’ entrance on the north side of the church. Clearly primed for violence, they gave vent to their anger at the way parliament was working. When some delegates were let in, a group of resolutely determined agitators seized the chance to force their way into the building. Only thanks to the spirited intervention by parliamentary attendants and several delegates was it possible to force the agitators back out of the church and prevent the storming of the National Assembly.

When the protests then shifted to the church’s eastern entrance opposite the stock exchange, a company of the 38th Prussian infantry regiment advanced on the revolutionaries, leaving three of them with bayonet wounds. Now there was no holding the crowd. On all sides, the call went up: ‘To arms! Drive the Prussians from the city! Barricades! Barricades!’
