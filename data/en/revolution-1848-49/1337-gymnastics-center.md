# Gymnastics center

*Zeil/Friedberger Anlage*

![Steel engraving with a bird's-eye view of the gymnasium](../../images/246abaeae818a1d265579bd61acd338e_original.jpg)
*1864 — Author: Friedrich Wilhelm Delkeskamp | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S8-9 Nr. 411 | Description: Steel engraving with a bird's-eye view of the gymnasium*

The period leading up to the March revolutions is called the Vormärz – literally, the ‘pre-March period’. During those years from 1830 to 1848, gymnastic associations and choral societies were not just about fitness and music. With many political clubs and societies banned between 1815 and 1848, choral and gymnastic associations were places where political discussions were possible.

Frankfurt’s gymnastics movement goes back to 1833, when August Ravenstein started a gymnastics club in the city. Five years later, he founded the Frankfurt gymnastics institute, which also offered courses for women and girls – a pioneering move at that time. A few weeks before the start of the revolution in 1848, the gymnastics club was disbanded by the police under suspicion of planning an uprising and a coup d’état.

After the March revolutions swept Germany, many rulers gave way to demands for greater freedom. When the freedom of assembly was announced in Frankfurt, the gymnastics club was re-established and the members took part in numerous torch-lit processions, receptions and welcoming ceremonies for parliamentarians. In summer 1848, the gymnastics movement in Frankfurt and across Germany split into a non-political faction, solely interested in exercise and sport, and a political faction calling for a republic. The latter is exemplified by the Hanau gymnasts who marched to Baden in 1849, ready to join revolutionary troops to defend the constitution by force of arms.
