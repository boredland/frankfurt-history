# Württemberger Hof (centre-left)

*Fahrgasse  25/27*

![Photo of the restaurant "Württemberger Hof"](../../images/dbbd8be81ee3ef9281116ec7c9d2881b_original.jpg)
*1915 — Author: Carl Andreas Abt | License: HMF C16763 | Description: Photo of the restaurant "Württemberger Hof"*

The Württemberger Hof Hotel once stood opposite today’s Löwenbrunnen – the Lion Fountain. Centre-left parliamentarians met in the Württemberger Hof from early June 1848. Initially, the group totalled around 100 members, including Carl Mittermaier from Heidelberg, legal scholar and preliminary parliament President; the Leipzig philosopher Karl Biedermann; Munich professor Jakob Philipp Fallmerayer; Austrian lawyer Carl Giskra; Marburg constitutional law expert Sylvester Jordan; Cologne journalist Franz Raveaux; Hamburg lawyer Gabriel Riesser, later the first Jew to be named a judge in Germany; Tübingen literary scholar and philosopher Friedrich Theodor Vischer; Leipzig historian Heinrich Wuttke; and Adolf von Zerzog, a large landowner from Regensburg.

The Württemberger Hof group supported a parliamentary monarchy and popular sovereignty. They also favoured a federal state which included Austria – the greater German solution. In their view, an imperial government ought to be subordinate to parliament and the imperial constitution should not need the agreement of every individual state.

Internal disagreements led a number of delegates around Mittermaier to split from the faction and form the Augsburger Hof group. In contrast to the Württemberger Hof, the new group aspired to the ‘lesser German solution’ of a nation-state without Austria, accepted a hereditary emperor, and stood for national liberal views.

The historical inn and hotel in the Fahrgasse bore the name Württemberger Hof from 1830. Originally built in 1598, it had then been called ‘Zum Löwen’ (literally ‘to the lion’). During the programme of refurbishing the old city centre in 1937, the hotel was demolished.
