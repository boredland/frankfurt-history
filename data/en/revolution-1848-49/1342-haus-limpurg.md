# Haus Limpurg

*Römerberg 19*

![Photo of Haus Limpurg (House Limpurg) located on Römerberg](../../images/0709cb292d5a675b5d478fb86a9bafae_original.jpg)
*1864 — Author: Carl Friedrich Mylius | License: HMF C17720 | Description: Photo of Haus Limpurg (House Limpurg) located on Römerberg*

After Frankfurt’s constituent assembly was ceremonially opened in the Römer’s Imperial Hall, it moved to Limpurg House. This building on the south of the square became the main venue for its discussions. From 5 November 1848, the 120 members of the constituent assembly held 81 sessions here with the aim of reforming the municipal constitution.

The constituent assembly suggested turning Frankfurt into a democratic free state, and put forward a radical programme of reform. In particular, the idea of a directly elected seven-member governing council as the executive and a people’s council as the legislative met with little approval among the town’s citizenry and sparked considerable resistance. In summer and autumn 1849, the disaffected townspeople came together in the Patriotic Society set up in July that year. The Society had around 2,500 members and, with the Frankfurter Volksbote newspaper, also had a channel to publish its views. The constituent assembly largely ignored the desired changes voiced by the church, societies and social associations as well as a petition signed by 2,000 citizens.

Due to the lack of support in the town, the Frankfurt Senate refused to allow the citizenry to vote on the proposed constitution adopted by the assembly on 3 December 1849. Instead, on 3 January 1850, delegates gathered around the constituent assembly’s President Nikolaus Hadermann found the doors of Limpurg House locked against them and the Senate announcing a date for new assembly elections. But since no final ballot was ever held, this marked the unspectacular end of discussions over a new Frankfurt constitution in the shadow of events in St Paul’s Church.
