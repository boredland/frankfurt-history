# Paulskirche

*Paulsplatz 11*

![Color lithograph of St. Paul's Church and St. Paul's Square](../../images/2179215ab0197a673d6ad9cab48b0b8d_original.jpg)
*ca. 1850 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 322 | Description: Color lithograph of St. Paul's Church and St. Paul's Square*

The oval-shaped St Paul’s Church, consecrated in 1833, was the main political site of the revolution. From 18 May 1848 to 30 May 1849, this was where the National Assembly held its sessions. As the first freely elected parliament of all German states, including German-populated areas of Austria-Hungary, it comprised a total of 809 elected delegates. Their task was to draft a constitution for the formation of a German nation-state. In 1848, suffrage was limited to men of independent financial means, and no women were allowed to vote. Nonetheless, women did attend the public debates and were intensely involved in the political process as journalists, fighters on the barricades, or as advisers to their husbands.

On 27 March 1849, after months of discussion, the parliamentarians agreed on a constitution envisaging a German nation-state under a hereditary Prussian emperor. As a result, Austria was not to be part of a future united Germany. Yet even though a centrist majority agreed to offer Friedrich Wilhelm IV, King of Prussia, a hereditary imperial crown, he refused to become emperor – and so the desire for a German nation-state (initially) remained unfulfilled.

St Paul’s Church continued to be used for Lutheran services until 1944, when it was severely damaged in the war. After reconstruction from 1946 to 1948, St Paul’s was no longer used for divine worship. Instead, reflecting its iconic status as – in John F. Kennedy’s words – the ‘cradle of German democracy’, this site has become a museum and centre celebrating democracy in debates, awards and memorial ceremonies.
