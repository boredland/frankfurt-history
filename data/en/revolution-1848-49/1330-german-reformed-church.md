# German-Reformed Church

*Berlinerstraße 17*

![Photo of the German Reformed Church](../../images/261cd5eb4d5b96cdc61c08e38a9470a5_original.jpg)
*1905 — Author: Gottfried Vömel | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7Voe Nr. 240 | Description: Photo of the German Reformed Church*

On 20 December 1848, in the building once located in the centre of today’s Berliner Strasse, the National Assembly’s delegates adopted the ‘basic rights of the German people’ . Due to work on the heating system, the ceiling and lights in St Paul’s Church, the Assembly met in the German Reformed Church once standing here for 40 sessions from 6 November 1848 to 9 January 1849.

The catalogue of fundamental rights adopted by the National Assembly guaranteed the freedom of the individual, equality before the law and the right to private property. Such freedoms also included the freedom of opinion, the press, religious beliefs, conscience, assembly and association, as well as the inviolability of the home and person. Furthermore, these basic rights abolished the privileged status of the nobility, ensuring equality before the law, guaranteed the free practice of religion – and hence full civic rights for Jews – , created tax equality and free access to all public offices, ended feudal law and secured the freedom of profession through the right to settle anywhere within the German Reich, acquire land and exercise a trade. In addition, the constitution contained a series of provisions on institutions as well as the principle of separating the judiciary and executive, and the church and the state.

Through their votes, the delegates ratified a catalogue of fundamental rights which became valid in many German states until it was revoked in 1851. In imperial Germany, only part of these fundamental rights were applied to issues of religious and legal freedoms. The constitution of the Weimar Republic in 1919 as well as the Federal Republic’s Bonn constitution in 1949 were both substantially influenced by the pioneering fundamental rights adopted in 1848. In this sense, our modern democracy is rooted in the constitution ratified by the Frankfurt National Assembly. The building where the basic rights were adopted has vanished from today’s cityscape. The German Reformed Church was badly damaged in the war and later demolished. In the 1950s, Berliner Strasse was constructed over the site of the former church.
