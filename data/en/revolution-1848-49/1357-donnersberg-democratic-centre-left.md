# Donnersberg (democratic centre-left)

*Mainkai 37*

![Photo of the "Donnersberg" restaurant (to the right of "Zähringer Hof"](../../images/c25f25b627bbb2a36132babf4d864072_original.jpg)
*ca. 1865 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7Z Nr. 1848-79 | Description: Photo of the "Donnersberg" restaurant (to the right of "Zähringer Hof"*

On 27 May 1848, a few days after the National Assembly’s inaugural session, the Donnersberg group split from the Deutscher Hof faction. Until late September 1848, the group met in the Holländischer Hof Hotel and over the following months relocated to the Donnersberg tavern in the building of the later Zähringer Hof Hotel.

The best-known parliamentarians in the group included Baden lawyer Lorenz Brentano; the Baden priest and grammar school headmaster Carl Damm; Damian Junghanns; Gustav Rée, mayor of Offenburg; Arnold Ruge, a writer from Rügen; the Bavarian lawyer Friedrich Schüler; Wilhelm Adolph von Trützschler, a politician from Gotha; Baden lawyer Maximilian Werner; Hugo Wesendonck, an entrepreneur from Elberfeld; and the Silesian teacher and journalist Wilhelm Wolff.

As a radical left-wing faction, the Donnersberg members demanded the unlimited sovereignty of the people and a strong parliament which would also scrutinise the executive. Consequently, they rejected the proposal of a hereditary imperial sovereign. Instead, in their view, the National Assembly ought to take over governmental power and elect an executive granted the authority to conduct foreign policy. Moreover, they championed the right of self-determination of all peoples – and hence called for an end to wars conducted by any German states. There is, though, a certain ambivalence in their support of the right to self-determination, since this faction’s parliamentarians took a strongly nationalist stance arguing that Schleswig, Poznan, Bohemia, Moravia and the Italian portions of Austria should be included in a future German state.

For the Donnersberg faction, the aims of an unconditional pursuit of democracy, systematic scrutiny of the executive and granting civil rights to all classes in society were all derived from the demands formulated in the March revolutions. These radical positions worried the wealthy parliamentarians from the nobility and bourgeoisie, especially since the Donnersberg group were the only faction which did not exclude a renewed revolution or the use of violence to impose the constitution.

In November 1848, outside the parliamentary sessions, the Donnersberg faction joined the Deutscher Hof and Westendhall groups to form the Central March Association.
