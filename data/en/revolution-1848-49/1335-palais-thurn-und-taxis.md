# Palais Thurn und Taxis

*Große Eschenheimer Straße 10*

![Color lithograph of the Palais Thurn und Taxis](../../images/7ef3aeb2bc24cb8c8d85c1e2724ff279_original.jpg)
*1863 — Author: verlegt im Verlag Völcker | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S13 Nr. 269 | Description: Color lithograph of the Palais Thurn und Taxis*

Now shops and offices, this site witnessed a turning point in the political events in 1848. The Thurn and Taxis Palace was a magnificent city residence dating from 1739. Once the headquarters of the Imperial Post, between 1815 and 1866 it housed the Federal Convention of the German Confederation. In 1848, this permanent congress of envoys of all member states entrusted the National Assembly with the task of drafting a constitution, and dissolved itself until 1849.

In addition to drafting a constitution, the National Assembly’s parliamentarians also hoped to create a provisional executive to give this body governmental power. The plan was highly controversial and the subject of heated debates. The Assembly President, Heinrich von Gagern, persuaded the delegates of a ‘bold stroke’ to resolve the issue. Without the support of the governments in all individual signatory states, the National Assembly adopted the Central Power Act on 28 June 1848, appointing itself as the ‘Provisional Central Power’ and hence the new executive. The following day it then elected Archduke John of Austria as the ‘imperial regent’ (Reichsverweser), the title of the new temporary head of state.

The new government took over the Thurn and Taxis Palace from the Federal Convention as its seat, yet apart from the rooms hardly any staff were available. Lacking sources of finance, an administration and a military, the Provisional Central Power had little scope for action. Most military power still lay with the states and local rulers. On the foreign policy front, the new government also found few supporters, with many countries refusing to recognise it. To put a positive spin on its lack of real power, the National Assembly and Provisional Central Power sought to gain popular support by staging political acts with a highly symbolic value, such as the entry of the new regent Archduke John of Austria into Frankfurt on 11 July 1848.
