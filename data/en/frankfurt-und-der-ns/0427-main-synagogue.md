# Main synagogue

*Kurt-Schumacher-Straße 41*

![Photo of the main synagogue at Kurt-Schumacher-Straße 41](../../images/C35410_jpg_a1b9677abe45c79015f2709a0293a039_original.jpg)
*ca. 1865 — Author: Carl Krause | License: HMF C35410 | Description: Photo of the main synagogue at Kurt-Schumacher-Straße 41*

This was the location of the Jewish community’s Main Synagogue inaugurated in 1860. The first synagogue here dated from 1462, constructed after the Jewish ghetto (“Judengasse”) was established. When that synagogue burnt down in 1711, a new one was built on the site. It was then demolished in 1854 to create space for a prestigious new synagogue. The modern sandstone building incorporated Byzantine and Oriental elements such as onion-domed towers, as well as Gothic-style pointed arches. The interior had approximately 500 seats for men on the ground floor with a similar number of seats for women on two galleries. The furnishings included around 42 Torah scrolls, silver Torah ornaments, lamps, ritual objects, valuable Judaica and Hebraica, an organ and a marble Torah Ark. Some of these pieces were several hundred years old.
 In the night of 9-10 November, the synagogue was set on fire by SA (Storm Trooper) squads. Although the fire service was alerted, they did not put out the fire. The building burnt down, leaving just the exterior walls standing. Risking his own life, the last chief cantor and rabbinical administrator Nathan Saretzki managed to save several valuable volumes of liturgical scores. At that time, Ernstotto zu Solms-Laubach was director of the Stadtgeschichtliches Museum (as the Historical Museum was known from 1934). As soon as he heard of the fire, he brought several other people with him to carry out of the synagogue “all the Torah scrolls, materials and books within reach”. After these objects had briefly been confiscated by the Gestapo, he then incorporated them into his own museum’s collection. Later, he claimed they had been destroyed in a fire.
 In 1939, the Jewish community was forced to sell the synagogue site to the city and pay for the costs of demolishing the ruins. Shortly afterwards, bricks from the synagogue were used to construct part of the wall in the main cemetery.
 In the 1950s, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) claimed compensation of DM 3,297,120 for the destruction of the synagogue. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. The compensation authority rejected the estimate as too high. It argued that, quite independently of the pogroms in 1938, the building would have been destroyed in the bombing raids. As for the objects which “were rescued or acquired by the city in 1938”, they had already been returned and it could no longer “be established to whom they had once belonged”.
