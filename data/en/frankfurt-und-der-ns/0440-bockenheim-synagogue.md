# Bockenheim Synagogue

*Schloßstraße*

![Schloßstraße](../../images/55bfacfbd8ed5fd919360f87019dbb8c_original.jpg)
*before 1911 — Author: Hermann Bückling | License: HMF, C20438a | Description: Schloßstraße*

The Bockenheim Synagogue in Schloßstrasse 3-5 was constructed around 1873–1874 on the site of an older synagogue. For over 65 years, this was a main site of Jewish life in Bockenheim. The synagogue could seat 150 men and 110 women. In September 1938, Frankfurt’s Israelitische Gemeindeblatt, the Jewish community’s newsletter, reported the synagogue would have to be closed. Since so many community members had emigrated, it now lacked the requisite source of income. 
 
 On 10 November 1938, during the pogroms, members of the Hitler Youth and the paramilitary SA (Storm Troopers) desecrated the synagogue. They wrecked the doors, windows and the interior, and destroyed ritual objects such as the Torah scrolls, embroidered curtains and prayer books. The Gestapo took valuable items of silver and bronze. These actions were seen by a master baker’s family, and after the war they testified to the destruction and looting. In 1939, the community was forced to sell the land to the city of Frankfurt. 
 
 After the Second World War, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) submitted a claim for compensation. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. Just the price of the antiquities, furniture, ritual and cult objects destroyed or looted was put at DM 148,800. The master baker’s eyewitness account played a crucial role in the compensation process. However, in 1961 the compensation authority only agreed to pay the JRSO DM 60,000. As in other cases, it argued that the damage suffered by the building and its fittings and furnishings was largely due to the “effect of the war”. Moreover, the authority considered the JRSO’s compensation claim to be overly high, and adjusted it to a lower figure. 
 
 In 1988, a memorial plaque was set in the pavement at Schloßstrasse 3 to commemorate the Bockenheim synagogue community. The old Jewish cemetery is at what is now Sophienstrasse 70.
