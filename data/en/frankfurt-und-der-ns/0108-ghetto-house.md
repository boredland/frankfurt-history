# Ghetto House

*Rechneigrabenstraße 18-20*

![Photo of an interior space at Rechneigrabenstraße 18-20](../../images/isg_ffm_s7_331ec944dc19ad48c2ce4830b5c2cf30_original.jpg)
*ca. 1938 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-18992 | Description: Photo of an interior space at Rechneigrabenstraße 18-20*

From 1923, the twin buildings of the Hospital of the Jewish Health Insurances served as an old people’s home. After the land and property was forcibly sold to the city of Frankfurt, the buildings were used as a “ghetto house”. During the mass deportations in 1941 and 1942, the site also functioned as a collection point with Jews brought here before they were deported to concentration and death camps. Gestapo officials checked the transport lists, identification papers and luggage. A court officer was also on hand to issue official documents stripping the deportees of their citizenship, thus robbing them of their German nationality and civic rights, and leaving them stateless.
 
 Ferdinand Levi was taken from this “ghetto house” as part of the ninth mass deportation on 15 September 1942. He recalled: “We spent two days and two nights in the former Jewish home for the elderly (...) Standing on trucks or crouching on our bundles, we were transported to an open railway track near the Osthafen. Down the entire route, a crowd yelled insults at us and shouted abuse. ‘Beat them to death, why waste that expensive coal on the transport train!’ Constantly shouted out, apparently rehearsed.” Ferdinand Levi was deported that same day with 1,377 other people of Jewish origin, most of them over 65 years old. The last major deportation from Frankfurt was on 24 September 1942. The list of deportees includes the names of nurses and doctors who had, to the last minute, taken over crucial tasks. As the Rechneigrabenstraße 18-20 compensation file shows, the SS destroyed and looted the building, synagogue and library on the day of the last transport.
