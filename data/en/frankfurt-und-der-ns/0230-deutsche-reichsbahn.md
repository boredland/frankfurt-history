# Deutsche Reichsbahn

*Idsteiner Straße 18*

![Forced labor camp, Idsteiner Straße 18](../../images/9d8f28a7ad7a9e93b7c194034aae0151_original.jpg)
*ca. 1944 — Author: Jürgen Engelhardt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7B Nr. 1998-4243 | Description: Forced labor camp, Idsteiner Straße 18*

In 2025, modern housing estates were under construction on a former Reichsbahn (Reich railroad company) site in Frankfurt’s Gallus district. In the nineteenth century, this area was the cradle of industrialization in the west of the city.

In 1890, the “Reichsbahn Repair Shop Frankfurt am Main“ (RAW) was established in Idsteiner Straße 18, close to the Main Station. Between 1940 and 1944, this was also the site of the “Kantine Junge” forced labor camp—one of around 20 Reichsbahn camps. A German Labor Front (DAF) list from 1943 names one hundred “Eastern workers” living there, as well as 150 foreign workers and 49 Italians. Many of these records were lost in the bombing raids. The particularly heavy air raids on 29 December 1944 and 8 January 1945 also destroyed a memorial on the site dedicated to employees killed in the First World War. The memorial was set between the RAW’s maintenance depot and the training workshop.

This was not the first time the repair shop at Idsteiner Straße 18 had been damaged by bombs. In March 1944, when the barracks at Idsteiner Straße 1 were destroyed in a bombing raid, a camp was then set up in Flörsheim am Main for the RAW forced laborers. Marcel Capodanno, a French forced laborer, first returned to Flörsheim in 1990. He gave an account of the harsh conditions: RAW forced laborers had to leave by train for work in Frankfurt early in the morning and only returned in the late evening to Flörsheim and their substitute barracks (in the Jahnstraße gymnasium or the “Zum Mainblick” or “Zum Taunus”, formerly inns).

André Rieu and Jean-Louis Saint-Jeane also had to work from June 1943 to March 1944 in Frankfurt at the RAW and slept in the “Zum Mainblick“ camp. As part of a delegation from Pérols in southern France, they visited Flörsheim in 1997 on the fifth anniversary of the twinning of the two towns and talked of their wartime experience.

The story of what happened in Flörsheim has been extensively documented by city historian Peter Becker. In his comprehensive work, he shows “what the criminal Hitlerism between 1939 and 1945 cost in people’s lives in Flörsheim.” The forced laborers were also commemorated. One of the three hundred commemorative crosses set up in 2001 was dedicated to the little girl “Tanja Tribowa.” Frau Gröschl, an older Flörsheim resident, recognized the name at once. “I held the little one in my arms, I knew her!” she said, speaking in the local dialect. As a contemporary witness, she recalled how the camp in the Jahnstraße gymnasium was heavily guarded. Men, women and children lived there in extremely cramped conditions, with hardly any furnishings and insufficient nutrition. The children (babies and two 10- and 11-year-old boys) stayed in the camp during the day with the Eastern women workers deployed as cleaners, while the adults worked as forced laborers at the RAW in Frankfurt. After she was born on 8 December 1942 in Mainz, the little Tanja Tribowa also had to spend her short life here in the forced labor camp. Frau Gröschl brought her a cot from the attic. As the documents in the Arolsen Archives show, Tanja Tribowa died on 1 May 1944.

Frau Gröschl’s family came into contact with some Eastern workers, both women and men, and discovered they had “just been taken off the street, simply seized” in their home villages in Russia. An uncle on home leave from the eastern front took a letter from one of the Russians to his family. One camp inmate was detailed to “pick off fruit” in the Gröschl family garden and was glad “that for once he had something different to eat“ than the “rubbish stuff being cooked there.” Sometimes, they gave the children an apple, and at Christmas the family brought them secretly into their house: “We brought them over here, they were so very happy.”

In 2001, Frau Höhler, born in 1924, also gave an account of her experiences. She found out that seven or eight French forced laborers and a couple (the woman came from Alsace and spoke German) were living in the “Mainblick” camp: “Living is overstated – just a roof over their heads.” In the small room “there were only beds, no cupboards, no tables, no chairs. Their suitcases were under the bed, that was all they had as a private sphere. For me, as a young woman from a well-appointed parental home, the sight was appalling.”

Every morning Frau Höhler took the same train as the French forced laborers and the woman from Alsace: “The French workers and me, we were all working in Idsteiner Straße for the Reichsbahn at the Repair Shop for passenger and goods carriages. At that time, I was employed in the payroll office and the woman from Alsace interpreted for me.”

The French forced laborers received the same pay as German unskilled workers, but the payroll office directly deducted the money for rent and board.

Badly wounded in the First World War, Frau Höhler’s father had a heart condition, a short leg and only one arm. The French couple and a Belgian called Noel helped him with jobs in the garden. Denounced by one of their neighbors, Frau Höhler’s father was ordered to the town hall and warned by Mayor Stamm to break off all contact to the forced laborers. Regretfully, he explained to his helpers they were no longer allowed to come there, could no longer work in the garden, and no longer “eat the dumplings mother cooked“.

But Frau Höhler as well as her father still caught the same train as the forced laborers – but now they only “greeted each other by looks”.

Due to bomb damage, one day the trains left from Frankfurt-Griesheim Station and not the Main Station. As Frau Höhler explained in 2001: “As I said, Noel was always with us, as of course we worked together. Well, huge as he was, he just picked up my father, who was rather small, and put him on his back like a sack of mortar and ran off with him, so we just managed to catch the train. And with that, without knowing it, Noel saved my father’s life; since shortly afterwards, a bomb fell exactly where we’d run. My father would have been killed.” After the war, Frau Höhler continued, the family tried to find Noel, who saved her father’s life, but in vain. Nonetheless, he is commemorated in the town’s cultural memory.
