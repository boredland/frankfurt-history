# Stolen place

*Waldschmidtstraße 99*

The multi-family dwelling at Waldschmidtstrasse 99 belonged to the Felsen family until 1939. The parents Meir and Ester (née Sonnenschein) had emigrated from the Polish city of Przemyśl to Frankfurt with their four children. The family attached importance to education and cultivated their Jewish cultural heritage. Their private library contained works by Goethe, Schiller, Heine, and Gorky as well as literature by Jewish scholars. The three daughters all earned their own livelihoods. Rebeka, called Regina, worked as a milliner, her sister Pesl, called Paula, was a domestic servant, and Leontine was a seamstress. Their younger brother Chaim Josef, called Heinrich, is known to have given Hebrew lessons. On 28 October 1938, Paula and Leontine Felsen were among the 17,000 Polish Jews in the German Reich to be arrested and deported to Poland. Rebeka had a visa that enabled her to remain in Frankfurt to arrange their parents’ move and the sale of the house. However, the visa was only valid until 9 April 1939. On 31 March 1939, she applied to the foreign exchange control office for the release of the household goods. They had been stored at Neue Mainzerstrasse 76, where the siblings had lived. The office released the items and Rebeka Felsen left the country for Poland. Paula and Leontine Felsen were murdered in Poland in 1941. An entry in the Central Database of Shoah Victims’ Names indicates that Rebeka was murdered as well. Nothing is known about the fate of their brother.
