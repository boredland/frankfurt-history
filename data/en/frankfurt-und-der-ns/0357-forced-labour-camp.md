# Forced labour camp

*Froschhäuser Straße*

![Site plan of the Froschhäuser Straße forced-labor camp based on the original construction drawing from 1942.](../../images/70ad96f6dd766a906fc20c17e97b4ca4_original.jpg)
*2025 — Author: Grafikdesigner: carloscusguen.com | License: Sascha Mahl | Description: Site plan of the Froschhäuser Straße forced-labor camp based on the original construction drawing from 1942.*

This was the site of a camp operated by the Adlerwerke company for 2,000 persons. The camp was surrounded by a barbed-wire fence 2.5 metres in height. The company established it in late 1942 because it needed to exploit a large number of forced labourers to remain in operation. Even before construction of the barracks camp was finished, 800 forced labourers from Eastern Europe lived on the grounds under abominable conditions. Building was completed in March 1943. The camp consisted of 24 barracks which also housed a laundry, a sewing room, a barber shop, and a repair workshop. The forced labourers had no more than two or three square metres of living space at their disposal each. The living conditions were inhumane. To this was added the brutal treatment by the Gestapo. Ten per cent of the Adlerwerke forced labourers were arrested and registered by the Gestapo. In addition to imprisonment they faced detention in the so-called work education camp in Heddernheim or deportation to a concentration camp. 
 
 Nineteen children were born in the camp, the first in the spring of 1943. That is because, from this point in time onwards, women from Russia and Poland were no longer allowed to return home when they discovered they were pregnant. There was a simple nursing ward for the children, but the care there was abysmal. Six children are known to have died within the first six months of their lives. 
 
 On 18 March 1944 the camp was severely damaged by an Allied aerial attack. 18 forced labourers from France and Eastern Europe were badly wounded by bomb splinters; 5 of them succumbed to their injuries. During another bombing four days later, nine of the inmate barracks, the guard unit lodging, and the kitchen were destroyed by fire. The forced labourers’ living conditions worsened. They either had to sleep on the floor—some in the gutted rooms of the Adlerwerke factory—or share a bed with two other persons.
