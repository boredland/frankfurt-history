# Forced Labor Camp

*Rückertstreet 53*

![Front view of Rückertstraße 53.](../../images/db2105cf8330c9d7f849d1dbceb9d8d7_original.jpg)
*2025 — Author: Christine Hartwig-Thürmer | License: Christine Hartwig-Thürmer | Description: Front view of Rückertstraße 53.*

In connection with deportations, the Reich Association of Jews was forced to sell off numerous buildings it owned in Frankfurt’s Ostend district for use as forced labor camps: the former Klaus Synagogue, the buildings at Hermesweg 5–7 and Ostendstrasse 18, and the dormitory for Jewish female teachers and students at Rückertstrasse 53. The City of Frankfurt and the Nazi organization “German Labor Front” (German abbreviation: DAF) also sold the building’s movables, bringing in 2,631.85 reichsmarks for the municipal treasury.

Already before the sale in January 1943, the Carl Presser hauling company and later the Ludwig Ziegler & Co (pipeline construction) had an assembly camp with nine rooms set up in the building to lodge approximately 65 French civilian workers. Until March 21, 1943, the Ceka Christalle & Kriegshammer at Hanauer Landstrasse 208 belonged to the “Rückertstrasse 53 Assembly Camp Interest Group.” Then, however, it established its “own” assembly camp for “Western workers” at Allerheiligenstrasse 26.

Altogether eighteen companies and businesses participated in the camp at Rückertstrasse 53. To accommodate their forced laborers, they had to finance—per laborer—one (bunk) bed, one locker, one stool, one straw mattress and headrest set, two wool blankets, and dishes, as well as cleaning equipment such as brooms and buckets and a coal bucket.

During the day, the French laborers received meals at their respective workplaces; for breakfast and dinner they had to provide for themselves. Life in the assembly camp was highly unpopular among the French civilian workers—especially in view of the fact that these specially skilled laborers had been promised private lodgings at the time of their recruitment. In the summer of 1943, a large number of them failed to return to Frankfurt from home leave.

In October 1943, the building was so badly damaged during an air raid—all windows were broken, the power supply was cut off—that the municipal building inspectors no longer permitted its use. The forced laborers were now lodged at Allerheiligenstrasse 26 or in the warehouses of the companies they worked for. This was the case, for example, at the Ludwig Edel building decoration and sign painting company at Fichardstrasse 28–30. The Seeber & Co and Otto Glöckler companies also lodged their French forced laborers on their company premises and assigned them to carry out nighttime air raid watch.

On November 7, 1943, the DAF began the process of dissolving the assembly camp for French workers at Rückertstrasse 53. In 1943/44, the City of Frankfurt sold the property to the tax authority. Today no trace of the building’s history remains.
