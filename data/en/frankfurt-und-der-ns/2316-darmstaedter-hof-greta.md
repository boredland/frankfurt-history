# Darmstädter Hof („Greta")

*An der Walkmühle 1*

![Darmstädter Hof, side view](../../images/9cf3e28a271e462c7577150dfe22d0d2_original.jpg)
*2026 — Author: Peter Brosche | License: Privat | Description: Darmstädter Hof, side view*

Forced Labor Camp

“The outbreak of World War II meant an end to parties and celebrations for the time being.” That is the caption of two photos by Andreas Wolf. They show gay-spirited village residents in front of the “Darmstädter Hof” inn at what was formerly Borngasse 115 (now An der Walkmühle 1) in Nieder-Eschbach, a community in what was then Friedberg county. The Nazi authorities had seized the party hall of Christian Heftrich’s popular establishment in 1940. Starting in April 1943, the Vereinigte Deutsch Metallwerke (VDM) metal works company in Frankfurt used that space as a place to lodge their approximately 100 Dutch forced laborers. In those days, Friedberg served as an “overflow county” for the nearby city of Frankfurt with its large and strategically important industrial operations such as the VDM. “Surveillance day and night by a camp overseer from the copper works,” reads the report by the local police department. In 1944, the Friedberg county administrator reported to the Darmstadt Gestapo that there was a camp in Nieder-Eschbach lodging 85 forced laborers who worked for the VDM aviation works in Frankfurt’s Heddernheim district.

However, from as early as 1940 onward, Polish and Dutch forced laborers were lodged there as well. What is more, from August 1940 to the end of the war in 1945, 28 French prisoners of war from the Stalag (POW camp) IX B Bad Orb were assigned to work in Nieder-Eschbach.

Owing to the conscription of their own young men for army duty on the Eastern and Western fronts, local farmers and small-scale enterprises were in need of workers. Especially after the German invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941, the employers called on the Frankfurt employment office to supply them with man- and womanpower from the occupied territories. As early as the summer of 1942, Soviet forced laborers were assigned to various workplaces in the town. Nine women, whose number rose to 12 in 1943, worked in Jan van Velzen’s vegetable-growing operation. Eight men worked for Heinrich Schaller, 2 for Otto Reuter, and a further 6 forced laborers for individual farmers. In 1942, the tenant farmer Wilhelm Rettenmeyer at Hauptstrasse 7 reported to the mayor that he had 9 Soviet forced laborers working for him.

On June 1, 1942, the mayor received a written directive to communicate the number of “‘East’ badges” needed for the reported “workers from the Old Soviet territory.” As was the case with Polish forced laborers, Soviet forced laborers had to wear such badges “on all articles of clothing.”

Over the further course of the war, other enterprises in Nieder-Eschbach successfully sought to have foreign workers assigned to them. By April 1943, the Hermann & Emmrich clay and brick works had 21 forced laborers in their workforce whom they lodged on their company premises. “The individual community camps are located in the company’s residential building, males on the lower floor, women on the upper floor,” a local gendarme noted in his report. The company foreman took charge of the camp’s surveillance. By May 1943, the number of forced laborers working for the company had risen to 31.

Wilhelm Schwenk lodged 11 Polish forced laborers on his farm at Borngasse 1 and likewise stressed that he adhered to the Nazi directives for the treatment of foreigners: “Men and women are housed in separate camps on the premises.”

In June 1943, the mayor of Nieder-Eschbach reported to the Friedberg county administrator “that in this community 28 prisoners of war, 71 Eastern workers [men and women], 95 Dutch, and others are employed and lodged.”

By June 1944, the number had increased to 207 and thus accounted for 20% of the local population. Of these laborers, 122 were lodged individually, most of them on farms.

After the war, the UN carried out a “foreigner search operation.” In 1949, the document “F Stufe III” listed the names, of 777 adults and 5 children—199 Polish adults and 5 small Polish children, 273 Dutch, 238 Soviets, 19 Belgians, 13 stateless persons, 9 Czechs, 8 Latvians, 6 Yugoslavs, and 2 Hungarians—along with their dates and places of birth and the duration of their stay in Nieder-Eschbach.

The 40-year-old Dutchman Henrik van der May died of “pulmonary tuberculosis” in January 1943 and was buried in the Nieder-Eschbach cemetery.

In the archives of the Nieder-Eschbach historical society, there is to date no recollection of the men, women, and children who had to perform forced labor in everyday wartime life in Nieder-Eschbach. What was that life like for the forced laborers—without whom Nieder-Eschbach’s enterprises large and small would not have been able to operate—?

After the liberation, the forced laborers who had been exploited and oppressed for years gave expression to their anger toward the Germans. On April 19, 1945, shortly after the end of Nazi rule, the mayor described the situation to the occupation authorities: “For several days now, a number of Eastern workers have settled in the barracks of Nieder-Eschbach’s former flak unit. As ever more join them, the number is constantly rising. [40–50] No night passes in which they do not break into private homes and steal whatever they find in front of them.” The burglaries were committed in part with firearms, he continued, adding that the locals were afraid to go to bed and the auxiliary police powerless.

On August 1, 1972, Nieder-Eschbach was incorporated into Frankfurt as administrative district 15. We have above all Monica Kingreen to thank for knowledge of the forced labor carried out in the towns incorporated into Frankfurt after World War II.
