# KZ "Katzbach"

*Kleyerstraße 17*

![Concentration camp prisoners clearing rubble in the Gallus district in 1944; drawing by former prisoner Zygmunt Śwista.](../../images/22c92215c46169c666689365b613c435_original.jpg)

A Concentration Camp in the Middle of Town

During World War II, the Adlerwerke AG (formerly Heinrich Kleyer AG) manufactured half-track vehicles, engines, and vehicle parts for the Wehrmacht (as the German army was called at the time). To that end, the company made extensive use of foreign forced laborers, prisoners of war, and concentration camp inmates. These people were lodged in several camps in Frankfurt. On August 22, 1944, the management had a concentration camp set up in the factory building and gave it the code name “Katzbach.” The basis for this arrangement was a contract with the Central SS Economic and Administrative Office, which was also responsible for guarding and provisioning the inmates. Altogether 1,616 inmates were transferred to the Adlerwerke company concentration camp from other concentration camps. The majority of them were Polish men who had been deported to Germany during the Warsaw Uprising. They were crowded into extremely confined quarters; they suffered from hunger and disastrous hygiene conditions and were exposed to air raids without protection. Shortly before the war’s end, two Ukrainian inmates—the 19-year-old Adam Golub and the 21-year-old Georgij Lebedenko—attempted to flee. Residents informed on them, and SS men shot them on the street in broad daylight.

In the eight months of the “Katzbach” camp’s existence, 527 persons died of hunger, illness, or torture. The dead were buried at Frankfurt’s main cemetery. The Adlerwerke requested replacements, and new inmates were set to Frankfurt from the Buchenwald and Dachau concentration camps. When the Allied troops advanced on Frankfurt in March 1945, some 450 sick inmates were locked up in boxcars and, days later, transported to the Bergen-Belsen concentration camp. Only eight inmates arrived there alive. On March 24, 1945, the SS forced the remaining inmates, approximately 360 in number, to set out on foot through the Kinzigtal Valley to Hünfeld. From there they were taken to Buchenwald in boxcars. Of all the inmates who had been in the “Katzbach” concentration camp, only about ten percent survived.

After the war, the “Katzbach” concentration camp was all but forgotten. It took a research project of the Paul Hindemith School and a study carried out by Ernst Kaiser and Michael Knorn to make the dimensions of forced labor in the Adlerwerke’s “Katzbach” concentration camp known to the public. The Adlerwerke works council and other activists addressed themselves to the topic. A painstaking remembrance process got underway. In 2021, the City of Frankfurt resolved to fund the establishment of a place of commemoration and education. The Adlerwerke Historical Site opened in March 2022 is overseen by the Studienkreis Deutscher Widerstand 1933–1945.
