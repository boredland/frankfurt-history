# Synagogue on Börneplatz

*Kurt-Schumacher-Straße 10*

![Photo of the synagogue at Börneplatz](../../images/48e6e9b8e308e3e64b232579c3eda8ae_original.jpg)
*1939 — Author: Fred Kochmann | License: HMF, Ph03821b | Description: Photo of the synagogue at Börneplatz*

The synagogue on the former Jews’ Market (1886–1935 Börneplatz, 1935–1978 Dominikanerplatz, from 1978 Börneplatz again) was dedicated on 10 September 1882. It had been built at the site of the Jewish hospital at the southern end of Jews’ Lane, Frankfurt’s former Jewish ghetto. The house of worship served the conservative wing of the liberal Jewish community as a religious centre. Designed in the Neo-Renaissance style, it seated 520 men and, in the gallery, 360 women. Altogether 400 seats were added in 1901. The synagogue had in its possession 30 Torah scrolls complete with decorations, numerous religious objects, and a marble Torah shrine. In 1919, the painter Max Beckmann perpetuated the building with its red sandstone façade and prominent copper-covered cupolas in his painting The Synagogue in Frankfurt am Main, now in the Städel.
 
 On the morning of 10 November 1938, the synagogue burnt to the ground after members of the SA (Storm Troopers) and the Hitler Youth had set fire to it. The entire inventory and interior furnishings were destroyed. The Jewish community was moreover forced to pay for the demolition of the remains. The stones were used to build the wall of the main municipal cemetery. In 1939, the Jewish community had to sell the property to the city.
 
 In the 1950s, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) demanded reparations in the amount of 3,181,780 Deutschmarks for the destruction of the synagogue and the ritual objects. The JRSO had been the official fiduciary for heirless Jewish property in the American-occupied zone since 1948.
 
 In 1946, the U.S. military government had a plaque installed to commemorate the synagogue near its original site. The foundations of the synagogue and 19 houses once making up part of Jews’ Lane were unearthed during construction work in the late 1980s. Parts of the black-and-white-tiled floor and fragments of the Torah shrine were among the finds. As a consequence of protests on the part of the Jewish community and citizens of Frankfurt, the Museum Judengasse (Museum Jews’ Lane) was opened in 1992 on the ground floor of the new municipal utilities administration building. Several of the finds are on display there. Since 1996, a memorial on New Börneplatz Square has commemorated the deported Jews of Frankfurt. Metal bars on the ground retrace the ground plan of the synagogue.
