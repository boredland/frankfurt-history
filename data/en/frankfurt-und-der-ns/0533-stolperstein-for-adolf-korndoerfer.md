# Stolperstein for Adolf Korndörfer

*Beethovenstraße 32*

Adolf Korndörfer (1880–1939)

Adolf Korndörfer was born in Michelbach in 1880, the son of a pharmacist. He grew up with five siblings. After attending high school and studying theology in Marburg, he took on a position as a private tutor in an aristocratic household in Northern Germany. After returning to Marburg, he studied German and history, then entered practical training as a teacher in Frankfurt. He spent World War I at the front and came back as a lieutenant in the reserve with the Iron Cross. In Frankfurt, Korndörfer lived at Beethovenstrasse 32 in the Westend district and taught at what was then the “Liebig-Oberrealschule” in the Bockenheim district for two decades. He became a representative of the Frankfurt chapter of the Association for German Culture Abroad (German abbreviation: V.D.A.) and accompanied youth groups to annual conferences in Germany and abroad. He was known as an intellectual with great charisma. In the 1920s, he made the personal acquaintance of Max Beckmann and Joachim Ringelnatz; he was on familiar terms with many other writers and artists as well.

On May 15, 1939, Adolf Korndörfer was arrested in Frankfurt. In a café, he had met a young man who, unbeknownst to him, worked for the Gestapo as an informant. The secret agents’ job was to induce homosexual men to engage in criminal offences. Korndörfer had been known to the criminal investigation department as a homosexual since 1936 on account of consensual sexual contact to men. In the criminal file it is stated that he “had already been involved in the balls of the Human Rights Club in 1925.” The Human Rights Association had been founded in 1920 to campaign throughout the Reich for the rights of homosexuals and the abolition of Section 175 of the German Criminal Code. In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and was severely tightened in 1935.

On July 27, 1939, the wardens of the pre-trial detention prison in Frankfurt found Adolf Korndörfer hanged from the bars of his cell window.

The Stolperstein was initiated by Waltraud Beck, funded by her and Klaus Baumgarten, and laid in 2019.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
