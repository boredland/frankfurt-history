# Forced labour camp

*Kleyerstraße 45*

![Picture postcard with the Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft](../../images/4dcf5d61dc8b0a013859066c4ceedced_original.jpg)
*n.d. — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF, Sammlung Church, C80376 | Description: Picture postcard with the Allgemeine Elektricitäts-Gesellschaft*

The Adler-Werke (formerly Heinrich Kleyer) was a leading manufacturer of engines and transmissions for tanks, aircraft, and military vehicles during the war. In its factory the company made use of a large number of foreign forced labourers, prisoners of war, and concentration camp inmates for whom it operated several camps in Frankfurt. A barracks camp for French civilian workers was built on the factory grounds in Kleyerstrasse in July 1941. Soon, however, the camp was too small to meet the demand for accommodations, and in late 1942 a larger barracks camp was built in Froschhäuser Strasse in Griesheim. Until its completion, the company lodged the forced labourers in several barracks on the factory grounds at Kleyerstrasse 17, 45, and 82 as well as in Weilburger Strasse and at Krifteler Strasse 47. There were separate camps for forced labourers from Western and Eastern European countries. The Russian, Belarusian, and Ukrainians were subjected to the worst living conditions. In 1942 the Adler-Werke made use of nearly 800 forced labourers, in 1943 1,727, including 545 Russian, 491 Ukrainian, and 618 French persons. On 22 April 1944 the factory was severely damaged during an aerial attack. 
 The Wehrmacht (the German army under National Socialism) deported many forced labourers from the occupied territories in Eastern Europe. The Nazis referred to them as “Eastern workers”. When the Wehrmacht was forced to retreat from Eastern Europe, this system no longer worked and there were no longer enough forced labourers to keep production going. On 22 August 1944, the Adler-Werke therefore established a concentration camp on its factory grounds, giving it the code name “Katzbach”. Inmates from other concentration camps were brought here to perform forced labour at the Adler-Werke. Owing to the inhumane working and living conditions, one third of the 1,600 inmates died in the seven months of the camp’s existence. They were replaced on an ongoing basis by inmates from the Buchenwald, Dachau, and Neuengamme concentration camps. As the Allied troops advanced on Frankfurt in March 1945, 500 of the surviving inmates were deported to the Bergen-Belsen concentration camp. On 24 March 1945, the SS forced the last remaining inmates to set out on a “death march” to Buchenwald which many of them did not survive.
