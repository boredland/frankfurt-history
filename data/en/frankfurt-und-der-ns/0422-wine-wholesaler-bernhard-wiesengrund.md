# Wine wholesaler Bernhard Wiesengrund

*Schöne Aussicht 7*

![Bernhard Wiesengrund Wine Wholesaler, Schöne Aussicht 7](../../images/C28162_jpg_785c65f8c52678c8aaef38d2ed4e3170_original.jpg)
*1930 — Author: Leonhard Kleemann | License: HMF, C28162 | Description: Bernhard Wiesengrund Wine Wholesaler, Schöne Aussicht 7*

The wholesale wine business belonging to the Wiesengrund family was located at Schöne Aussicht 7 until 1939. The company had been founded back in 1822 and was now being operated in the third generation by Oskar Alexander Wiesengrund. It owned a wine press house in Mülheim on the Mosel as well as shares in other wine businesses. Oskar’s son was the philosopher Theodor Wiesengrund Adorno, who held a chair at the University of Frankfurt. Adorno was forced to take leave of absence immediately after the National Socialist accession to power. His teaching licence was revoked in September 1933. Soon afterwards, he emigrated to England. He lived in Oxford until 1937 and then went to the U.S. As he did not have a work permit in England, he was financially dependent on his parents, who were only able to support him, however, for a short time. On 9 November 1938, during the pogroms, the wine shop was raided, presumably by members of the SA (Storm Troopers) and the SS (Schutzstaffel: a major paramilitary organization of the Nazi party). They vandalized the rooms, wrecked the furniture, and destroyed the large stock of wine. An eyewitness reported the destruction of antique clocks and cabinets as well as two oil paintings with wine cellar motifs. Three barrels of wine each holding 5,000 litres of wine were also damaged, and 11,000 litres of wine were lost from them alone. The witness also reported that 600 bottles of wine and 300 bottles of liqueur and rum were smashed or drunk. While all this was going on, Oskar and his wife Maria Calvelli-Adorno were in their home in Oberrad, where they were attacked and arrested. After his release, Oskar Wiesengrund went about liquidating his company. He was compelled to sell off the remaining furnishings and wine below value. In early December 1938, the wine producers’ association forced the family to close down the business. Robbed of their livelihood, the couple tried to obtain a permit to leave the country. This was denied them because they had not yet paid the so-called Jewish property tax or the so-called Reich flight tax. The tax office claims amounted to over 110,000 Reichsmarks. To pay them, Oskar Wiesengrund had no choice but to pledge his securities and sell his home and business premises. In April 1939, the couple finally managed to emigrate to the USA by way of Cuba. They both died in New York, he in 1946, she in 1952. In the early 1960s, Theodor Wiesengrund Adorno successfully demanded compensation for the tremendous financial damages inflicted on his parents.
 
 There are several Stolpersteine in front of the family’s house at Seeheimer Strasse 19 in Oberrad.
