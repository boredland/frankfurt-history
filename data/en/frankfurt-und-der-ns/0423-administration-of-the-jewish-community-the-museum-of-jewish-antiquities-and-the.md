# Administration of the Jewish Community, the Museum of Jewish Antiquities and the Rothschild Museum

*Fahrgasse 146*

![Fahrgasse 146](../../images/1ad3e4b62313ee098fa086edb8747783_original.jpg)
*ca. 1900 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF, Ph00758 | Description: Fahrgasse 146*

The Museum of Jewish Antiquities was situated at this site. It was opened in 1922 in the former business premises of the Frankfurt banking house M.A. Rothschild & Söhne. The Rothschild Museum was also located here, as were the offices of the Jewish Community administration. During the November Pogroms, all three institutions were all ransacked and vandalized. Under the oversight of Dr. Ernstotto zu Solms-Laubach—the director of the City History Museum (Stadtgeschichtliches Museum), as the Historical Museum had been renamed in 1934—, museum staff took 1,000 Jewish ceremonial objects (Judaica) from the Museum of Jewish Antiquities and Frankfurt’s synagogues. In the records, this robbery is referred to as “Sicherstellung”: “securing”.
 
 The director of the Municipal Archives described his role in “securing” the Jewish community files as follows: 
 
 “On 10 November … Chief Inspector Richter rang … and informed me that, on the mayor’s instructions, I was to take all measures needed to secure the Jewish community’s archival materials. I immediately took five men and we made our way to the Fahrgasse [the site of the Museum of Jewish Antiquities], where I found total chaos: broken windows, smashed doors, slashed paintings, … heaps of files and books scattered all over the ground … Under the circumstances, the only feasible option was to secure all the written materials …. The directors of the other institutes involved (Municipal Library and City History Museum) carried out the same measures.”
 (Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Municipal Archives diary, entry of 11 October 1938)
 
 After the war, Solms-Laubach argued that this action had saved the Judaica from further damage. For about fifty years, the museum and municipal authorities maintained the legend of “rescuing” the objects. It took the changing remembrance culture around the millennium to create a new awareness of the injustice suffered by the original owners.
