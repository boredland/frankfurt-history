# Insti­tute for Social Rese­arch

*Senckenberganlage 31*

![Photo of the Institut für Sozialforschung ( Institute for Social Research)](../../images/c3053fee6451e2f38ff24ea467bd1666_original.jpg)
*ca. 1935 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-13255 | Description: Photo of the Institut für Sozialforschung ( Institute for Social Research)*

The Institute for Social Research founded in 1923 was located at Viktoria-Allee 17 (now Senckenberganlage) from 1924 onwards. It had been endowed by Felix Weil, who came from a Jewish entrepreneurs’ family. The institute was devoted to critical research on contemporary society. Max Horkheimer was the director from 1929 onwards. Erich Fromm, Friedrich Pollock, Leo Löwenthal, Theodor W. Adorno, Herbert Marcuse, and others also belonged to the institute. The used a novel approach to explain the crisis of the Weimar Republic. They considered economic processes such as rising prices and impoverishment important. They also observed that people were tending increasingly towards violence and rejecting democracy. The members of the institute looked to Sigmund Freud and Karl Marx as forerunners for their work. The Nazis accordingly referred to the institute disdainfully as “Café Marx”. The very day after Adolf Hitler was appointed Reich Chancellor, the SA (Storm Troopers) of the NSDAP (National Socialist German Workers’ Party) occupied Horkheimer and Pollock’s private home in Kronberg. Horkheimer had already taken the institute’s assets to Switzerland and moved into a hotel near Frankfurt’s main station. In March 1933, the police searched the institute and ordered its closure. The National Socialist Students’ League moved into the facilities in May 1933. On the 26th of that month, the Free State of Prussia confiscated and expropriated the building and its furnishings. Horkheimer, Adorno, and the other members of the staff—some of whom were subjected to persecution on account of their Jewish descent—were forced to flee the country. The institute was moved first to Switzerland (Geneva) and then to the U.S. (Columbia University, New York). It returned to Frankfurt in 1949 and took up quarters in a new building at Senckenberganlage 26 in 1951.
