# IG Farben Headquarter

*Norbert-Wollheim-Platz 1*

![Photo: Staatliche Bildstelle Berlin, 1937, HMF C31431](../../images/C31431_jpg_6c3cd7858d4fbb52ae73dd9370ff1257_original.jpg)
*1937 — Author: Staatliche Bildstelle Berlin | Description: Photo: Staatliche Bildstelle Berlin, 1937, HMF C31431*

The main administration building of the dyestuffs and chemical manufacturer Interessengemeinschaft Farbenindustrie AG (I.G. Farben), founded in 1925, was located at Grüneburgplatz 20 (now Norbert-Wollheim-Platz 1). The I.G. Farben-Haus building was erected between 1928 and 1931 according to plans by the architect Hans Poelzig. It had its own casino (cafeteria) building and a laboratory wing. The company was deeply embroiled in the National Socialist system. It profited from forced labour and the Nazi exploitation methods on a vast scale. It made use of more than 11,800 forced labourers in Frankfurt alone, 8 of whom were assigned to work in the administration. The building survived the Allied aerial attacks largely unharmed.

After World War II, the Allies disbanded the chemical company, which had collaborated with the Nazi regime in a wide range of different ways.
 In the post-war period, the building was used by the American military government. It was here that Dwight D. Eisenhower signed the “birth certificate” of the State of Hesse in September 1945. For several decades the building housed the Fifth Corps of the U.S. Army. The Terrace Club, a jazz venue also extremely popular with the people of Frankfurt, was located in the casino building. From 1948 onwards, the grounds around the I.G. Farben-Haus were publicly accessible and used by Frankfurters as a recreational space. That changed following two assaults by the Rote Armee Fraktion (RAF) in 1972 and 1976, in which one person was killed and several injured. Until the withdrawal of the troops in 1995, the grounds were a heavily secured restricted area. The building complex has been in the possession of the State of Hesse since 1996. Today it is used by the Goethe University. In 2008 the Norbert Wollheim Memorial was dedicated to the memory of the forced labourers exploited by I.G. Farben. Norbert Wollheim performed forced labour for the company in the Auschwitz concentration and extermination camp. In 1951 he successfully sued I.G. Farben for compensation.
 
 In the park in front of the former administrative headquarters of I.G. Farben is an exhibition of photos of young people later committed to the Buna/Monowitz concentration camp. The photos are a reminder of the lifeworlds that were destroyed. In the pavilion at the left side of the park, visitors can listen to 24 video interviews with survivors of the Buna/Monowitz camp. The square was renamed Norbert-Wollheim-Platz in 2014.
 
 In seminar room 1.812 of the casino building, which is located behind the main building, is a wall painting by the artist Georg Heck of Frankfurt (1897–1987). Heck had completed it in 1934; not long afterwards it was painted over by the Nazis, who vilified his art as “degenerate”. The painting was exposed again in 2006.
