# swing youth gathering place

*Zeil 123*

![Black-and-white drawing with ink and foil pencil of a dancing couple inside a bar](../../images/6cc229e585670999cbfe2083b0b1ee34_original.jpg)
*2022 — Author: Katharina Müller | License: VG Bild-Kunst, Bonn 2022 | Description: Black-and-white drawing with ink and foil pencil of a dancing couple inside a bar*

Café Regina was a popular concert and dancing venue that had been in existence since 1940. Young people liked to gather there. Because they were subject to extremely strict regulations, however, many of them came with their parents. We know this from the recollections of Klaus Bauernfeind, who frequented the café himself in those days. He also recalled that well-established musicians of the Nazi era such as Rosita Serrano, Teddy Stauffer, Juan Llossas, and Bernard Etté performed at Café Regina. Serrano, for example, appeared in 1939 in the radio programme Wunschkonzert für die Wehrmacht, which played the song requests of members of the German army. Bernard Etté was a hunting mate of Hermann Göring’s, and Juan Llosas, a follower of the Spanish dictator Franco, repeatedly performed for the “Strength through Joy” organization. The only one who provoked the disfavour of the Reich Chamber of Music with his jazzy swing tunes from 1939 onwards was Teddy Stauffer. In other words, most of the musicians who performed at Café Regina were sanctioned by the Nazis, as Franz Kremer of the “Harlem Club” also later recounted. This “club” was a group of young people who listened to the outlawed music known as swing. The musician Emil Mangelsdorff also went to the “Regina” when bands from abroad—for example the occupied Netherlands—had guest performances and gave jazz-like concerts for soldiers. Café Regina was forced to close in February 1943.
