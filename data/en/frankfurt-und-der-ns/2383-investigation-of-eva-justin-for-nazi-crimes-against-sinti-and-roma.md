# Investigation of Eva Justin for Nazi Crimes against Sinti and Roma

*Frankfurt District Court*

![Eva Justin in 1946. ISG FFM S7P Nr. 7326.](../../images/155514aea7ffcd2be0d81d357d1dd0b5_original.jpg)
*1946 — License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte Frankfurt | Description: Eva Justin in 1946. ISG FFM S7P Nr. 7326.*

Between 1948 and 1960, the Frankfurt district attorney’s office carried out several preliminary investigations into the persecution of Sinti and Roma by the Nazis. In 1958, it launched an investigation of Eva Justin, an employee of the “Racial Hygiene Research Department.”

In the “Racial Hygiene Research Department” (RHF), a sub-section of the Reich Board of Health founded in 1936, Eva Justin and Robert Ritter together carried out “racial-biological assessments.” These reports served as a foundation for the sterilization of Sinti and Roma, their deportation to Auschwitz, and their murder.

Despite extensive investigations, the district attorney’s office was unable to prove any malice on the part of Eva Justin. To be sure, it acknowledged that the “racial-biological” reports drawn up by Justin had served as the main basis for deportations and murders. However, it did not consider there to be adequate evidence that she had been aware of or intended the consequences of her actions. Verifiable crimes such as abetment of planned forced sterilization were considered lapsed. The investigators assumed that the young Justin had acted under the influence of her teacher Robert Ritter and had later, now a devout Catholic, changed her outlook. The investigations went largely unnoticed by the public. The proceedings against Eva Justin were discontinued on December 12, 1960.

It was not until 1963 that the broader public came to know of the “Justin case” through an article in Spiegel magazine. The article discussed Eva Justin’s call for the forced sterilization of Sinti and Roma in her dissertation, her participation in the deportations to Auschwitz on the basis of the data collected at the “Racial Hygiene Research Department,” and her post-war career in Frankfurt.

Already on the day of the article’s publication, the Social Democratic caucus in the Frankfurt city council assembly demanded that the magistrate review the accusations and question Justin’s suitability for civil service. Before the week was out, she was dismissed from her position as head of the Bornheim child counseling agency and transferred to a central counseling unit of the youth welfare office.

The judiciary also reacted. The Frankfurt district attorney’s office and the Hesse state ministry of justice took a fresh look at the accusations. However, they saw no reason to reopen the previously closed investigation proceedings.

On May 16, 1963, the Hessian broadcasting company aired a television documentary entitled “Der Fall Dr. Eva Justin” (The Case of Dr. Eva Justin) produced by Irmgard and Valentin Senger. On the day of the broadcast, she went on leave, evidently on the advice of the personnel office. She subsequently remained on leave—still receiving her full salary—as the review of the accusations continued. In the context of this investigation, several Sinti and Roma who had appeared in the television documentary were questioned.

The municipal administration came to the preliminary conclusion that Justin had not committed any criminal offense. The question as to whether her role in the Nazi system should have consequences under civil service law went unanswered. Ultimately she remained a municipal employee but was barred from carrying out any further work in child counseling.

Although the proceedings against Eva Justin were quashed—as the investigations against Robert Ritter as well as the police officers Johannes Himmelheber and Ernst Mohr had been—they differed from those investigations substantially with regard to extent and depth. Justin’s more extensive investigation can be attributed above all to the circumstance that it was carried out during the tenure of Attorney General of Hesse Fritz Bauer, himself a survivor of Nazi crimes who stood for a new phase in the legal investigation of those crimes in Hesse.

The “Justin case” can nevertheless be considered a representative example of how post-war society in the Federal Republic of Germany dealt with the Nazi past from the late 1950s onwards. To be sure, the previously widespread solidarity with former Nazi perpetrators was gradually beginning to crumble. However, many more years would pass before a more in-depth critical reappraisal of the ideological and structural causes of the crimes was undertaken.

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
