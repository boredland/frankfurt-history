# forced labour company

*Hainer Weg 37-53*

![Forced labor company, Hainer Weg 37-53](../../images/8f98adc6357dd82abc258b26c45486d5_original.jpg)
*1933 — Author: Hannah Reeck | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-29360 | Description: Forced labor company, Hainer Weg 37-53*

Alfred Feist-Belmont, the manager of the family-run Feist & Bellmont’sche sparkling wine factory, looked back on a long tradition in the business. His family had founded a wine shop in Koblenz back in 1828. Feist-Belmont was Protestant but had Jewish ancestors and under National Socialism was considered a “half-Jew”. He nevertheless managed to continue running his company for many years. In 1941 he changed its name from Sektkellerei-Feist to Feist & Belmont’sche Sektkellerei. According to the Institut für Stadtgeschichte (Institute of Municipal History) it made use of 10 female forced labourers from Ukraine. It is not clear where they had their lodgings.
 
 Alfred Feist-Belmont was arrested by the Gestapo on 18 April 1944 and subsequently held for several months. The grounds for his imprisonment were that he had “not exercised sufficient restraint as a half-Jew and had undesirable social intercourse”. On 4 January 1945 he was deported to Buchenwald, where he was registered as a political prisoner. He died on 8 March 1945. He is commemorated by a Stolperstein in the Westend district of Frankfurt.
