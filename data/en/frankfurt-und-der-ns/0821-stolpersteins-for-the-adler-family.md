# Stolpersteins for the Adler Family

*Löherstraße 21*

The Adler family

Since 2011, there have been nine Stolpersteins commemorating members of the Adler family at Löherstrasse 21 in Frankfurt’s Sachsenhausen district. The family consisted of the parents Reinhold and Margarete Adler and their children Wanda, Gisela, Heinz, Herbert “Ricky,” Rolf, Gertrud, and Ursula.

Reinhold Adler was a post office clerk. Until their internment in the camp on Dieselstrasse, the family lived here at this address in a five-room apartment with its own water connection. In the camp, the entire family was lodged in disastrous conditions in a battered old mobile home. In 1942, the Dieselstrasse internment camp was relocated to Kruppstrasse with all its remaining inmates. The living conditions there were no better. The eleven-year-old Rolf Adler died there performing forced labor.  

In 1942, the family was deported to Auschwitz-Birkenau. Of its nine members, only three survived the genocide: Wanda, Heinz, and Herbert “Ricky” Adler. After his liberation, Ricky returned to Frankfurt.

Along with the Ricky-Adler-Strasse and the Stolperstein commemorating Kunigunde Klein, the Stolpersteins for the Adler family in Frankfurt represent a step towards making Sinti and Roma of the present and past more visible in the city.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
