# Industriepark Höchst

*Brüningstraße*

Before 1945, a street called Farbenstraße crossed the I.G. Farben works site in Höchst. This was probably where the street ran. During the Second World War, I.G. Farben had seven camps set up along the street, five camps on the upper section and two on the lower section. In 1940, the first camp inmates were prisoners of war from the occupied countries of Poland and France. They were followed from 1941 by civilian workers recruited from Italy, an ally of the German Reich, as well as the occupied countries of France, Belgium, Denmark and the Netherlands. Civilian workers from western Europe were allowed to leave the camp; some lived in accommodation off the works site.

After the German Wehrmacht invaded the Soviet Union in June 1941, the company also deployed prisoners of war and civilian workers, as they were called, from countries in areas such as today’s Ukraine. The civilian workers did not come to Germany entirely of their own accord. The German occupation policy forced many labourers to work in Germany simply because, for instance, most resources, such as food, needed for the troops in military campaigns were looted from the occupied countries. This policy led to a famine in the Ukraine.

In 1943 and 1944, between 2,500 and 3,000 forced labourers a month were exploited in I.G. Farben’s works in Höchst. They did not stay there for long, since the authorities and the responsible Deutsche Arbeitsfront (German Labour Front) deployed these workers at a succession of locations. In addition, some workers did not return when granted leave, others became ill, fled or were arrested by the Gestapo. The names are known of 8,173 civilian and forced labourers at the I.G. Farben works in Höchst. Most likely, there were many more. In the last two years of the war, the workers had to be even more productive despite the steadily worsening food supply. The living conditions for eastern European forced labourers were especially bad.
