# Sichel family

*When lines are crossed!*

![Alte Gasse](../../images/291f61b0288f7a7f609eef64860034e5_original.jpg)
*2024 — Author: Chiara Mascolo, Conrad Wiedemann | Description: Alte Gasse*

Families are often considered the most important foundation in a person’s life. But what happens when this foundation is torn apart by political events, as it was during the National Socialist regime? This article sheds light on the challenges and consequences that such events bring.

Henriette Sichel, a German-Jewish woman, was born on June 3, 1860, as Henriette Neumann in Burgpreppach. She later moved with her three children to Frankfurt am Main, where her husband Sali owned several businesses, including a slaughterhouse and a cigarette company.

After the Nazis came to power in 1933, these businesses were looted and destroyed. In 1937, they were fully confiscated and closed, resulting in a trade and access ban for the family, and the Sichel business was taken and shut down. Henriette's residence was repeatedly looted and vandalized. Although her children fled abroad, she remained alone in Germany, refusing to leave due to her age. In 1941, she was deported to the Buchenwald concentration camp.

Her eldest son, Julius, was born on June 2, 1890, in Hochstadt. Julius and his wife Sidonie lived at Scheffelstrasse 7, but after their escape, the house and all their possessions were confiscated. Rental agreements or documents that could verify their time there were never found, likely due to the persecution and dispossession at the time.

The cigarette company “S. Sichel & Sons” was founded in 1880 by Sali Sichel, with all his children later working in it. After Sali's death in 1931, his sons Max and Julius, along with brother-in-law Leopold Buchheim, continued to run the company. Max, the youngest son, received power of attorney for the business in 1925, along with his brother-in-law. But when he faced arrest and deportation, he fled to Santo Domingo, leaving Julius as the sole managing director.

The Nazi takeover in 1933 brought severe restrictions, and the family was eventually forced to flee. Julius and his family were expelled from Germany. In 1937, Julius and Sidonie Sichel applied for an inheritance from Julius' deceased mother, including her apartment, jewelry, and furnishings—assets that would have been theirs under normal circumstances. But as the political situation worsened, they had to abandon their plans. Between 1937 and 1939, they fled to the Netherlands, hoping to find safety there. However, even Jewish refugees were not safe in the Netherlands. After the German occupation in 1940, the Jewish population faced increasing surveillance and threats. Many Jews were destined for deportation and were forced to flee once again. Julius and Sidonie, too, had to flee again. In 1939, they managed to escape to Australia, just before the Netherlands fell completely under German control. Julius and Sidonie Sichel emigrated to Australia, where they finally found a safe home.

Klara Sichel, born on August 5, 1892, in Hochstadt, was the only daughter of Henriette and Sali Sichel. She grew up with her Jewish family in Frankfurt. In 1922, she married the Jewish merchant Leopold Buchheim, who worked in her father’s business. Klara occasionally worked in the family business, primarily handling phone calls and office work. Her salary, along with her husband's share of the profits, was directed back into the company. Klara worked there until the business was closed in 1937. After the closure, the family had to start over in a foreign country. They emigrated with their child to Amsterdam, where Klara did not work and lived off the support of her relatives.

In 1938, the family moved on to New York, where Klara attended the George Washington School and tried to start a new life. She found work in a hairnet factory, but the persecution and life in exile weighed heavily on her. Her husband Leopold died in 1943 of a heart attack, which was attributed to the strain of persecution. After his death, Klara was left on her own. In 1958, she applied for compensation, but her request was denied because the German government did not recognize compensation for her job loss. Under the Federal Compensation Act, only people who lost their professional livelihood due to persecution and had a formal employment contract with an employer could be compensated. Klara's role in the family business was not considered regular employment, as she only occasionally helped out. Moreover, the business was small, and there was no indication that it was large enough to require a third employee. Leopold Buchheim also received no compensation, as all his files, including business tax records, were destroyed, leaving no evidence of his economic status and existence.
