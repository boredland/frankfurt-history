# Arbeitserziehungslager Heddernheim/Concentration Camp

*Oberschelder Weg 10-12*

![Aerial photograph taken by the US Air Force of the Frankfurt-Heddernheim region. The area of the former AEL Heddernheim is outlined in red for illustrative purposes.](../../images/a3312a6a34427534fa826996cbf60ae6_original.jpg)
*Author: Unbekannt | License: NARA RG 373 / F-14748 | Description: Aerial photograph taken by the US Air Force of the Frankfurt-Heddernheim region. The area of the former AEL Heddernheim is outlined in red for illustrative purposes.*

Between April 1942 and March 1945, the Frankfurt Gestapo operated the Heddernheim labor re-education camp (German abbreviation: AEL) on the grounds of an old brick factory. The conditions there resembled those of concentration camps. The Gestapo used the facility to detain forced and civilian workers they accused of inadequate work performance, lack of discipline, lack of obedience, or insubordination. The Nazis accordingly called camps of this kind “labor re-education camps.” The term concealed the fact that they actually bridged the gap between forced labor and the Nazi extermination policy. Everyday life in the camp was characterized by extreme violence and executions.

The AEL Heddernheim existed for three years. Within that period, 10,000 people were held here, the majority of them from foreign countries—above all the Soviet Union, Poland, France, and the Netherlands. They had to serve sentences of approximately six to eight weeks. Among the inmates there were, however, also German workers as well as persons persecuted for political reasons. In isolated cases, even women, Jewish men, and children from so-called mixed marriages were detained here. The inmates were put to work in armaments companies in the vicinity, for example the Vereinigte Deutsche Metallwerke (VDM). Small businesses and private households also exploited their labor—a practice from which the Gestapo made a profit.

Designed to accommodate 200 persons, the camp was constantly overcrowded. It consisted of three barracks, several sheds, a delousing room, a dog kennel, and lodgings for the guard units. The inmates had to wash out of doors. The so-called bunker, a small space built of bricks, still exists today. In it, inmates were punished and tortured. Measuring 1,250 square meters in size, the grounds were enclosed in a wooden fence and kept under surveillance from a central watchtower. The camp director was Hans Tauber, a Frankfurt Gestapo official. The Vereinigte Deutsche Metallwerke company doctor, Dr. Gerhard Pietroch, was appointed camp physician. The security personnel consisted primarily of civilians on emergency duty.

Towards the end of the war, the AEL Heddernheim also served the Frankfurt Gestapo as a “protective custody” and transit camp. Many inmates were deported to euthanasia facilities or concentration or extermination camps and murdered there. But there were also numerous deaths in AEL Heddernheim itself, brought about by the extremely heavy labor the inmates were forced to perform and their constant undernourishment. Only few inmates succeeded in escaping; anyone who tried was shot to death. The Frankfurt Gestapo repeatedly used the camp as an execution site. The inmates were hung on mobile gallows or shot. The executions were carried out by civilian camp guards. The bodies of those murdered were later buried in the Frankfurt Main Cemetery.

On March 23, 1945—six days before the liberation of Frankfurt—the AEL Heddernheim was shut down on orders from Gestapo chief Reinhard Breder. The SS forced the enfeebled inmates to make their way towards Alsfeld on foot. During the torturous hike, one Italian inmate was shot to death. After the war, the guards were never prosecuted for the abuse or the executions they had carried out. Only the camp commander, Hans Tauber, was sentenced to five-and-a-half years in prison in 1951.

Since 1987, there has been a memorial on Oberschelder Weg to commemorate the camp and its inmates’ suffering.
