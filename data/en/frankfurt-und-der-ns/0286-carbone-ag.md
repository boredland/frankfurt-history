# Carbone AG

*Talstraße 107*

![Photo of a building belonging to Deutsche Carbone AG, which operated a forced labour camp at Talstraße 107](../../images/e0b6711829b3b22f3d07d6f673ef36c1_original.jpg)
*ca. 1900 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-29233 | Description: Photo of a building belonging to Deutsche Carbone AG, which operated a forced labour camp at Talstraße 107*

During the years of the Nazi regime, Kalbach was a small village in the Hochtaunus region. Between 1939 and 1945, it had to take in numerous prisoners-of-war (POWs) and forced laborers. Kalbach’s largest company was Carbone AG at Talstraße 107. For many years, this carbon brush and battery component factory was run by its founder and director Otto Adam Neumann. Forced to leave the company in 1938, he was murdered in Auschwitz five years later.

When France was occupied by the Wehrmacht, Carbone AG’s parent company, Carbone-Lorrain in Gennevilliers, had to cooperate with the Nazi regime. From 1942, Fritz Sauckel, as General Plenipotentiary for Labor Deployment, ensured the occupied countries provided skilled laborers, applying very violent methods to force their compliance.

To fulfil its many orders for the Wehrmacht and Luftwaffe, the Carbone AG management in Kalbach demanded sixty French workers from Carbone-Lorraine. When the first of these skilled workers arrived in the village in early October 1942, they were accommodated in “Carbone Haus” at Homburger Landstraße 642. The company management wanted the French workers to be housed directly on the works site, but this first required authorization to construct more accommodation there. Initially, some of the French workers were still deployed in other armaments producers such as VDM Heddernheim or the Adlerwerke.

Carbone AG had already been provided with the barracks model IV/2 OKH, measuring 8.14 x 13.35 meters, and finally received permission to construct them in late 1942. Frankfurt architect Friedrich also supplied the building materials needed such as bricks, cement, wooden pegs, wire fence, water pipes and electric cables. A water storage pool for firefighting was also constructed. Since this was a popular place for the children in Kalbach to play, the pool was fenced in. During air raid alarms, the French workers could use the Carbone AG air raid shelters.

Despite all the company’s efforts to obtain more French workers, the number never reached the original figure requested. Carbone AG had a maximum of fifty French laborers working there simultaneously, including two French women. Other foreign workers of unknown nationalities were also deployed there.

Given the lack of accommodation, the thirty women “Eastern workers” demanded by Carbone AG as early as November 1942 never came to Kalbach. Dr. Brunner, a member of Frankfurt’s Chamber of Commerce and Industry, offered to accommodate the young women “in his communal camps“ in Riederwald and Rödelheim, an offer rejected by company director Willy Heinrich. The distance is too great, he noted, “especially as, due to their inadequate clothing, the Russian women could not be sent through the street on rainy days and in severe cold weather.”
