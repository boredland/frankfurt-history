# Gleisbautrupp 6

*Today: Frankfurt-Griesheim railway depot*

![Aerial photograph from August 15, 1944. On the left edge of the image: Old Griesheim. No. 12: Reichsautobahn (today's A5). No. 8: Railcar depot where the forced labor camp was located. From 1941 to 1945, there was a large forced labor camp of the Deutsche Reichsbahn (German Reich railroad company) on the grounds of the present-day Frankfurt-Griesheim ICE railyard. The administrative headquarters of the track-laying crew “Gleisbautrupp 6,” a Reich railroad labor detail consisting of several hundred forced laborers, was presumably also located here. The eastern section of Frankfurt’s Griesheim district borders the track apron of its main station. Since the 1920s, there had been plans to construct a large railyard here to relieve rail traffic congestion in the city. In the 1930s, diesel railcars were introduced to supplement the existing steam locomotives. To that end, the Reichsbahn built a 150-meter-long railcar depot in Griesheim between 1937 and 1939. It was in this facility, which was equipped with workshop tracks, offices, and storage spaces, that the new diesel railcars were to be maintained.

In September 1939, before the first diesel railcar even went into operation in Griesheim, the German government launched its war of aggression against Poland. In order to conserve the fuel vital for the war effort, all diesel-powered locomotives had to be taken out of service immediately, not to run again until after the war in 1945. The newly built depot in Griesheim had thus lost its intended function. The tracks and other equipment in the large hall were dismantled in 1941 and transported to Smolensk in Russia for war purposes. Now the Reichsbahn put up a barbed-wire fence around the site and built one or several wooden barracks in the huge hall to accommodate forced laborers. Publications of the present-day German railroad Deutsche Bahn state that French and Russian prisoners of war are thought to have been interned here. Surviving Reichsbahn insurance documents provide evidence that, in fact, Italian and French forced laborers were lodged in the facility from February 1941. They were soon followed by workers from several other countries, including Belgium, Poland and the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia.

Elek Nyiri, a French forced laborer born in Hungary, worked as an assistant in Gleisbautrupp 6 starting in February 1942. In 1944 he was sentenced to four months in prison for selling butter allegedly stolen from the express goods dispatch by his fellow inmates, or exchanging it for cigarettes. His court file notes that, as one of approximately 150 persons from various countries, he was housed at the Griesheim depot in a communal camp overseen by a Mr. Pfeifer.

The adults and children lodged here were put to work in express goods dispatch and many other tasks, including shunting and heating, in the goods yard and the track superstructure, in railroad maintenance, and as general assistants. In late 1943 the eastern section of the hall was hit by a bomb. However, it was the looting that took place during the final days of the war and the first days after its end that left the facility in ruins.
