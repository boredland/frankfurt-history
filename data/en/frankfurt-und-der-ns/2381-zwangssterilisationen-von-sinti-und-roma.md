# Zwangssterilisationen von Sinti und Roma

*Universitätsinstitut für Erbbiologie und Rassenhygiene*

![Outside view of the Institut für Erbbiologie und Rassenhygiene (Institute for Hereditary Biology and Racial Hygiene)](../../images/8dda0eeac18da8e04119e445464dbb69_original.jpg)
*1936 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 2002-131 | Description: Outside view of the Institut für Erbbiologie und Rassenhygiene (Institute for Hereditary Biology and Racial Hygiene)*

In Nazi Germany, the “Law for the Prevention of Offspring with Hereditary Diseases,” having already been passed by July 14, 1933, went into effect on January 1, 1934. Intended to ensure the so-called “eugenic purity” of the German people, it formed a basis for persecuting persons considered “genetically diseased” and as such allegedly “inferior” or “unfit for life in society.” It enabled the forced sterilization of members of certain groups including Jews as well as Sinti and Roma. Already in a status report of April 1934, Werner Fischer-Defoy, the head of the municipal social welfare and health offices, demanded that all “gypsies” be sent to a hereditary assessment center where the possibility of their forced sterilization would be reviewed.

One of the “counseling centers for hereditary and racial welfare” was located at the University Clinic for Genetics and Racial Hygiene, a part of the Frankfurt university hospital. The head of this counseling center, Otmar Freiherr von Verschuer, was a public health officer and in that position decisively involved in Nazi sterilization practices. Every year, his institute examined around 1,000 persons, decided on whether they were fit for marriage, drew up sterilization appraisals, and applied for forced sterilizations. As a medical diagnosis was a prerequisite for sterilization, Sinti and Roma were frequently diagnosed with ostensible “innate mental deficiency.”

Even under the Nazi regime, the forced sterilization of Sinti and Roma was thus initially illegal. That changed, however, after Heinrich Himmler’s “Auschwitz Decree” of December 1943 if not before. This “decree” not only ordered the deportation of Sinti and Roma to Auschwitz but also forced those not deported to submit a “consent to sterilization.” In cases of refusal to comply, the Reich Criminal Investigation Department decided on the sterilization order. Starting in October 1943, Ernst Mohr, a Kriminalsekretär (roughly: detective police sergeant) of Frankfurt, subpoenaed Sinti and Roma to police headquarters and was able to pressure many of them into signing a declaration of consent. This was anything but voluntary: the people were faced with the threat of committal to concentration camp if they did not comply. Forced sterilization and marriage prohibitions were intended to prevent Sinti and Roma from having children and thus ultimately render them extinct. The two measures were elements of Nazi extermination policy and thus of the genocide committed by the regime.

Between 1933 and 1945, some 400,000 persons were compulsorily sterilized in the German Reich. It is not known exactly how many of them were Sinti and Roma. However, the percentage of Sinti and Roma subjected to the procedure was significantly higher than that of other groups. For many, it was a trauma they carried with them for the rest of their lives. Many did not even know what had befallen them. As one person reported many years later: “When I came to, I was told my appendix had been removed. It was only later, when I was 18, that my family told me: You were sterilized.”

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
