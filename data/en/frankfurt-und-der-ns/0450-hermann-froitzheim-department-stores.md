# Hermann & Froitzheim department stores'

*Zeil 91-93*

![Photo of the Hermann &amp; Froitzheim department store](../../images/Ph21690_jp_1deee3dbf94e010f2a4dce178a9a31ad_original.jpg)
*before 1941 — Author: Otto Emmel | License: HMF Ph21690 | Description: Photo of the Hermann &amp; Froitzheim department store*

Hermanns & Froitzheim was a fashion and mail-order company with branches in Frankfurt and other major German cities. The mail-order business was operated from Frankfurt. In 1935, the company sent out product catalogues prominently advertising their delivery service. Neither the packages nor the delivery vans displayed the company name. This was intended as a means of allowing customers to shop “discreetly”—that is, without getting into trouble with the Nazis for buying from “Jewish businesses”—and thus of safeguarding the futures of Karl Marum and Ludwig Feitler, the Jewish owners of Hermanns & Froitzheim. Before the November Pogroms, the company branches were plastered with anti-Semitic images and slogans. By 1938 there were only 300 Jewish-operated business left in Frankfurt. As a consequence of increasing anti-Jewish propaganda and the law enacted on 1 January 1939 prohibiting Jews from operating retail shops, only four remained. All others were forced to sell or liquidate their businesses.
 Karl Marum and his wife Helene were later forced to move to Reuterweg 91. This was the location of a Jewish home for the elderly that would be converted first into a ghetto house and then into a forced labour camp. Helene Marum died there on 7 June 1942 at the age of 67. Her husband Karl was deported to Terezín on 18 August 1942. For his place in the Terezín Ghetto he was required to conclude a so-called home purchase contract. This robbed him of all his assets. Karl Marum was murdered in Terezín. His business partner Ludwig Feitner had managed to emigrate to the USA with his wife Lina and their children Hans and Marianne.
