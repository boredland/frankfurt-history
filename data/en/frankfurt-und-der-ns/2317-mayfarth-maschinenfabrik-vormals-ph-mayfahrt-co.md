# May­farth Maschi­nen­fabrik (vor­mals Ph. Mayfahrt & Co)

*Orber Street 4-6*

![Picture postcard with the Mayfarth plant in the Mainkur](../../images/baf0717772727e0b31862785bc48676b_original.jpg)
*n.d. — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF / Sammlung Church C80387 | Description: Picture postcard with the Mayfarth plant in the Mainkur*

Forced Labor Camp

Ph. Mayfarth & Co was founded at Baumweg 7 in Bornheim, back then still a suburb of Frankfurt, as a trading partnership for imported farm machinery. Just three years later, on February 1, 1875, Philipp Mayfarth left the company. The co-founder Samuel Moser now became sole owner and successfully kept it running. With his excellent sales skills, he managed to expand the distribution area as far as East Prussia and establish a branch—the company’s first—in Insterburg.

In 1881, Samuel Moser purchased a 3,000-square-meter factory compound on Hanauer Landstrasse, where the company began manufacturing its own chaff-cutting and threshing machines. The iron foundry established at Galluswarte in 1887 then enabled the it to produce cast parts for assembling farm equipment. Sales branches went into operation in Posen, Osnabrück, Berlin, Breslau, Leipzig, and Cologne, later also in Vienna. A second factory was built in Austria.

Now the Frankfurt trading company needed larger office premises, which it built for itself at Hanauer Landstrasse 8 in 1906. Samuel Moser donated the Ph. Mayfarth & Co. property on Baumweg to house a Jewish kindergarten.

In 1910, as production continued to grow, a large factory was erected on Orber Strasse 4–6 in the Mainkur industrial area near Fechenheim.

World War I and Samuel Moser’s sudden death in 1917 meant losses and setbacks for the company, which had to close its foreign branches and many of its domestic ones. Leo Moser, the founder’s son, was now in charge as sole owner and managed to keep operations going by tapping sales markets in South America.

Starting in 1933, the Nazis’ economic policies—informed, as they were, by racist ideology—led to the dispossession of Jewish company owners and the exclusion of chief executives classified as Jews, even if they belonged to Christian religious groups.

In 1936, Leo Moser was barred from entering his company. At the instigation of the creditor banks, Ph. Mayfarth & Co. was “Aryanized.”

“In 1938, the Frankfurt Maschinenbau AG [machine engineering company], formerly Pokorny & Wittekind, took possession of the Mayfarth company’s business shares and converted production to fill army orders,” Lenore Hinkel writes. Hinkel carried out research on Soviet prisoners of war who performed forced labor there and were compelled to walk four kilometers and up a steep slope to reach their sleeping quarters at Im Sperber 4 in Bergen-Enkheim.

At least 500 forced laborers from Western and Eastern European countries (among them 137 Russian women, 120 Russian prisoners of war, 54 “Easterners,” 69 Bulgarians, 25 French prisoners of war, and 37 Italians) worked at the Mayfarth machine-engineering factory. Many of them had their lodgings in barracks on the factory grounds at Orber Strasse 4. The company had another forced labor camp in the Prinz Otto Hotel at Ottostrasse 5. What is more, in 1942, the building at Wilhelmshöher Strasse 106 is thought to have lodged 45 Ukrainians.

The prisoners of war were put to work in the iron store, where they had to carry out especially grueling labor. In wintertime, they often had no protection against the cold, and during air raids slit trenches provided only inadequate shelter from life-threatening injuries. The industrial facilities in Fechenheim were often a target in the Allied air war. Between 1943 and 1945, factory and storage buildings belonging to the Mayfarth company were destroyed. Already in 1942, two lodging barracks had been gutted by fire; they were subsequently replaced by new buildings.

After evaluating sources in the Hesse main state archive, Lutz Becht commented in his overview of forced labor camps in Frankfurt:

“There are numerous documents showing that, at Mayfarth, even minor violations of the rules were punished with rigid disciplinary measures. The German workers and the responsible (German) foremen usually received mere warnings. In contrast, deductions were made from the foreigners’ already low wages for broken tools, even if the amounts involved were insignificant. In the process, no one even checked whether the tool’s destruction had been caused by the instructions for use—which were usually far too short—, by wear and tear, or by sabotage.”
