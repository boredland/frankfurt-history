# Cassella

*Hanauer Landstraße 528*

![Forced labor camp, Hanauer Landstraße 528](../../images/d520898a5c46c2c87a314c71e3330088_original.jpg)
*1931 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-29253 | Description: Forced labor camp, Hanauer Landstraße 528*

The administration building of the Leopold Cassella & Co. dye factory was located at Hanauer Landstrasse 528. It had belonged to the Interessengemeinschaft der deutschen Teerfarbenindustrie (later I.G. Farben) since 1916. Arthur von Weinberg (1860–1943) and his brother Carl (1861–1943) managed the company. In 1925, the Cassella works merged with the Interessengemeinschaft Farbenindustrie Aktiengesellschaft (literally “dye industry syndicate stock corporation”, referred to as I.G. Farben). Arthur von Weinberg was now a member of the supervisory board and the administrative board of I.G. Farben. He oversaw the areas of science, patents, and the fabrication of dyes. Carl von Weinberg was also on the supervisory board and responsible for sales as well as reparation negotiations. Both von Weinberg brothers were forced to give up their posts between 1935 and 1937. On 25 April 1938, the central committee of I.G. Farben decided to dismiss all Jewish employees.
 
 After the deaths of their non-Jewish wives in 1935 and 1937, the two brothers were subjected to ever more palpable persecution by the National Socialists. Arthur von Weinberg was arrested by the Gestapo on 2 June 1942 and deported to Terezín, where he died on 20 March 1943. Carl von Weinberg fled to Italy and died there in 1943.
 
 During the war, forced labourers were exploited in the former Cassella works. They had their lodgings at this site. After World War II, I.G. Farben was dismantled and the Cassella works operated independently again. The Höchst AG took over the company in 1995.
