# "The Rita Polish camp" A Reichsbahn camp in the Eichendorff School

*Waldschulstrasse 83*

![The Waldschule (later renamed Eichendorffschule) in its opening year, 1918](../../images/e4c878e09b26949d6d283e5f30b30911_original.jpg)
*1918 — Author: Verlag Georg Emrich, Griesheim | License: Geschichtsverein Griesheim e.V. | Description: The Waldschule (later renamed Eichendorffschule) in its opening year, 1918*

The construction of the Eichendorff School on Waldschulstrasse got underway in 1914; in 1918, after the end of World War I, the building opened as a primary school called Waldschule. Equipped with a training kitchen, sports facilities, and a light-flooded drawing and physics room, it was considered very progressive. The school received its present name, Eichendorffschule, in 1928 when Griesheim was incorporated into Frankfurt. Shortly after the beginning of World War II, in 1939 and 1940, its basement was altered to serve as a “rescue station for safety and emergency services” within the framework of air-raid protection. In the new basement “station,” the ceiling structure was reinforced, and showers and additional toilets were installed.

As the air raids on Frankfurt increased in 1943 and 1944, however, the majority of the Eichendorffschule’s pupils were sent to the rural community of Lollar for protection. Now hardly any instruction took place in the school building in Griesheim. On March 22 and 24, 1944, so-called blockbuster bombs hit the school, destroying more than half of it. In the school chronicle, the principal wrote: “The building was damaged to such an extent that it will never be serviceable for teaching purposes again.” A few months later, presumably in the fall of 1944, the “Rita Polish camp” was nevertheless set up in the ruins. It provided accommodation to 200–400 Polish forced laborers working in the Reichsbahn train repair workshop in Nied. One of the forced laborers lodged here, Tadeusz Grąbal of Warsaw, kept a diary on little pads of paper he hid in his wooden shoes. In the 1990s, he visited Frankfurt and shared his memories of his months in the Eichendorffschule as well as his detainment in the Heddernheim labor re-education camp. Thanks to Grąbal, a food card from the camp in the Eichendorffschule has come down to us.

*Author: Sascha Mahl*
