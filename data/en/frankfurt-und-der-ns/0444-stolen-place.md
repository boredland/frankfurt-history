# Stolen place

*Reichsburgstraße 2*

![<p>Bildpostkarte mit dem Gebäude Reichsburgstraße 2, HMF / Sammlung Church C80401</p>](../../images/19b935b7a6714c6347974681d1398c4f_original.jpg)
*unbekannt — Author: Postkartensammlung Church | Description: <p>Bildpostkarte mit dem Gebäude Reichsburgstraße 2, HMF / Sammlung Church C80401</p>*

This residential building belonged to Sally Fleisch and his wife Selma (née Sternfels), who lived here with their three children. It was also their place of work: Sally Fleisch was a master butcher and had taken over the shop from his father in 1926. Sales declined noticeably as a result of the boycotts of Jewish businesses from 1933 onwards. The Fleisches were compelled to close their butcher’s shop in June 1938. The couple had to pay “Jewish property tax” in the amount of 12,500 Reichsmarks (RM). All three children managed to leave the country. The 22-year-old Herta and her fiancé went to South Africa in 1937. The 19-year-old Heinrich emigrated to Northern Rhodesia by way of South Africa the following year. Walter, the youngest child, attended the Jewish vocational workshop in Fischerfeldstrasse, where adolescents were prepared for emigration. He managed to flee to England in 1939. In 1938/39, the family was forced to sell all of the real estate in their possession, including farmland in Rödelheim, Sossenheim, and Eschersheim and a stable in Wehrhofstrasse. Their house at Reichsburgstrasse 2 was purchased by the non-Jewish banker Dr. Fritz Muth (the owner of their house bank J. Muth & Co., based in Rödelheim) for 70,000 RM. In February 1939 Sally Fleisch applied to the foreign exchange control office of Frankfurt for a higher monthly allowance. At the time, the accounts of Jews were blocked by this office, which stipulated how much the account holders were permitted to withdraw every month. Those who - like Sally - needed more money to finance their family’s livelihood, had to submit an application. Sally Fleisch not only supported his son to enable him to take his training course, but also his wife’s brother, who had been wounded at war. On 27 February 1940, the foreign exchange control office ruled that Sally could have only 250 RM at his disposal per month. As this was not enough to pay their living expenses, the couple moved in with Sally Fleisch’s sister Paula Nussbaum, who lived at Kettenhofweg 94, on 18 March 1940. She also gave them securities to guarantee thier livelihood. On 20 October 1941, Sally and Selma Fleisch were deported to Lodz, where they were murdered. Their assets were confiscated the following month. The banker Dr. Fritz Muth, in whose bank the Fleisches had their account, complained. It was he who had purchased their house. Now he claimed that part of the Fleisches’ money was rightfully his, as they had sold him the house without informing him of the substantial damages to the roof. In other words, he wanted part of the money from the account to have the roof repaired.
