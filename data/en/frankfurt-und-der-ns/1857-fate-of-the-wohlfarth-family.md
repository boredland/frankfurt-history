The Wohlfarths: a family’s fate under National Socialism
The Jewish Wohlfarth family from Frankfurt suffered persecution under National Socialism. Some family members were murdered, while others fled abroad. Today, there are memorial stones that remind people of what they suffered.

Fate of the Wohlfarth family
The Wohlfarth family came from Frankfurt and consisted of parents Auguste and Seligmann and their seven children Abraham, Theodor, Julius, Hugo, Betty, Paula and Agathe. Seligmann was a businessman who ran a manufacturing company called Veit Wohlfarth. After the National Socialists seized power in 1933, the Wohlfarth family found itself suffering more and more under the new regime. By 1937, their company could no longer withstand the pressures of antisemitism and was forced to close its doors. Hugo Wohlfarth, who had been an attorney, lost his right to practise in 1933 and was forced to close his law firm. 
During the November pogroms of 1938, Julius, Theodor and Hugo Wohlfarth were arrested and taken to the Festhalle, where they were ill-treated before being taken to the Buchenwald concentration camp. Hugo was released after one month, but Theodor and Julius were murdered before the year was out. After being released from Buchenwald, Hugo fled to Palestine in 1939 with his mother Auguste, where he lived until his death in 1960. 
The family’s remaining children also fled the Nazi regime. Betty and Paula emigrated with their families to Palestine, while Agathe fled to the USA. Abraham and his family escaped first to France, and then Switzerland, before continuing on to England. In 1947, they went to the USA, where they lived in difficult conditions. The Wohlfarth family is a typical
example of how Jewish existences were destroyed in National Socialist Germany. Today, there are memorial stones in Frankfurt that remind people of what they suffered.
