# Ghetto House

*Hermesweg 5-7*

![Floor plan of the former Israeli religious school and synagogue on Hermesweg, which was used as a ghetto house and as the headquarters of the Hesse/Hesse-Nassau district office of the Reich Association of Jews in Germany](../../images/28fc45c70689e0b1180147843d68a335_original.jpg)

From 1942 to 1944, the Gestapo (Geheime Staatspolizei) used the former synagogue and religious school in Hermesweg as “community accommodation for Jews”. Such “ghetto houses” were collection and transit points where Jews were forced to live until they were deported. Frankfurt’s inner city had around 300 ghetto houses. At times, the rooms in Hermesweg had to accommodate up to 40 people. Eyewitnesses reported that, in despair at their imminent deportation, some people even jumped to their deaths from the top floor. The ghetto house fell under the remit of Administrative Inspector Ernst Holland, one of the so-called Old Fighters of the National Socialist German Workers’ Party (NSDAP) and, from 1930, a member of the SS (Schutzstaffel), renowned for its readiness to use violence. After the Nazi party’s assumption of power in 1933, Holland was given a position in the city administration. In 1940, he was appointed “Commissioner of the Gestapo’s Department of Jewish Welfare in Frankfurt ”, a position which only existed in Frankfurt. Holland’s offices were at Hermesweg 6. Ernst Holland was responsible for all Jews still living in Frankfurt and had to supervise the Jewish welfare and community facilities as well as any other real estate. Renowned for his brutal methods of interrogation, he denounced many people or handed them over to the Gestapo to speed up their deportation. A woman warden who worked for some months in Hermesweg gave an account of the interrogation of a young man: “One day a young man (of the Jewish confession) (…) was summoned to appear before Holland. (…) I sat in the room adjacent to Holland (…). Then I just heard ranting and raving, and the sound of blows. Soon afterwards, Holland called the Gestapo (…) and officials arrived to collect the young man, who was streaming with blood. (…) I also myself saw traces of blood in Holland’s room.” Holland also managed the dissolution of the assets and property of deported Jews. For this work, he deployed several of those held at Hermesweg 5-7 and also lined his own pockets. One witness at the post-war denazification tribunal recalled: “In this way, since he was indeed acting as a key figure, (....) Holland misappropriated valuables (for himself) without any legal authority.”
 In October 1943, when Holland was drafted into an SS medical unit in Stettin, the Jewish welfare organisation was disbanded and the assets of the Frankfurt community dissolved.
