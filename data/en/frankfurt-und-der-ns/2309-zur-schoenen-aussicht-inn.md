# "Zur schönen Aussicht" inn

*Im Sperber 24*

![Picture postcard with the restaurant "Zur Schönen Aussicht"](../../images/b48f075984d615d6113471b9c3f4ad44_original.jpg)
*Author: Postkartensammlung Church | License: HMF / Sammlung Church C80374 | Description: Picture postcard with the restaurant "Zur Schönen Aussicht"*

Forced Labor Camp

Not everyone has forgotten the Soviet prisoners of war who, for three years, lived locked up in the “Schöne Aussicht” inn and had to work for the Mayfarth company located four kilometers away: “Elderly residents of Bergen-Enkheim still remember the clip-clop of the wooden clogs worn by the Soviet prisoners of war who marched through the streets of Bergen-Enkheim in the early mornings on their way to Mayfarth in Fechenheim.” Lenore Hinkel researched this all-but-forgotten history and was able to interview first-hand witnesses in the community that only became a district of Frankfurt in 1977.

From March 1942 to the end of World War II, some 110 prisoners of war from the Soviet Union were lodged in the inn at Im Sperber 24 in Bergen as Kommando 703 Stalag IX B Bad Orb. “The windows were all fitted with iron bars. Our house now resembled a prison because a three-meter-high fence was installed in the garden and further reinforced with barbed wire,” the later head of the “Schöne Aussicht,” Karl Halbow (1925–2016), wrote in his autobiography.

The inn’s two large rooms were extremely crowded with four cots lined up one next to the other in each. The straw sacks and blankets were infested with vermin. There were not enough toilets or washrooms and the existing ones were inadequately equipped. The Gestapo and soldiers of the German Wehrmacht kept the prisoners closely guarded, especially on the way to and from Orber Strasse in Fechenheim, where they were forced to work for the Mayfarth company.

Philipp Mayfarth & Co had been seized from the co-founders and owners of many years: the Mosers, a Jewish family. The production of farm machines for the European market had come to a halt in 1938, and the company turned to manufacturing armaments for the German Wehrmacht.

The Soviet forced laborers had to toil six days a week in two twelve-hour shifts and received nothing to eat but thin soup with a bit of cabbage, turnips, and potatoes in it. Viktor Morosenko later recorded his horrible memories in poems: “In Germany, in the factory, labor, oh my God. Hungry and tormented we came home.”

The people of Bergen-Enkheim heard their melancholy songs. Luise Heise, a young mother at the time, recalled that she sat on a bench near the “Schöne Aussicht” with her little boy to listen to the singing. In view of the threats of punishment, the prisoners’ attempts to make contact to the people on the outside, for example by throwing home-made wooden toys and paper airplanes over the fence for the children, were in vain.

The camp was disbanded after the arrival of the U. S. Army in March 1945. The prisoners of war were taken back to the Soviet Union on transports. Only one young Ukrainian, Viktor Morosenko, stayed behind in Bergen: He was aware of Stalin’s order (no. 270 of August 16, 1941) to kill prisoners of war from Germany immediately or ban them to prison camps in Siberia as “defectors” and “traitors to the Fatherland.”

To date there is no commemorative plaque for the forced laborers in Bergen-Enkheim.
