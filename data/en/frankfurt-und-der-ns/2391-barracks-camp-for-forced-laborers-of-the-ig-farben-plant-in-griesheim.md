# Barracks camp for forced laborers of the I.G. Farben plant in Griesheim

*Stroofstraße 31*

![Construction plan for the camp for forced laborers and prisoners of war at I.G. Farben, Griesheim plant.](../../images/f110b7453ce79f51bc1d9840fd97eecf_original.jpg)
*2025 — Author: Grafikdesigner: carloscusguen.com | License: Sascha Mahl | Description: Construction plan for the camp for forced laborers and prisoners of war at I.G. Farben, Griesheim plant.*

In 1942, the I.G. Farben company’s Griesheim plant began the gradual process of setting up a large barracks camp for forced laborers behind the factory gate opposite what was then the Schwanheim Bridge. The array of wooden accommodation barracks included many of the Wehrmacht’s “horse-stable barrack” type, which was also used in many concentration camps, among them Auschwitz. From 1943 onward, there was a fence-enclosed “Easterners’ camp” in the eastern section of the camp. It was for men and women from the Soviet Union, primarily from Ukraine. In the western section was a barrack designated for French prisoners of war. The other wooden barracks lodged forced laborers from Italy, Slovakia, Belgium, France, Holland, and elsewhere. The majority of the foreign workers were men. In the so-called “Russian” group, which included many Ukrainians, there were also several young women. A number of these women got pregnant during their time in Griesheim. They were advised to undergo abortions. When a woman decided to have the child anyway, she had to place it in the care of other women in the barracks camp shortly after its birth so that she could continue fulfilling her work assignment.

A group of several interconnected buildings in the northern section of the camp housed two kitchens, cold stores, storage rooms, dining halls, and sculleries. It is highly probable that the meals for the “Eastern workers” were cooked in a different kitchen than those of the others, and that this inmate group had to eat in a separate dining hall. In keeping with Heinrich Himmler’s “Eastern worker decrees” of 1942, “Eastern workers” received fewer calories and simpler meals than other workers. What is more, whereas “Western workers” were permitted to leave the camp in their free time, “Eastern workers” usually had to remain in the enclosed “Easterners’ camp.”
