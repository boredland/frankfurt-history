# Samson-Raphael-Hirsch-Schule

*Bernhard-Grzimek-Allee 6*

![Der Neubau des Heinrich-von-Gagern-Gymnasiums, Deutsche Nationalbibliothek, Foto: Alexander Paul Englert](../../images/6854aba6c07712c6c6f274f232606fbc_original.jpg)
*1928 — Author: Alexander Paul Englert | Description: Der Neubau des Heinrich-von-Gagern-Gymnasiums, Deutsche Nationalbibliothek, Foto: Alexander Paul Englert*

From 1853, this was the location of a school run by the secessionist orthodox Israelitische Religionsgesellschaft (IGG). Comprising a middle school focusing on practical subjects and a grammar school, the institution was named after its founder and principal, Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch. He also established the neo-orthodox secessionist community as a separate entity from the main Jewish community. The school’s curriculum followed Orthodox Judaism and comprised ancient Hebrew, studying the Tanakh (the Hebrew Bible) and the Talmud. The school had nearly 400 pupils. 
 In 1933, the Nazi regime issued a law ordering the dismissal of Jewish civil servants, doctors and lawyers, and prohibiting Jews from working in these professions. As a result, the school turned to teaching more practical subjects and also offered vocational training. In addition, to prepare the pupils for possible emigration, greater weight was given to foreign languages. Since Palestine was a major emigration destination, modern Hebrew was also introduced as a subject in all classes from 1935 on. Over the following years, with so many families having to emigrate, the numbers of pupils dropped dramatically. During the 1938 November Pogroms many of the school’s teachers were arrested. In the wake of the pogroms, the Jewish elementary school also moved into the building. With the Friedberger Anlage Synagogue now in ruins, services were held in the school gymnasium. The school was forced to close in 1939 and the property and land was forcibly sold to the city. At this point, the school only had 84 pupils. Some of them were then saved on the Kindertransports abroad. In 1944, the building was severely damaged in a bombing raid. In the 1960s, the school building was demolished. 
 In that same period, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) submitted a claim for compensation for the destruction of the building during the 1938 November Pogroms. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. The compensation authority rejected the claim, arguing that the testimony from eyewitnesses was contradictory, and that “with a probability bordering on certainty (…) even without the persecution measures,” the damage “would have occurred from the effects of bombing”.
 Today, the site is home to the new building of the Heinrich von Gagern Grammar School.
