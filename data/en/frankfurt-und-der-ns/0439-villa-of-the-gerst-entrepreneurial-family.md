# Villa of the Gerst entrepreneurial family

*Niederräder Landstraße 10*

![Picture postcard with exterior view of the Jakob Sprenger School](../../images/C80410_jpg_352d9bf86f0049bbc5cd492da7368f82_original.jpg)
*1938 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF, Sammlung Church, C80410 | Description: Picture postcard with exterior view of the Jakob Sprenger School*

The residence of the businessman and “Kommerzienrat” (honorary title, literally “Councillor of Commerce”) Gustav Gerst was located at this site. He was the owner of the H&C Tietz department stores in Bamberg and Chemnitz. He had conducted his business from Frankfurt since 1919. His wife Ella came from the famous Tietz family of entrepreneurs, who ran about 100 department stores throughout Germany. Four business enterprises had their origins in this department store dynasty. During the Weimar Republic, Gustav Gerst provided generous support to the Goethe University, the Senckenberg Institut, and other institutions. In 1931 a donation from Gerst enabled the construction of the Goethe Tower, which is still one of the city’s landmarks today. After the National Socialist accession to power in 1933, the Tietz family was forced out of their business and dispossessed. The Leonhard Tietz company became the Westdeutscher Kaufhof (now Galeria Kaufhof) and the Hermann Tietz department store became Hertie. Both are still in existence today. Gustav Gerst’s department stores also suffered heavy losses on account of the boycotts of Jewish businesses starting on 1 April 1933. In August 1934 he was forced to relinquish the management of the two stores and was nevertheless held responsible for the losses. On 11 November 1938, during the November Pogroms, the Gestapo occupied his sales establishment in Chemnitz. In the early morning, around 5.30 am, four members of the SA (Storm Troopers) and the SS (Schutzstaffel: a major paramilitary organization of the Nazi party) forced their way into the villa of the Chemnitz store manager Hermann Fürstenheim and murdered him. On 30 November 1938, the Gestapo also closed the store in Bamberg. Only the employees were now permitted to purchase the merchandise. Despite the forced closure, the company was obligated to continue paying the wages. Gerst was moreover prohibited from selling his department stores to a non-Jewish businessman. On 5 December, as a result of these Nazi measures, the company filed for bankruptcy.
 
 Gustav and Ella Gerst’s private life also changed noticeably for the worse. After the City of Frankfurt annuled their hereditary building rights in November 1936, the couple were compelled to move out of their villa. They also had to sell their art collection far below value. The collection contained works by famous painters, for example Max Liebermann. In the spring of 1939, Gustav and Ella Gerst emigrated to Sweden; from there they went to New York in 1946. A short time later, Gustav Gerst demanded that his company and art collection be returned to him, but to no avail. All he received was the property in Bamberg. He died in New York in 1948.
