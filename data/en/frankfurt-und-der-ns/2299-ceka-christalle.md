# Ceka Christalle

*Allerheiligenstreet 26*

![Allerheiligenstraße: View of the street, including destroyed houses nos. 22, 24, and 26.](../../images/d6c4ffb8f9588694f5a738ebc6995d75_original.jpg)
*approx. 1994 — Author: Unbekannt | License: ISG FFM S7B Nr. 1998-2410 | Description: Allerheiligenstraße: View of the street, including destroyed houses nos. 22, 24, and 26.*

Bomb damage during World War II and car-friendly traffic routing in the context of post-war reconstruction led to substantial structural changes in the eastern section of the city center—also in the layout of Allerheiligenstrasse. As a result, there is no longer any trace of the forced laborers’ camp that once existed at Allerheiligenstrasse 26.

From 1926 to July 1942, there was a restaurant here—“Zum Grünen Wald,” run by a certain Balthasar Sauer—that served as a meeting place for trade unionists and Social Democrats. Then a camp for forced laborers was established there. The initiative for this “assembly camp for Western workers” had come from the Ceka Christalle and Kriegshammer company at Hanauer Landstrasse 208, which wanted a place to lodge its 18 forced laborers along with the foreign workers of the “AG für kleine Wohnungen” (Elbestrasse 48), the Heinrich Briel leather warehouse and army effects factory (Gutleutstrasse 75), the uniform maker Brückner & Meister (Friedensstrasse 10), and the Rudolf Brummer lighting and switching systems company (Güntherstrasse 26). Altogether 25 businesses shared in the undertaking. In August 1943, the camp had a population of 71 French, Belgian, and Dutch workers. ”Splinter group camps” of this kind were highly disliked, in part because the workplaces were often quite a distance away.

Henri Flattot, for example, had to walk to and from his workplace in the Greger & Co. metal and machine tool factory at Rhönstrasse 7. We don’t really know how the Frenchman fared. But his protest against the living and working conditions can be inferred from the charges brought against him: Starting in November 1943, the forced laborer spent eight weeks in investigative custody for “dawdling at work,” scribbling on vehicles, and “anti-Reich comments.” As the company then suffered a total loss during an air raid, it was no longer interested in Flattot’s labor.

Guard units watched over the assembly camp at Allerheiligenstrasse 26, but there were evidently ways of leaving the camp secretly. This we can assume because at some point the company overseeing the camp lost track of who was actually lodged there. On August 6, 1943, for instance, the German Labor Front Commissioner for Facility and Accommodations Procurement complained to the company: “Let me remind you of my instruction that persons may leave and return to the splinter group camps only with my permission. It is unacceptable for foreigners to leave the camp without our knowledge. I request to be informed of every departure and return.”

Just a few weeks later, on the nights of October 4 and 12, 1943, the assembly camp building was badly damaged in an air raid. Many forced laborers took advantage of the chaos and never returned to their workplaces. Others were transferred to other camps, for example the one at Reuterweg 91. From November 1943 onward, the forced laborers of various companies found a makeshift dwelling for themselves in the ruins of Allerheiligenstrasse 26 until severe bombing made the building uninhabitable in March 1944.
