# Secondary residence of G. Wild

*Lützowstraße 3*

![The building in which G. Wild's appartment was located still stands today.](../../images/5294e7aca0d7cc8a6968ee1b414cb956_original.jpg)
*2026 — Author: Jasmin Klotz | License: gemeinfrei | Description: The building in which G. Wild's appartment was located still stands today.*

During the Nazi era, G. Wild set up a makeshift home for themself in an apartment building at Lützowstrasse 3, now called Karlsruher Strasse. Their primary place of residence, however, was in Idar-Oberstein.

In 1937, the “Central Reich Office for Combating Obscene Images, Writings, and Advertisements” in Berlin called on the criminal investigation department in Idar-Oberstein to search G. Wild’s home. The search was carried out in the context of criminal proceedings against the custom tailor Hella Knabe. The police had found several letters from G. Wild among Knabe’s belongings. In these letters, Wild had ordered—among other items—gloves, green garters with bows, and black shoes with high heels. In one letter they also mentioned a four-day visit from the trans Eva Schreiber and asked about a black wig.

Hella Knabe was known for helping trans and/or gender nonconforming persons by obtaining their preferred clothing for them. She also took orders for items such as hormone creams, books, shoes, and chastity belts.

*Author: Jako Wende*

In Idar-Oberstein, the police interrogated G. Wild. Wild said they had hidden the items they had ordered at the University of Frankfurt to keep their wife from finding out about their sexual inclinations. They we r e the head of the Institute of Gemstone Research in Idar-Oberstein, an external institute of the University of Frankfurt founded in 1936.

A police officer from Idar-Oberstein accompanied Wild to Frankfurt. There Wild had to hand over the items. The items were not at the university, as Wild had initially stated, but in a sparsely furnished apartment at Lützowstrasse 3. The objects found there, which Wild had ordered from Hella Knabe, were immediately confiscated.

The criminal file on G. Wild is today in poor condition and no longer complete. It is unclear whether the criminal investigation department continued to prosecute Wild and informed their wife.

Explanatory remark: The text refers to the person in question as “G. Wild” because that is how this person signed their letters to Hella Knabe. The text uses the pronouns “they/their/them” to refer to G. Wild because it is not clear from the file what pronoun Wild preferred.
