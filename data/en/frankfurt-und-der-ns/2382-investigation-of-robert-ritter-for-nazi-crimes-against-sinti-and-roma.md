# Investigation of Robert Ritter for Nazi Crimes against Sinti and Roma

*Frankfurt District Court*

![Robert Ritter around 1947. ISG FFM S7P Nr. 11819.](../../images/be02fc1c4848604776546f2ebd66f75b_original.jpg)
*ca. 1947 — License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte Frankfurt am Main | Description: Robert Ritter around 1947. ISG FFM S7P Nr. 11819.*

Between 1948 and 1960, the Frankfurt district attorney’s office investigated the persecution of Sinti and Roma by the Nazis. The first proceedings were directed against Dr. Robert Ritter, the onetime head of the “Racial Hygiene Research Department.” This “research department” had been set up in 1936 by the Reich Board of Health, and the work it carried out served as a pseudo-scientific basis for the forced sterilization and murder of Sinti and Roma.

It was Vinzenz Rose, a surviving Sinto, who set the criminal prosecution proceedings in motion when he hired a private detective to track Ritter down in 1947. In 1948, the “State Commissariat for Politically, Racially, and Religiously Persecuted Persons” finally initiated the official investigation proceedings by filing a criminal complaint. Statements by Sinti survivors, including the brothers Oskar and Vinzenz Rose, formed the basis. The Roses were among the few who found the courage and strength to take legal action against the perpetrators so soon after the war’s end. They would later become key figures in the early Sinti and Roma civil rights movement.

Robert Ritter was accused of using coercive measures within the framework of his “racial-biological” examinations, committing physical abuse, and—with his expert assessments—contributing to the forced sterilization of Sinti and Roma and their deportation to concentration camps by the thousands. As early as 1947, he was nevertheless once again in the employ of the Frankfurt health office as a municipal physician. Despite the ongoing investigations, the city opposed his suspension and treated the matter confidentially.

In the course of the investigations, the district attorney’s office interrogated 62 witnesses, many of them Sinti and Roma. Their credibility, however, was systematically questioned, while Ritter’s own statements went largely unchecked. Ritter submitted Nazi assessments he had drawn up himself as supposed evidence to discredit the survivors’ statements. The district attorney’s office questioned the witnesses’ credibility due to their own involvement in the case—a clear testimony to the continuation of anti-gypsy prejudice in the post-war judiciary.

For example, the investigators believed Ritter’s claim that he had known nothing about either the deportation of Sinti and Roma to the Auschwitz concentration and extermination camp or the fatal consequences of that deportation. Ritter had headed not only the “Racial Hygiene Research Department” but also, from 1942 onwards, the “Criminal Biology Institute” within the Reich Criminal Investigation Department. It can therefore be assumed that both Ritter and his assistant Eva Justin were informed about the mass deportations as early as 1943 and participated in their preparation. The district attorney’s office nevertheless closed the proceedings on August 28, 1950 for “lack of evidence.”

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
