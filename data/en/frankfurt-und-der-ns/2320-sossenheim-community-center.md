# Sossenheim Community Center

*Siegener Street 22*

![Community Center Sossenheim](../../images/8a99714faf4d6b9c852c33811391845f_original.jpg)
*Author: Bernhard Ott | License: ISG FFM  Best. S7A Nr. 1998-25583 | Description: Community Center Sossenheim*

Forced Labor Camp

The Volkshaus (community center) Sossenheim at Siegener Strasse 22 had been built in 1924, complete with a gym and a kindergarten, as a meeting place for workers’ associations. However, the National Socialists expropriated the building and set up a camp for foreign workers there. Approximately half of the 322 forced laborers in Sossenheim were made to live in this building or in the barracks built on its grounds.

For fear that prisoners of war might engage in espionage, sabotage, or propaganda, the Nazi administration had the sixty French POWs from the Stalag IX B Wegscheide camp as well as the labor detachments 393 and 906 guarded day and night by armed Wehrmacht soldiers.

All forced laborers were subject to severe penal measures. In addition, the building contractor Jakob Noll worked with the company in charge of the Siegener Strasse 22 “community camp” to erect concrete solitary confinement cells.

People from 15 nations were crowded into the Volkshaus Sossenheim. In 1943, German Labor Front (DAF) lists counted 42 Russians, 3 Ukrainians, 14 Lithuanians, 9 Poles, 1 citizen of the Protectorate, 62 French, 1 Walloon, 4 Flemings, 3 Dutch, and 29 Italians.

The camp inmates were put to work in various plants in the vicinity, including the Noll construction company on Silostrasse, the model maker Knebel (Kurmainzer Strasse 81), the Rowley & Kieser shoe machine factory and the Günther & Kleinmond tool factory (both in Rödelheim), the Breuerwerke engineering works (Kurmainzer Strasse 81), and the Farbwerke Höchst chemical company. After what were in some cases lengthy treks to and from their workplaces, insufficient nourishment, and ten- to twelve-hour work shifts—day or night—they returned to the camp to face further humiliation and harassment. Head of the camp Dietrich Krebs took deliberate measures to exacerbate the already lacking hygiene conditions: he had a pit latrine dug right outside the windows of the “Frenchmen’s barracks.” The stench was unbearable, especially at the height of summer.

The inmates were denied any means of washing, bathing, or cooking. Juliane Kinkel, a staunch Catholic and a French teacher in Sossenheim who volunteered with the German Labor Front as an interpreter and social welfare worker, reported: “If people washed their clothes in the air-raid protection buckets, he threatened to report them.”

In 1946, several of the French forced laborers pressed charges against Dietrich Krebs. They accused him of stealing food rations, refusing sick people food and care, and denying injured people medical attention. He reportedly also threatened with committal to a labor re-education (AEL) or concentration camp. The accusers claimed that Krebs bore the responsibility for the death of two people as well as for permanent damage to the health of the survivors. The responsible Sossenheim physician, Dr. Ludwig Roos, also contributed by declaring sick forced laborers “fit for work.”

Operations manager Noll, camp head Krebs, and Dr. Roos were friends and stood by one another when it came to investigations. Juliane Kinkel once spoke out in favor of a Frenchman who was reported for sabotage simply because a drill bit had broken while he was working with it. This time, the local Nazi party group leader took her side.

The Sossenheim local history society reported: “Camp head Krebs’s food theft went to the point where he was arrested by the Gestapo, but he was released again after just a few days in pre-trial detention. He did, however, at least lose his post as head of the camp.”

Heinz Hupfer, who devoted himself extensively to researching local history, regrets that none of the persons involved received due punishment after the liberation from Nazi rule. Only the Sossenheim SA leader Karl Lorenz Knebel was sentenced to a fine of 500 Mark—while at the same time being classified as a mere “follower.” Knebel was the father-in-law of Dieter Krebs, the head of the Volkshaus Sossenheim forced labor camp.
