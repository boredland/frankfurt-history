# "Carbone House"

*Homburger Landstraße 642*

![Renovation plan for the Carbone House in Frankfurt am Main – Bonames, Homburger Landstraße 642, dated May 17, 1942.](../../images/74823c3e29f41bf242522ff8b2accbbb_original.jpg)
*n.d. — Author: Unbekannt | License: Fritz Koch | Description: Renovation plan for the Carbone House in Frankfurt am Main – Bonames, Homburger Landstraße 642, dated May 17, 1942.*

Even as early as 1939, Polish prisoners-of-war (POWs) were billeted in the little village of Kalbach in the Hochtaunuskreis district. In 1940, they were joined by French POWs.

In January 1941, Carbone AG, a carbon brush and battery components factory, had 260 employees, making it the largest employer in Kalbach. With many workers called up to fight in the war, there was a shortage of labor for the numerous contracts for the Wehrmacht and the Luftwaffe. In early October 1942, the company management contacted Fritz Sauckel, General Plenipotentiary for Labor Deployment, with a request for 60 skilled workers to be provided by Le Carbone-Lorrain, the French parent company. The first thirty skilled workers arrived in Frankfurt from France towards the end of October 1942.

Since accommodation was needed to house the next group of workers, Carbone AG paid approx. 63,000 RM for a former farm at Homburger Landstraße 642 in Bonames, then the northernmost district of Frankfurt. The company commissioned Frankfurt architect Friedrich Krämer to convert the farmhouse. At the cost of 9,000 RM, dormitories were constructed for women on the ground floor and men on the floor above. An apartment was also created for an administrator. Washrooms and toilets were installed in auxiliary buildings across the yard. In winter 1942–1943, “Carbone-Haus,” as it was called until its sale in 1958, was ready to accommodate 39 French male workers and one French woman worker.

Some of this group of workers recruited for Germany had volunteered. Aside from personal belongings, each worker brought with them a blanket and bed sheet. The Carbone AG had to register each of them individually at the Bonames police station; for the allocation of food, they had to be registered with the Eckenheim Food Supply Department. The French workers were each given an Arbeitsbuch (work book) and had to join the German Labor Front (DAF).

Despite the Carbone management’s intense efforts to have all the 60 skilled workers they needed accommodated centrally on their factory site, permission to construct barracks at Talstraße 107 was only granted in March 1943.
