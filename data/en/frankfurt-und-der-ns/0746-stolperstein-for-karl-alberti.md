# Stolperstein for Karl Alberti

*Kelsterbacher Straße 73*

Karl Alberti (1905–1941)

Karl Alberti was born in Frankfurt, the fifth of his parents’ children. His father was a shoemaker. The family lived at Kelsterbacher Strasse 73 in the Niederrad district of Frankfurt. Alberti attended regular public school until he was 14, then the Wiesenhütten special-education school for one year. He worked as an unskilled houseboy in—among other places—the Kaiserkeller in Frankfurt’s station district. After his parents’ death—his mother died in 1936—he eked out a living with cleaning and sewing jobs.

In 1937 he was, by his own account, sterilized without having been told the reason. Under the Nazi regime, procedures of this kind were often carried out by force under the so-called “hereditary health laws.”

In 1939, Karl Alberti was arrested for “fornication with men” under Section 175 of the German Criminal Code. In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and was severely tightened in 1935. Alberti had been informed on—that is, incriminated by other detainees—in the framework of a major police operation against homosexual men. He was accused of having had sexual contact to adult men.

The public prosecutor’s office appointed Prof. Dr. Rolf Hey of Frankfurt University’s Forensics Institute to draw up an expert opinion. As in many other legal proceedings against homosexual men, Hey pronounced Alberti “torpid imbecile.” “Imbecility” was a dismissive term used in those days for mental disability. “Torpid” means “sluggish” or “slow.” Hey also drew on so-called “genetic assessments” of Alberti’s siblings. These assessments were a pseudo-scientific procedure for examining families for alleged “hereditary diseases.”

On July 12, 1939, at the request of the public prosecutor's office and the court-appointed counsel for the defense, the Frankfurt district court decided to have Karl Alberti forcibly committed to a sanatorium. On November 15, 1939, he was taken from Frankfurt’s Preungesheim prison to the Eichberg regional sanatorium in Rheingau. From there he was transferred to the Hadamar regional sanatorium near Limburg on January 22, 1941.

Alberti was killed immediately upon arrival at Hadamar. He was 35 years old. On his death certificate, the authorities falsely stated that he had died of “pneumonia” and “circulatory insufficiency.”

The Stolperstein was initiated by Martin Dill and Heidi Stögbauer of the Frankfurt Stolperstein Initiative, funded by the Coordination Office for LGBTIQ Issues of the Department of Multicultural Affairs of the City of Frankfurt am Main, and laid in 2019.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
