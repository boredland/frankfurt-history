# Stolen place

*Zeppelinallee 58*

The banker Ernst Wertheimber had his villa built for himself, his wife Anna Maria Franziska (née de Bary), and their three children. The estate in Bockenheim covered an are of 13,197 square metres. One of the daughters, Anna Maria Wertheimber-de-Bary, worked as a bookkeeper in her father's bank, which was based at Taunusanlage 15. She later worked as a librarian in the Rothschild Library and as an antiquarian bookseller in the Walter Schatzki bookshop, an establishment well-known among — and highly appreciated by — booklovers. She shared her passion for art and literature with her husband Count Leo Lanckoroński, who was the president of the local and regional court of Frankfurt am Main. He was dismissed from his post in 1937 because his wife was a "half-Jew" as defined by the National Socialist race laws. 


Ernst Wertheimber's bank accounts were blocked on 20 July 1938. On 27 September of that year he was forced to sell his villa to the City of Frankfurt, which intended to use it as a theatre academy. The purchasing price was 225,000 Reichsmark (RM). Wertheimber was not able to make use of the money as he pleased, however. He and his wife now lived at Siesmayerstrasse 7. Their son Eduard emigrated to New York in 1938. Ernst Wertheimber fell seriously ill and was in the hospital for several months. In the spring of 1940, the foreign exchange control office ruled that he could have only 500 RM a month at his disposal. He moreover had to pay the "Jewish property tax" and the "Reich flight tax", together amounting to 211,000 RM. Ernst Wertheimber died on 11 September 1940. Because his wife was his sole heir and not a Jew, the security order was revoked.


The Kunsthaus Heinrich Hahn auctioned off Ernst Wertheimber's collection of Japanese art, which comprised colour woodblock prints and paintings as well as numerous netsukes (small carved figures) and lacquer boxes.
