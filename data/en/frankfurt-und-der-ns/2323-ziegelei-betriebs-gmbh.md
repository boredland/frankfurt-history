# Ziegelei-Betriebs-GmbH

*Westerbachstreet 300*

![Stumbling block at Westerbachstraße 300.](../../images/178861a3bc24e37093d88e18deeb847c_original.jpg)

Forced Labor Company

A brickworks once occupied a site between Westerwaldstraße, Westerbachstraße 272-303 and Siegener Straße. Initially known as the Hagelauer Brickworks, the business later became the Ziegelei-Betriebs-GmbH. In the Nazi years, forced laborers from occupied territories abroad were also deployed here, accommodated in barracks on the premises. To begin with, company director Eduard Rübsaamen had a group of Jewish forced laborers working under the foreman Dölle. Eighteen of them are known by name, one from Offenbach and seventeen from Frankfurt. Only two of these forced laborers survived the Holocaust.

In 1942, Eduard Rübsaamen negotiated with the SS over the deployment of Soviet forced laborers. One of his papers has survived listing 27 names of Russians and Ukrainians forced to perform hard labor and risk their lives in the brickworks. In 2024, to commemorate their suffering in Sossenheim, a “Stolperschwelle” threshold memorial was laid at Westerbachstraße 300. 

Forced labor did not spare children either. The forced laborers living in the barracks on the works site also included a 15-year-old girl as well as three 15-year-old boys and one boy just 14.

Nikolai Ekschijan, one of the forced laborers, was accused of leaving his workplace without permission. Eduard Rübsaamen and Dölle had him arrested by the Gestapo and taken to the Heddernheim labor re-education camp (AEL), where conditions were like a concentration camp.

After the war, no attempt was ever made to critically review and come to terms with the history of forced labor in Sossenheim. As far as is known, the companies have not offered compensation. Until 1948, Eduard Rübsaamen remained in office as the brickworks company director. As local historian Heinz Hupfer has discovered, it is striking how the city archive (today: Institut für Stadtgeschichte) is missing precisely the address and household books of streets where those affected lived.
