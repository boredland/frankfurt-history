# Rödelheim Synagogue

*Inselgäßchen gegenüber 8*

![Picture postcard with Niddapartie and synagogue in Rödelheim](../../images/C80402_jpg_42d755b38db22ab2ab852a16d9ee7bef_original.jpg)
*1927 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF, Sammlung Church, C80402 | Description: Picture postcard with Niddapartie and synagogue in Rödelheim*

In 1838, to replace the old synagogue from 1730, a new synagogue was inaugurated at Inselgasse 9 (formerly Judengasse 9; now Inselgässchen). This event was also followed in places outside Rödelheim. The inauguration ceremony’s opening speech was given by Offenbach’s liberal Rabbi Dr. Salomon Formstecher, an influential religious philosopher in Reform Judaism. The new synagogue, equipped with valuable ritual objects, could seat around 90 men and 48 women. The light prayer room with its high ceiling had exquisite curtains, an old Torah cover, old silver items, and a richly decorated Torah Shield and crowns from the early baroque. The synagogue building also held a library, a religious school and a women’s mikveh (ritual bath). In 1932 to 1933, the school was attended by 16 children. Before the synagogue was destroyed in November 1938, the local pastor had visited it and reported seeing an elaborate Torah Ark to hold the Torah scrolls. He also stated that the synagogue was set on fire on 9 November 1938 “by people with their faces covered” and burnt to the ground. In 1939, the community was forced to sell the plot to two non-Jews.
 
 After the war, on the basis of the pastor’s eyewitness account, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) claimed compensation amounting to DM 241,900 for the destroyed synagogue building, the interior and furnishings, as well as the ritual and cult objects. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. In 1961, the compensation authority only agreed to pay a sum of DM 104,720. It claimed the damage to the building and the interior could also have been caused during the war, and reduced the compensation figure accordingly. 
 
 A memorial set up in Brentano Park in 1979 recalls the destruction of the synagogue and the deportation of residents from Rödelheim. Rödelheim’s old Jewish cemetery can be found in Zentmarkweg, while the new one is located on Westerbachstrasse within the municipal cemetery.
