# Stolen Place

*Finkenhofstraße 29*

![Finkenhofstraße 29](../../images/5c16d826bd096d986ffeec6b90072b5b_original.jpg)
*ca. 1900 — Author: Albert Klöckner | License: ISG | Description: Finkenhofstraße 29*

The apartment building at Finkenhofstraße 29 originally belonged to the Jewish widower Markus Siegel from Mannheim. The property covered an area of 734 square meters. The four-storey building contained four 6-room and two 2-room apartments. The large apartments were connected to a newly installed central heating system. The annual rental income amounted to 6,540 Reichsmark. The property was encumbered with a security mortgage from the Mannheim tax office in the amount of 24,000 Reichsmark to secure any Reich Flight Tax that might be due for the Reich. Due to the persecution, they had to sell their house to a non-Jewish café owner from Kaiserslautern and his wife for 43,000 Reichsmark under a contract dated July 12, 1939. The officially tax-assessed value was 36,500 Reichsmark and the transport route 46,400 Reichsmark. The contract costs, including land transfer tax, were borne by the buyers. In February 1940, the contract partners asked for quick approval, as Markus Siegel had a visa to emigrate to the USA and could only do so after the purchase contract had been completed. The building authorities finally approved the contract on June 29, 1940. The Nazi authorities did not set a compensatory levy to the German Reich, but reserved the right to do so in the event of rent increases.
