# Burning of books

*Römerberg*

![Painting depicting the book burning on the Römerberg, oil on canvas](../../images/B1614_jpg_c2c481455aee1ae5e47def229dacefa3_original.jpg)
*1933 — Author: Heinz Woelcke | License: HMF B1614 | Description: Painting depicting the book burning on the Römerberg, oil on canvas*

On the Römerberg, a bronze commemorative plaque between the Fountain of Justice and the Old St. Nicholas Church marks the site where the Nazis staged a book burning on the square on 10 May 1933. Here, Nazi university students burned books by 131 authors including Bertha von Suttner, Thomas and Heinrich Mann, Hilde Marx , Erich Kästner, Erich Maria Remarque and Anna Seghers. Organised by the German Student Union, which was dominated by the National Socialist German Students’ League, a total of 22 book burnings took place at university cities across the German Reich. 
 On the evening of 10 May, students, faculty staff and SA and SS members set off from the university for the Römerberg accompanied by an SS marching band. The books were piled up on an ox cart borrowed from a farmer. When they arrived at the Römerberg, the books were thrown onto a bonfire built there by the Nazi students. Protestant university pastor Otto Fricke and Georg Wilhelm Müller, SS Group Leader at the university, gave speeches praising the book burning as an avowal of loyalty to the new state and the “German nation” (Volk).

Resistance activist Lore Wolf describes the book burning as follows:

„On May 10th 1933, I was on Römerberg. Pyres were burning. SA-men brought books of our best writers on dung carts and threw them into the flames while cheering and shouting foul-mouthed insults. My heart was pounding in my throat. I could not restrain myself any longer and cried out: ‘They are burning the German spirit!’

The people around me moved away from me, no-one said anything, icy silence. Maybe they had not come to this place voluntarily.“

 The book burning, which was publicly announced, caused an outcry abroad with hundreds of thousands demonstrating in New York on 10 May. The Dutch Hilversum radio network broadcast excerpts from the books banned by the Nazis, while Paris and Prague also saw initiatives against the book burnings.
