# St. Lawrence Parish

*Kalbach Main Street 7*

![Laurentiuskirchgarten with chapel, Catholic Church of St. Laurentius, which has belonged to the “Parish of Catherine of Siena” since 2016.](../../images/d588850cffb78d6c0b1f59c44a98e3ed_original.jpg)
*approx. 1980 — Author: Adam Ruppel | License: ISG FFM S14Ru Nr. 1660 | Description: Laurentiuskirchgarten with chapel, Catholic Church of St. Laurentius, which has belonged to the “Parish of Catherine of Siena” since 2016.*

They were all welcome in the St. Lawrence rectory at Hauptstraße 7 – the forty-five Polish prisoners-of-war (POWs) from Stalag IX B Bad Orb (Wegscheide) sent to this little village in November 1939, as well as around thirty Polish women and men taken to Germany as forced laborers. Father Josef Pipberger greeted them on the street and kept the rectory and Catholic Church open for all the faithful. For Sunday Mass, a Polish teacher helped to translate the sermon. Polish hymns were sung, and Communion was taken together with the Catholic congregation in Kalbach.

But the Gestapo soon interrogated and arrested this courageous priest. After the war, Father Pipberger gave an account of his experience in a persecution report dated 29 August 1945. With the tightened measures in the decree in 1941 signed by Gauleiter (regional leader) Jakob Sprenger, forced laborers could only receive pastoral care or attend divine service outside a church. This led Father Pipberger to invite the Polish workers into the rectory’s parish hall once a month for Mass.

From 1940, a number of Polish POWs were deployed as civilian workers in agriculture, some even as late as June 1945 – one month after the war ended.

The files in Frankfurt’s Institut für Stadtgeschichte (Institute for the History of Frankfurt) also mention twenty-one French prisoners-of-war from detachment 481-1 and 481-2 in the POW camp Stalag IX B Bad Orb. Although they remained in Kalbach from June 1940 to March 1945, their accommodation there could not be identified with absolute certainty.

In addition, in the village of Kalbach, at that time part of Hochtaunus district, around forty forced laborers from Belgium, Holland, Italy, Russia, Ukraine, Yugoslavia, and Switzerland, as well as stateless persons, were working for local farmers. They were deployed to, among others, G. Stumm and Ph.Westerfled, or on farms, or in the Taunus restaurant run by Karl Westerfeld.

For its many armament orders, Carbone AG, a carbon brush and battery component factory, needed foreign skilled workers. In 1942, it set up a forced labor camp on a derelict farm at Homburger Landstraße 642. It was not until March 1943 that Carbone AG could build accommodation barracks supplied by the Army High Command on their factory premises at Talstraße 107.
