# Stolperstein for Valentin Born

*Dominikanergasse 7*

![Stolperstein for Valentin Born at Dominikanergasse 7](../../images/ede662a3667b88978085dd8ffb7e492e_original.jpg)
*unknown — Author: Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt am Main | Description: Stolperstein for Valentin Born at Dominikanergasse 7*

Valentin Born (1891–1945)

Valentin Born was born in Partenstein near Lohr in Lower Franconia in 1891. He earned his living as a factory worker; his last job was at the I. G. Farben-Werke in the Hoechst district of Frankfurt. His last address was Klostergasse 11. The house he lived in was destroyed during the war. The address of the building now at that site is Dominikanergasse 7.

Over the years, Born had several minor previous convictions for “begging” and “vagrancy” (that is, roaming with no permanent place of residence or work). In 1942, he was arrested for allegedly making sexual advances on a soldier. On May 5, 1942, the Friedberg district court sentenced him to an extremely harsh prison term of one year and six months for “fornication with men under Section 175.” In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and was severely tightened in 1935. The court justified the severity of Born’s sentence by stating that the man in question was a soldier “who may soon be in active duty again.”

Born was first committed to the Bad Nauheim municipal jail, then transferred to the prison in Diez an der Lahn. His sentence would normally have ended on September 17, 1943. In keeping with a directive issued by the Chief Office of National Security in 1940, however, homosexuals were not released after serving their prison sentences but placed in so-called “preventive custody.” That meant imprisonment for an indefinite period without new sentencing.

Valentin Born was handed over to the Frankfurt criminal investigation department even before serving his sentence in full. On September 8, 1943, he was committed to the Preungesheim prison in Frankfurt. On November 10 of the same year, he was taken to the Natzweiler-Struthof concentration camp in Alsace. There he was given the inmate number 5901 and categorized as “asocial”—a blanket term used by the Nazis to refer to people they dismissed as “outsiders to the community.”

Later he was transferred to the Flossenbürg concentration camp in Upper Palatinate, where he was registered as inmate number 5195. Valentin Born died there on March 5, 1945, just a few weeks before the camp’s clearance and liberation. He was 53 years old.

The Stolperstein was initiated by Martin Dill and Heidi Stögbauer of the Frankfurt Stolperstein Initiative, funded by Stefan Diefenbach, and laid in 2019.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
