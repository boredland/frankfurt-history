# Stolperstein for Jakob Kahn

Jakob Kahn (1882–1940)

 Jakob Kahn was born in Witzenhausen und attended the Jewish school there. In 1897, he moved to Frankfurt, where he completed a commercial apprenticeship. He worked for various companies as an accountant and from 1914 to 1920 for the municipal tax authority. He was single, Jewish, and had no children.

Following a nervous breakdown, he was ill and unable to work. From August 1921 to March 1922, he underwent treatment in a sanatorium in Köppern. After leaving there, he received a small disability pension and worked various temporary jobs. Until 1937 he lived at Hegelstrasse 20 as a subtenant.

On April 10, 1937, Kahn was arrested in Frankfurt on suspicion of “fornication with men.” On July 22, 1937, he was sentenced to two years in prison, partially because of an earlier conviction. He was classified as “incorrigible.” Professor Dr. Rolf Hey certified that he was “homosexual without any actual predisposition.”

Jakob Kahn served his sentence in the Preungesheim prison in Frankfurt. His petition for clemency was rejected. He registered Allerheiligenstrasse 26b as his new address. He had meanwhile been compelled to take on “Israel” as his middle name. On June 23, 1939, on the basis of various denunciations by other prisoners, he was once again sentenced to prison—now eight months—for offenses under Section 175. He served his sentence, first in the Frankfurt-Preungesheim prison and then in the prison in Diez an der Lahn. After completing his sentence on January 1, 1940, he was not released but turned over to the Frankfurt police.

In early 1940, probably on February 8, he was committed to the Sachsenhausen concentration camp, where he was classified as a Section 175 inmate and a Jew. According to the SS, he died of a swollen leg and circulatory insufficiency.

The Stolperstein was initiated by Martin Dill and Heidi Stögbauer of the Frankfurt am Main Stolperstein Initiative, funded by Gregor Schorberger, and laid in 2019.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
