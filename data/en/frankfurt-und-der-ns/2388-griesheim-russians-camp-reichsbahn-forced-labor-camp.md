# “Griesheim Russians’ Camp” Reichsbahn Forced Labor Camp

*Alt Griesheim 72*

![The forced-labor camp was located near the weir.](../../images/b7f88776d8abbdaebe9c32541ae8374f_original.jpg)
*ca. 1930 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, S7A Nr. 1998-21650 | Description: The forced-labor camp was located near the weir.*

The Reichsbahn (Reich railroad company) camp at Alt Griesheim 72, also known as the “Lager Staustufe” (“Dam Camp”), was unique in Griesheim. Whereas the district’s other camps were located more on its periphery, this one was in a restaurant on the main street of Griesheim—that is, in the middle of a residential neighborhood. A group of Polish forced laborers were presumably the first to be lodged in the camp when it went into operation. By June of 1941, if not before, French forced laborers followed, in September 1941 Belgians. In October 1942, the German Labor Front (DAF) reported that the camp lodged 49 Italians, 6 Walloons, and 22 Frenchmen (altogether 77 men). Over the course of the year 1942, one or several barracks were added for the accommodation of Russians and Ukrainians, now also including women. From that time onward, the camp was commonly known as the “Griesheim Russians’ camp.” In April 1943, the German Labor Front counted the camp population at altogether 334 persons from six different countries. There were numerous Poles and “Eastern workers” from the Soviet Union among them, people who, under the “Polish” and “Eastern worker decrees,” were obliged to wear the Polish and Eastern worker badges in plain sight on their clothing when they left the camp.

The entire camp population worked for the Reichsbahn. Their workplaces were track-laying crew 6 and the repair workshops in Nied and Gallus. A long-established Griesheim family reported that the camp was fenced in and the camp residents were so hungry that they begged for food through the fence. This memory offers evidence that the camp at Alt Griesheim 72 adhered to the regulations stipulated by the “Eastern worker decrees”: enclosure in a fence and the camp residents’ deliberate undernourishment—treatment in keeping with the Nazis’ racist worldview.

Eugeniusz Bulanowski of Warsaw, Poland was one of the camp’s first residents. On January 7, 1943, after two and a half years performing labor duty in the Reich repair workshop in Nied, the Frankfurt Gestapo committed him to the Buchenwald concentration camp. He was accused of falsely claiming to be too sick to work. Classified as “work-shy Reich” (ASR) and assigned inmate number 3154, he had to remain in the concentration camp until the war’s end.

There were also children among the camp’s residents—a circumstance of which little Wasili Nagaitschuk’s death certificate, for example, provides evidence. Wasili was born in Kiev (now Ukraine) on October 12, 1941 and died in Griesheim on February 3, 1944. August Scheurer, the Reichsbahn camp commander, submitted the death certificate to the Frankfurt registry office, citing “measles, pneumonia” as the cause of death.
