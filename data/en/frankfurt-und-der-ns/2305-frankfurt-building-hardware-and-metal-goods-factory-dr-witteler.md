# Frankfurt Building Hardware and Metal Goods Factory Dr. Witteler

*Reuterweg 91*

![Apartment building at Reuterweg 91 / Irrigation plan, site plan, floor plan, sectional vie, Nov. 11th, 1942.](../../images/49942cee1205fec78c6dbc00dc2bd5f5_original.jpg)
*n.d. — Author: Unbekannt | License: ISG FFM, Best. A.63.03 Nr. 18484 | Description: Apartment building at Reuterweg 91 / Irrigation plan, site plan, floor plan, sectional vie, Nov. 11th, 1942.*

Forced Labor Camp

Until 18 August 1942, Charlotte Kahn, Siegfried Fay, Helene Grünebaum and 150 other old people from Frankfurt and its environs lived crammed into Reuterweg 91. Ernst Holland, the Gestapo’s Commissioner for Jewish Public Welfare, had turned the apartment building into a “Judenhaus-like old people’s home.” From here and other “Judenhäusern” (“Jew houses“) in Frankfurt, 1,022 people, mostly over 70 years old, were deported to the Theresienstadt ghetto.

The city of Frankfurt paid L. Satori from Niederlahnstein a monthly rent of 1,000 RM – from fall 1943 1,200 RM – for the apartment house with four floors and seven rooms on each floor. The plan was to use the building as an assembly camp for “Western workers.” With Frankfurt companies complaining about a lack of labor for their armament orders and the problem of accommodation, the Dr. Witteler Frankfurt Construction Fittings and Metal Goods Factory made the 24 living areas ready for 175 Dutch and French civilian workers from 46 companies. Each company had to pay 200 RM as a deposit and provide their workers with blankets, straw mattresses, (cooking) utensils, crockery, etc.

In November 1942, an architect surveying the house noted soaked, decaying ceilings and floors and inadequate and faulty fixtures and fittings. In January 1943, Hans Müller, the German Labor Front (DAF) Commissioner for Facility and Accommodations Procurement, inspected the building and complained about the inmates peeling potatoes and preparing food for themselves on the beds in the dormitories. Moreover, he noted, Ukrainian woman cleaners were at work while the men were sleeping after their night shifts. The threat of taking this option of forced labor accommodation away from companies immediately led to setting up a system of stewards, a ban on women cleaners in the house, and a rule that only tea and coffee could be prepared there. There is, however, no record of whether the structural damage was repaired.

In October 1943, after the wartime destruction of forced labor camps – including  Allerheiligenstraße 26 – foreign forced laborers were housed in Reuterweg 91. Companies paid 0.50 RM as an overnight fee per person as well as 1 RM billeting per capita and day.

In May 1943, neighbors complained about noise, stench and vermin – a sign of the inadequate facilities and provisioning of the forced laborers. Nonetheless, the assembly camp for “Western workers” continued to be used until the end of the war. After a general inspection of the forced labor camps in May 1945, Frankfurt’s Police President reported on Reuterweg 91: “Apartment house with four floors, six rooms on each floor, 150 beds. Now cleared. Must be cleaned and de-loused.”
