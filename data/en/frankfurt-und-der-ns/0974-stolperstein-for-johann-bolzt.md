# Stolperstein for Johann Bolzt

*Savigny Straße 55*

Johann Bolzt (1893-1942)

Johann Josef Bolzt was born an illegitimate child in Frankfurt on September 19, 1893. His mother had five more children with her second husband. Bolzt learned the profession of greengrocer from his stepfather. He was drafted to the military during World War I and served as a private. In the summer of 1915, he was severely injured. His right arm had to be amputated, and he now had mobility problems. After the war, he worked for various companies, including VDO Tachometer, as a concierge. By 1940, Bolzt had four convictions for homosexual relationships under Section 175/175a of the German Criminal Code. In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and was severely tightened in 1935.
 On May 26, 1940, Bolzt was arrested at the Nidda outdoor swimming pool in the Nied district of Frankfurt. He had tried to make contact with and touch a 17-year-old youth. The boy reported him to the police. The same day, Bolzt was sentenced to one year in a “Zuchthaus”—a particularly harsh form of imprisonment—for “attempted seduction of a man under 21 years of age.” On August 22, 1940, he was committed to the Ludwigsburg “Zuchthaus.” During the trial, Prof. Dr. Rolf Hey of the Frankfurt Forensics Institute furnished an expert assessment. He described Bolzt as “feeble, passive, disoriented, psychopathic, but sane.” Assessments of this kind were drawn up about many persecuted homosexuals. On May 26, 1941, after serving his sentence, Johann Bolzt was handed over to the Frankfurt criminal investigation department, which put him in so-called “preventive custody.” On July 10, 1941, he was transferred to the Dachau concentration camp. He died there eight months later, allegedly of heart failure. His last address in Frankfurt was Savignystrasse 55.

The Stolperstein for Johann Josef Bolzt was the first in Frankfurt to commemorate a victim of the persecution of homosexuals. It was initiated by gab magazine and the Initiative for a Memorial to the Persecution of Homosexuals, funded by Dr. Reinhard Dietrich, and laid in 2007.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
