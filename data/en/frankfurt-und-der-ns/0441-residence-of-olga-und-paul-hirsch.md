# residence of Olga und Paul Hirsch

*Neue Mainzer Straße 57*

![frankfurthistory@stadt-frankfurt.de](../../images/1e0b7c79a351939d86554bd72a5c51a4_original.jpg)
*1931/32 — Author: Leonhard Kleemann | License: HMF | Description: frankfurthistory@stadt-frankfurt.de*

This was the site of the residence of Paul Hirsch, a Frankfurt industrialist and music lover, and his wife Olga, née Ladenburg. An excellent violinist himself, Paul Hirsch built up a music library which gained a considerable international reputation. From 1909, Hirsch opened his library to experts in the field for their research, and it attracted international musicians, musicologists and students. An enthusiastic bibliophile, Olga Hirsch specialised in collecting exquisitely and elaborately bound books. In June 1936, when Frankfurt’s city administration discovered the couple were planning to emigrate to England, Mayor Friedrich Krebs tried to confiscate the renowned library. Although most of the music library could be taken to England by 12 August 1936, 24 crates were confiscated. In late October 1936, Professor Müller-Blattau, a music expert at Frankfurt University, and the library director Dr. Kirchner, expert for the Frankfurt police headquarters, drafted a report on the value of the books. Paul Hirsch was required to leave seven valuable works with the city of Frankfurt as a gift, otherwise the confiscated crates would not be released. The couple agreed, receiving no money in return. The seven valuable volumes were divided between the Municipal Library, the Kunstgewerbemuseum (Museum of Decorative Arts), and the Bibliothek für neuere Sprachen (formerly the Carl Mayer Freiherr von Rothschild public library ). Paul Hirsch offered his library to Cambridge University as a loan so that it could again benefit scholars and students. To secure their livelihood in Cambridge, the couple saw themselves having to sell individual items from the collection. In 1936, Paul and Olga Hirsch suffered a financial loss through the forced sale of their business and their property. The special taxes and unfavourable exchange rates set by the Nazi regime led to further losses when they transferred their assets to England. In 1938, the family’s remaining assets in Germany were confiscated. After the war and her husband’s death in 1951, Olga Hirsch continued to fight for financial compensation and the return of the books stolen from them. But this took several years, and the city of Frankfurt only returned the last book in 1953.
 
 Paul Hirsch provided the inspiration for the figure of Eduard Wertheim in Yesterday’s Streets, a novel his great-niece Silvia Tennenbaum. Published in America in 1981, her book was later issued in German as Straßen von gestern.
