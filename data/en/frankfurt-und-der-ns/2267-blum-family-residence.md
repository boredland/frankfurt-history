# Blum family residence

*Arndtstreet 51*

![The house in Arndtstr. 51 today](../../images/3954065ffa6923c08c89fdaa8da9bdbb_original.jpg)
*Author: Susanne Thurnay | License: Privat | Description: The house in Arndtstr. 51 today*

The house at Arndt street 51 was built in 1882–83 for investment purposes. In March 1907, Prof. Dr. Ferdinand Blum and his wife Emma bought the right half of the house. On November 18th, 1939, Ferdinand Blum transferred his share to his daughters to avoid confiscation as Jewish property. When the Nazis later also banned descendants of only one Jewish parent from owning property, the women passed their shares on to their non-Jewish husbands, the house was never confiscated.

Prof Blum had close contacts with Jewish colleagues and patients. After 1933, the Blum family considered it only natural to help their friends. The house developed into a contact point for persecuted Jews. Here they met with Ida & Louise Cook, who arranged visas and guarantees for their escape to England.

Gertrud Roesler-Erhardt continued the relief efforts even after her father's emigration in 1939. She smuggled money and valuables for friends who had often been forced to leave with only hand luggage. From October 1941, the family hid several acquaintances in the house to save them from deportation.

During the war, the house was badly damaged by an incendiary bomb. The façade was still standing, but most of the rear walls had collapsed, and the ceilings could only be saved by shoring them up. The residents made do as best they could in the ruins. It was not until 1951 that the family was able to rebuild the house.

*Author: Susanne Thurnay (Studienkreis Deutscher Widerstand 1933-1945 e.V.)*
