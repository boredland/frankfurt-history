# Synagogue of the Israelite Religious Society with mikvah

*Friedberger Anlage 6*

![View of the synagogue at Friedberger Anlage, 1907](../../images/1c012f953aaca0f88d8eef98460d3416_original.jpg)
*1907 — Author: info@frankfurt-und-der-ns.de | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte | Description: View of the synagogue at Friedberger Anlage, 1907*

This was the site of the synagogue of the “Israelitische Religionsgesellschaft” (IRG) founded by Rabbi Samson Raphael Hirsch in 1851. Its members were secessionist orthodox Jews who rejected the Reform movement’s modernisation of Judaism and had left the main Jewish community. The IRG established their own synagogue in Schützenstrasse, but this quickly became too small for the growing community. In 1905, after a call for donations, work began on a new synagogue in the Ostend district, a residential area favoured by the orthodox community members. The building costs amounted to over one million gold marks. With its mix of elements from Romanesque, Orientalism and Art Nouveau, the architecture deliberately set itself off from Christian churches. The interior was designed for the Orthodox ritual. The prayer hall, with 60 places for singers, could seat 1000 men on the ground floor and 600 women on the galleries. Apart from the community offices, the facilities included a mikvah (women’s bath), lecture rooms and a library with Judaica and Hebraica. After the synagogue was inaugurated in 1907, it was the largest synagogue in Frankfurt. In the pogroms on 10 November 1938 and the following days, SA and Hitler Youth members set fire to the synagogue, destroying the interior completely. Silver ritual objects as well as valuable Torah and bimah covers stored in the cellar were looted by the Gestapo. When the community council complained to the police, they started an investigation against Jewish arsonists. Ultimately in 1939, the community had to pay the cost of demolishing the building and was forced to sell the plot of land to the city administration. In 1942, the city used the site to construct a civil defence bunker, which Jews were not allowed to enter.
 In the 1950s, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) called for the bunker to be demolished and the land returned. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. In the end, the city of Frankfurt and the JRSO agreed on compensation amounting to DM 130,000, with the city also giving an undertaking to establish a permanent memorial in commemoration of the synagogue. The memorial site was opened in 1988.
