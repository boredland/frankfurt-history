# Investigation of Frankfurt Police Officers for Nazi Crimes against Sinti and Roma

*Frankfurt District Court*

![Final report on the investigation against Johannes Himmelheber](../../images/5cbb8615de27e23519ec0104a0d7959a_original.jpg)
*1947 — License: Hessisches Hauptstaatsarchiv Wiesbaden | Description: Final report on the investigation against Johannes Himmelheber*

Between 1948 and 1960, the Frankfurt district attorney’s office carried out several investigations into the persecution of Sinti and Roma under National Socialism. In addition to the proceedings against Robert Ritter and Eva Justin, these included inquiries into the Frankfurt police officers Johannes Himmelheber, Ernst Mohr, and Paul Fiedler.

From 1937 onwards, Johannes Himmelheber headed the Frankfurt internment camp for Sinti and Roma located first on Dieselstrasse and later on Kruppstrasse. First-hand witnesses described the extreme brutality with which he treated the inmates. During his interrogation, he deliberately belied the true circumstances. He negated the persecution situation, claiming instead that many Sinti and Roma had moved to the camp voluntarily in hopes of a more carefree life. Although he was initially categorized as an offender, he was ultimately permitted to re-enter police service in Frankfurt.

Ernst Mohr was the head of the “Office of Gypsy Affairs” at Frankfurt police headquarters and as such largely responsible for the camps’ operation and the deportation of approximately 100 Sinti and Roma to Auschwitz on March 9, 1943. During the investigation proceedings, he claimed that he had not known of their fates, either at the time or after the war. He was temporarily suspended from duty because of his membership in the Nazi party NSDAP. For his activities in the framework of the persecution of Sinti and Roma, however, he was categorized merely as a “follower” and rehabilitated in the 1950s. He received his full pension and in 1957 was even promoted retroactively.

Paul Fiedler was the head of a unit within the Frankfurt Criminal Investigation Department responsible for inspecting, among other agencies, the “Office for Gypsy Affairs.” He was among those officers who, in early 1943, drew up deportation lists of the Sinti and Roma living in Frankfurt. During his interrogation he blamed the persecution of Sinti and Roma in Frankfurt on the Gestapo and characterized his own role as insignificant. His denazification proceedings categorized him as “exonerated.”

All investigations were closed on April 18, 1958 on grounds that the defendants could not be found to have been “aware of the unlawfulness of the deprivation of liberty with the ultimate goal of extermination and nevertheless wanted to take action.” The course of the investigations and the justification for their discontinuation are representative examples of how the justice system handled the genocide of Sinti and Roma in the post-war period. There was a lack of serious willingness to investigate, however difficult it would have been to prove individual guilt. Conspicuously, whereas Sinti and Roma were characterized across the board as untrustworthy and self-serving, the defendants’ statements went largely unquestioned and unchecked. Anti-gypsy prejudice went hand in hand with the practice—prevalent in the 1950s—of sparing Nazi perpetrators from criminal prosecution for political reasons.

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
