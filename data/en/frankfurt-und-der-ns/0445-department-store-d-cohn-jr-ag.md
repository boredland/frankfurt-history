# Department store D. Cohn Jr. AG

*Zeil 109*

![Photo of department store D. Cohn Jr. AG](../../images/160e87af122a9fac8e7a80da045db971_original.jpg)
*ca. 1930 — Author: Lauterwasser | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-10249 | Description: Photo of department store D. Cohn Jr. AG*

D. Cohn Jr. AG was a family company located at Zeil 109. Founded by David Cohn, it specialized in ladies’ wear and fur goods. The building belonged to David’s wife Bertha. The couple had three children. Their son Fritz followed his father as company owner. One of the daughters, Gertrud Cohn, married Richard Purwin, a shareholder and member of the executive board of D. Cohn Jr. AG. In the early 1930s the company had about 300 employees and an annual turnover of three to four million Reichsmarks (RM).
 
 In April 1933, Fritz Cohn was arrested and detained for several days. In the face of threats, his brother-in-law Richard Purwin left the company. He and his wife emigrated to Johannesburg in present-day South Africa.
 
 To save the company, Fritz Cohn made the non-Jewish businessman Karl Stier a partner. The name was changed to Wagener & Schlötel. In 1938, Cohn was forced to sell his shares in the company as well as the business premises in Zeil to Karl Stier.
 
 In the autumn of 1938, Fritz Cohn travelled to London to explore business prospects there. In the wake of the November Pogroms, he did not return to Frankfurt but fled to Mozambique, then still a Portuguese colony, by way of Johannesburg. His children Ellen and Dieter managed to get to London on a Kindertransport in August 1939; his wife Else and his mother Bertha remained behind in Frankfurt.
 
 The two women also tried to emigrate. Bertha Cohn was unable to access the 546,000 RM she had received for the forced sale of the business premises because the customs investigation office had issued a "security order" on 15 September 1938. Measures of this kind were designed to prevent families from taking their assets with them when they left the country. From 14 December 1938 onwards, Bertha Cohn had free disposal of a mere 300 RM per month to pay her living expenses.
 
 From the money in her blocked account, she had to pay compulsory charges such as the “Reich flight tax” (65,000 RM), the “Jewish property tax” (24,650 RM), and a fee to the German Golddiskontbank (50,000 RM). She also paid the “Reich flight tax” for her son-in-law Felix Ochs, the husband of her daughter Martha. These payments were the condition on which the authorities approved a person’s departure. Felix and Martha Ochs managed to emigrate to the USA in 1939. Bertha and Else emigrated to Mozambique in February 1940. Before she left Germany, Bertha gave part of her remaining assets to the residents of Melemstrasse 11, where she had lived for a time.
