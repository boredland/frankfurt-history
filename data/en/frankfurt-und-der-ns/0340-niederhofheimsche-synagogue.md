# Niederhofheim'sche synagogue

*Rechneigrabenstraße 3-5*

![Photo of the exterior façade of Rechneigrabenstraße 3-5; the building housed, among other things, a synagogue, which was also used as a forced labour camp at times.](../../images/isg_ffm_s7_8b4b1707cc660d2fdf1c43063b62be75_original.jpg)
*1939 — Author: Fried Lübbecke | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 2005-207 | Description: Photo of the exterior façade of Rechneigrabenstraße 3-5; the building housed, among other things, a synagogue, which was also used as a forced labour camp at times.*

The first floor of Rechneigrabenstrasse 5 was home to the Niederhofheim Synagogue and the Russian-Polish Prayer Hall. Both these institutions are thought to have been inaugurated in the late 1800s. The Russian-Polish Prayer Hall was larger and could seat 120 men and 50 women. Polish or Lithuanian Jews who had come to Frankfurt had their own traditions of praying and learning, and so preferred to have their own Prayer Hall. The Niederhofheim Synagogue could seat 60 men and 20 women. The synagogue’s elaborately furnished interior also held valuable ritual objects including five sixteenth-century Torah scrolls, Torah ornaments, lamps and prayer books. One highlight was a sixteenth-century “memorbuch”, a memorial book for the community members who had died, and especially for those killed for their faith. The comprehensive library went back to Rabbi Benjamin Niederhofheim (1810-1885), who also gave his name to the synagogue. His private library contained rare books and sixteenth-century manuscripts. Talmud scholar Ephraim Ginsberg described the Niederhofheim Synagogue as “small, but with unforgettably beautiful furnishings”. 
 
 In the pogroms on 10 November 1938, members of the Hitler Youth and the SA (Storm Troopers) desecrated the synagogue and prayer hall. 
 
 The building belonged to Emilie Cohn, née Perlstein, who was forced to sell it. From 1942, it was used as accommodation for forced labourers at the Messer & Co. company at the Osthafen. The building was totally destroyed during an Allied bombing raid.
 
 After the war, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) registered a claim for compensation for the destruction of the interior, the library, and the ritual objects. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. The compensation authority agreed to pay the JRSO DM 105,000.
