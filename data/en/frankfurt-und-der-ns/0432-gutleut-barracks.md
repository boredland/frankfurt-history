# Gutleut Barracks

*Gutleutstraße 112-116*

![DXR](../../images/29bbed895c86b6c37bc0e5308c3999d4_original.jpg)
*n.d. — Author: DXR | License: CC BY-SA 4.0 | Description: DXR*

The Police Battalion 306 was trained in summer 1940 at the Gutleut Barracks. The participants were “volunteers” from today’s federal states of Hesse, Rhineland-Palatinate, Baden-Württemberg (wider Mannheim area) and Saarland as well as professional police officers who oversaw the basic training. The volunteers had previously worked as painters and decorators, bricklayers, roofers, carpenters or as public service employees. Professional police officers were favoured for the leadership positions in Police Battalion 306. They were appointed, for instance, as “Gruppenführer” (literally “group leader”) or “Zugführer” (“platoon commander”). The battalion's commander was the 48-year-old Major Ernst Dreier. He belonged to the Nazi Party’s so-called “Old Fighters”, a group who, for example, as members of the paramilitary SS (“Schutzstaffel”) had paved the way for the regime to take power by violently persecuting and murdering its political opponents. On 3 October 1940, the battalion was sent to Lublin in today’s Poland. Police Battalion 306 quickly developed into an exceptionally brutal unit taking part in numerous war crimes. Their victims not only included Jews but also Polish civilians. The battalion also forcibly collected food from the Polish farmers, and set fire to the farmhouses of anyone not contributing enough. During such actions, the battalion also regularly murdered civilians. In late October 1942, the cruelty and brutality of Police Battalion 306 was again prominent in “clearing” the Pinsk Ghetto. For the Nazis, “clearing” was the term for deporting people to concentration or extermination camps or murdering them in mass shootings. In the “clearing” action, members of the Police Battalion 306 threw hand grenades into some of the houses, attacked people with axes, battens and truncheons and even shot infants and small children. Around 20,000 Jews were murdered. In November 1942, they killed the residents of two villages in Belarus and then set the villages on fire. After the end of the war, 33 members of Police Battalion 306 were again working for Frankfurt’s police force. They only first had to confront their crimes in court cases held from 1962. In this process, 218 former battalion members were interrogated. Although some made confessions, only six former battalion members received sentences of up to three years in prison.
