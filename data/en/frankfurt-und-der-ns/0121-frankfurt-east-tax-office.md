# Frankfurt-East Tax Office

*Stiftstraße 9-17*

![Picture postcard with the Hansahaus](../../images/7cc4f4861b9f5e93db070a4c60957310_original.jpg)
*1903 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF / Sammlung Church C80383 | Description: Picture postcard with the Hansahaus*

This was the location of the Frankfurt East and West tax authorities. Just like all other tax authorities across the German Reich, they also worked to financially disadvantage those persecuted as Jews and ultimately rob them of their entire financial assets. The “Reich flight tax” (“Reichsfluchtsteuer”) imposed on prospective emigrants was one instrument to achieve this aim. Originally introduced in 1931 by the government under Reich Chancellor Heinrich Brüning, it primarily targeted the very wealthy or high earners. But in May 1934 the Nazi regime reduced the tax-free allowance so radically that even members of the middle class had to pay a mandatory 25% of their assets and income. The remaining money could only be exchanged into foreign currency by the Deutsche Golddiskontbank, a process incurring high fees. The “Reich flight tax” was one of the most lucrative sources of income for the Nazi regime. 
 Anyone emigrating without paying this compulsory tax had all their assets confiscated, the fate of many political opponents and writers who hastily fled Germany in fear of their lives in spring 1933. However, this punitive tax mostly affected those persecuted as Jews who fled abroad.
 
 From 1934, the Nazi regime took measures to strip foundations of their charitable status if their objectives were not congruent with the ideological aim of “benefiting the ‘Volksgemeinschaft’” (“national racial community”). In a first step, these foundations were deprived of tax advantages, making their work more difficult. After the Nuremberg Laws to protect so-called “racial purity” came into force in 1935, the foundations faced further restrictions. Now, all non-profit foundations not purely supporting members of the “Volksgemeinschaft” had to pay the full corporation and property tax. 
 
 From 1936, all child benefits for Jewish families were cut. In 1938, they also lost any claim to tax allowances for children. Under a new ordinance in April that same year, Jews were required to register their entire assets. After the November Pogroms, under the so-called “Jewish property tax” (“Judenvermögensabgabe”) all Jews with over 5,000 Reichsmark had to pay a tax amounting to 25% of their assets. If the tax office heard of or even suspected a plan to emigrate, it could “secure” a quarter of the person’s assets, i.e., freezing accounts so the owner could no longer access them. From 1939, Jews had to pay far higher taxes than other citizens, and could no longer claim against tax the costs of mortgage savings plans, or health and accident insurance. From 1941, they had to pay an additional “social compensation tax” (“Sozialausgleichsabgabe”) amounting to 15% of their income. This tax was also levied on the meagre wages of Polish forced labourers. This series of measures illustrates just how hard it was for Jewish families to scrape together enough money for their daily lives, their children’s education and training, and for the costs of emigration as well. Ultimately, the tax authorities also played their part in expropriating people deported to concentration and death camps, ghettos and mass execution sites.
