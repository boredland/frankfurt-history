# Villa Buchenrode

*Niederräder Landstraße 38*

![frankfurthistory@stadt-frankfurt.de](../../images/1e0b7c79a351939d86554bd72a5c51a4_original.jpg)
*Author: unbekannt | License: HMF | Description: frankfurthistory@stadt-frankfurt.de*

The chemist, entrepreneur, and patron of the arts Dr. Arthur von Weinberg lived here at Haus Buchenrode (formerly Forsthausstrasse 148) until 1938. He had moved into the newly built villa in 1909 with his wife Wilhelmine, née Huygens, and their two adopted daughters. The racecourse and the Waldfried Stud Farm, which he co-managed with his brother Carl, were located nearby. During World War I, while her husband was serving as a major in the cavalry on the Western Front, Wilhelmine and her daughters had run a military hospital for the severely injured in the villa.
 Arthur von Weinberg, who had been entitled to bear the Prussian title of nobility since 1907, was a founding benefactor of the University of Frankfurt and honorary president of numerous companies as well as head of the Cassella dye works and co-founder and member of the supervisory board of I.G. Farbenindustrie AG (dye industry syndicate stock corporation). The Senckenbergische Naturforschende Gesellschaft (Society of Natural Sciences) and the Städelsches Kunstinstitut (Städel Art Institute) were among the institutions he supported. He was granted honorary citizenship of Frankfurt in 1930.
 Originally from a Jewish entrepreneurs’ family, Arthur von Weinberg had been baptized a Protestant in 1880. He and his wife were members of the Protestant Paul Gerhardt congregation. After the Nazi accession to power in 1933, he was initially unaffected by the anti-Jewish laws owing to his status as a decorated front-line fighter. That changed when the Nuremberg Race Laws went into force on 15 September 1935. Now he was subject to persecution on account of his Jewish descent, forced out of his offices, and robbed of his fortune. On 30 December 1938, he had to sell the villa, complete with the park, to the city far below value and surrender the proceeds to the municipal revenue office. From 1939 to 1945, the city housed the Musisches Gymnasium here, an elite Nazi boarding school for musically gifted boys. 
 By 1940, Arthur von Weinberg’s assets had shrunk from 14 to 1.3 million Reichsmarks (RM). He had managed to sign over some 8 million RM to his daughters. The rest he lost to compulsory charges such as the “Jewish property tax” and the “Reich flight tax”. The remainder was in accounts to which he had no access; he was permitted to dispose of only 10,000 RM per month. After the forced sale of his villa, Arthur von Weinberg, whose wife had died in 1935, moved to the home of his adopted daughter at Pähl Castle on Lake Ammer. The eighty-two-year-old was arrested there on 2 June 1942 and deported to Terezín, where he died on 20 March 1943. In 2012, a Stolperstein commemorating Arthur von Weinberg was installed at Buchenrodestrasse 26, the site of his former villa.
