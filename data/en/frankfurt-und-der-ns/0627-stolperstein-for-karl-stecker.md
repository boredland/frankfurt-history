# Stolperstein for Karl Stecker

*Frankenallee 60*

![Stolperstein for Karl Stecker at Frankenallee 60](../../images/fcce203541ba5bb19f56aa06deabc1cd_original.jpg)
*2019 — Author: Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt am Main | License: Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt am Main | Description: Stolperstein for Karl Stecker at Frankenallee 60*

Karl Stecker (1900-1942)

Karl Stecker was born in Frankfurt am Main on 13 January 1900. His parents ran a kiosk in the Bockenheim district of Frankfurt. He was a christened Protestant, single, and a mechanic; his last address was Frankenallee 60. From 1932 onward he was convicted repeatedly of violations of Section 175 of the German Criminal Code. In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and was severely tightened in 1935. In 1934, Karl Stecker was sentenced to a particularly harsh punishment of two years in prison and three years of loss of honor, which in practical terms meant the loss of voting rights, memberships in clubs, and public positions. The court also ordered his “preventive custody”—that is, indefinite imprisonment—after serving his sentence. Stecker spent the following years in various prisons and institutions, including Freiendiez, Ziegenhain, Schwäbisch Hall, Esterwegen, and Butzbach. In 1942, like many other prisoners in “preventive custody,” he was handed over to the SS and deported to the Mauthausen concentration camp. He died shortly after his arrival at the Gusen subcamp in December 1942. A particularly remarkable feature of Karl Stecker’s persecution is that in 1946 he was listed as wanted because his “preventive custody” was to be continued. The files were not closed until 1948 after the police reported that he had probably been transferred to a concentration camp and died there.

The Stolperstein was initiated by Martin Dill and Heidi Stögbauer of the Frankfurt Stolperstein Initiative, funded by the “Mainsirenen e.V.” men’s choir, and laid in 2019.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
