# Synagogue of the Jewish Community Heddernheim

*Alt Heddernheim 33*

![Placeholder image](../../images/Hier fehlt_dfdc6d631f271e842082099187aa5623_original.jpg)
*License: HMF*

Replacing an older dilapidated synagogue, the new building from around 1760 provided seating for around 48 men and 32 women. It also included a mikvah (women’s bath) as well as an apartment for a religious teacher. In 1842, the synagogue inventory listed six Torah scrolls, eight silver Torah ornaments, six embroidered curtains and several other silver ritual objects. Some of these were several hundred years old, testifying to the long Jewish history in this region. The Prayer Hall walls may also have been decorated with murals. Certainly, renovations in 1925 uncovered a fresco on the eastern wall showing an idealised picture of Jerusalem. During the 1938 November Pogroms, the synagogue and community centre were ransacked and vandalised, and the Torah scrolls and prayer books destroyed. This is one of the few cases where the names of the perpetrators are known, and their leader was later convicted. According to the statements in court in 1953, on 9 November 1938 the district leaders of the Nazi Party and the German Labour Front (DAF) ordered SA Obersturmführer (first lieutenant) Paul Venten “to play the people” and take part in the coordinated wave of antisemitic terror. Venten was personnel manager at the Telefonbau und Normalzeit GmbH, a company expropriated from its original Jewish founder, businessman Harry Fuld. Together with 20 company employees dressed in civilian clothes, Venten roamed the inner city, chanting antisemitic slogans to incite the crowds. They wrecked several Jewish shops and businesses before they took a truck to Heddernheim. After the pogroms, Heddernheim’s Jews were forced to move into so-called “Jews’ houses” before they were deported. In 1942 the synagogue and community centre were forcibly sold and demolished one year later. In the 1960s, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) submitted a claim for compensation of DM 254,235 for the destruction of the synagogue and the ritual objects. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. The compensation authority argued the exact loss could not be established and so “an amount of DM 83,000 (... would be...) appropriate to compensate for the entire damage”.
 
 In 1988, a memorial stone and plaque was set up at the site of synagogue.
