# Prisoner-of-war camp

*Wilhelmshöher Straße 146-148*

![Picture postcard with exterior view of the inn “Zum Löwen”](../../images/04b963907928821cbca9c50a0297bcf2_original.jpg)
*ca. 1900 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF / Sammlung Church C80413 | Description: Picture postcard with exterior view of the inn “Zum Löwen”*

After the war began, all factories adapted their production to the needs of the Wehrmacht (the German army under National Socialism). The J. S. Fries Sohn metal company founded in 1748, for example (the builder of the Eiserner Steg bridge), manufactured tank parts and submarine sections. To uphold production vital to the war effort, the company made use of several hundred forced labourers. Many of them had to replace the employees who had been called up for military service. Fries operated altogether four camps during World War II. One of them was in a residential building here at Wilhelmshöher Strasse 146–148. The forced labourers presumably worked in a company factory located at Daimlerstrasse 40 in the Osthafen (east harbour), which was about a 45-minute walk from here. The Institut für Stadtgeschichte (Institute of Municipal History) documented that, in 1943, 71 Russians were lodged here, 8 of whom were women, as well as 19 French and 15 Dutch persons. In 1946, the 6th police district reported to the United Nations Relief and Rehabilitation Administration (UNRRA) that 30 forced labourers from France, but also forced labourers from the Netherlands, Belgium, and Ukraine had been lodged on these premises. The UNRRA was in charge of registering persons from the member states of the U.N., looking after them, and helping them return to their home countries. The 6th police district also reported that the French prisoners of war had previously been in custody in Bad Orb, presumably in the Stalag Ixb camp. There they were assigned to a labour detachment and sent to Frankfurt. They were here from March 1943 to March 1945. Frankfurt was liberated by Allied troops on 29 March 1945 and the prisoners of war were able to return to France. The document is in the holdings of the Arolsen Archives.
