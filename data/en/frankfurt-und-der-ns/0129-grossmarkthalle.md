# Großmarkthalle

*Sonnemannstraße 20*

![Photo of the wholesale market hall](../../images/C27570a_jp_b295c3b34ea83e686f08bab4b7a56c2c_original.jpg)
*before 1929 — Author: Südwestdeutsche Luftverkehrs AG | License: HMF C27570a | Description: Photo of the wholesale market hall*

Between October 1941 and September 1942, the Secret State Police (Gestapo) rented the eastern section of the wholesale market hall cellar to serve as an assembly point for ten mass deportations. The Chief Office of National Security organized the arrest and deportation of persons subject to persecution because they were Jewish. The Gestapo oversaw the operations on the local level. The first deportation on 19 October 1941, in which 1,180 citizens of Frankfurt were transported to Lodz Ghetto, was carried out with help from members of the Storm Troopers (SA) and the National Socialist German Workers’ Party. They were tasked with fetching the people from their homes and guarding them. For later deportations, officers of the criminal investigation department and the uniformed police provided assistance. Some 10,000 women, men, and children from Frankfurt and the surrounding region were led down the ramp here and into the cellar. As the cellar was usually used to store fruit and vegetables for the market, the rooms were very cold. Gestapo officers checked the people’s identification documents and luggage and carried out brutal and humiliating body searches on the men. Welfare office staff searched the women and children. Tax officials collected the eight-page list of assets filled out by the families beforehand, and the keys to their homes. The persecution victims were required to pay for their train tickets themselves. Then they waited in a room with mattresses laid out on the floor. Many of them were subjected to humiliating and abusive treatment there. Some died. German Reich Railway trains transported them from the tracks outside the hall to ghettoes, concentration and extermination camps, and execution sites. These destinations were located in Eastern European countries occupied by the Wehrmacht (the German army under National Socialism). The daily market operations continued regardless of these events. Eyewitness reports provide evidence that the wholesale market employees were aware of what was happening. Between 1943 and 1945, eighteen further deportations left Frankfurt from the market hall, the East Station, and the Main Station.
 
 In 2015, a place of remembrance was established in Philipp-Holzmann-Weg on the left side of the former wholesale market hall. The Jewish Museum of Frankfurt offers guided tours and workshops for school pupils here. Quotations by victims, survivors, and witnesses are inscribed in the paths and walls.
