# Forced Labor Camp Adlerwerke

*Kleyerstreet 17*

![Aerial photograph taken by the US Air Force in 1944. The Adlerwerke factory is in the center. Forced labor camps were located on the premises and in the adjacent streets.](../../images/9550d7d7a180977deb9f9062b884f130_original.jpg)
*1944 — Author: US-Army | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Sig. S6b-93 Nr. A-14-4 | Description: Aerial photograph taken by the US Air Force in 1944. The Adlerwerke factory is in the center. Forced labor camps were located on the premises and in the adjacent streets.*

During the First and Second World Wars, the Adlerwerke (formerly Heinrich Kleyer AG) was an armaments production site. Under the Nazi regime, it manufactured engines and transmissions for tanks, airplanes, and military vehicles. Starting in 1942, the management took advantage of the labor of thousands of foreign forced laborers, prisoners of war, and concentration camp inmates. These people were lodged in several camps in Frankfurt. There were separate camps for forced laborers from Western and Eastern European countries. Those from Russia, White Russia (now Belarus), and Ukraine were subjected to the worst living conditions. For Western European forced laborers, there were camps on Kleyerstrasse and one on Krifteler Strasse. For so-called Eastern workers there were two barracks on Weilburger Strasse and two barrack camps on the grounds between plants I and II. In 1943, a camp for Italian military internees was set up on Schwalbacher Strasse. A portion of the “Eastern workers” and the concentration camp inmates were lodged directly in the factory building on Kleyerstrasse. The Western workers had their quarters in so-called communal camps on Kleyerstrasse 45 and 82.

For the Adlerwerke’s forced laborers, there were further camps on Weilburger Strasse (for Eastern Europeans), and on Krifteler Strasse 47 next to plant III. In September 1942, the both sites lodged nearly 800 forced laborers, and in 1943 1,727, including 545 Russians, 491 Ukrainians, and 618 French people.
