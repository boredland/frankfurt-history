Between persecution and a new start: the life of Hans Bauer

Hans Bauer (1918–2013) was born in Frankfurt, and it was here that he experienced the National Socialist era. Following the pogroms in November 1938, he fled to the USA, where he started a new life, shaped by persecution and emigration.

Life in the German Empire

Hans Bauer was born in Frankfurt in 1918, the second child of Hugo and Martha Bauer. Before 1933, the family led a happy life and had few ties with the Jewish faith. When the National Socialists seized power in 1933, Hugo Bauer lost his job and had to retire. Similarly, his son Hans was forced to leave Goethe-Gymnasium and began a carpentry apprenticeship.
In 1936, Hans’s sister Hildegard emigrated to Palestine. His father, Hugo, escaped to the USA. And in 1937, following a lengthy wait in Hamburg, his mother Martha and youngest sister Doris fled to the USA. Hans initially remained in Frankfurt, where he was active in the Zionist movement and lived in Beth Haluz, the Jewish “pioneer house”. After he was attacked and injured following the pogroms in November 1938, Hans decided to leave Germany. On obtaining a visa in 1939, he escaped to Paris and then to the USA

Life after emigrating

In the beginning, Hans had a difficult time in the USA. Having married Eva Guggenheim in 1940, he joined the US Army and fought against the Germans in the Second World War.
Hans and Eva had three children and the family was very much affected by the persecution. During an interview in 1990, Hans spoke of the horrible experiences that shaped his life and that of his family. The laying of memorial stones for the Bauer family in Frankfurt on 29 October 2024 was, for the family, a reminder and an acknowledgment of all they had gone through.

Emigration

Following the pogroms in November 1938, many Jewish people like Hans Bauer wanted to escape from Germany. However, the National Socialists imposed strict rules to make emigration more difficult. This meant that families not only needed a lot of money to emigrate, but they also had to have a sponsor and a visa. Besides this, there were difficult bureaucratic hurdles to overcome and people were forced to leave almost everything behind. They also had to pay high taxes. As of 1939, people who wished to leave Germany had to pay an emigration levy as well.
There was an aid centre that provided assistance to Jews who wanted to flee Germany, helping them deal with the authorities and covering the costs of those who did not have much money. These costs were financed by donations and by the Jewish community. Although some 7,000 Jews from Frankfurt were able to flee between 1938 and 1941, it became increasingly difficult to do so once war broke out in September 1939.
