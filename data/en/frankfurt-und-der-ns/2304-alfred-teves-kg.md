# Alfred Teves KG

*Marburger Street 28*

![Street front of the building complex at Marburger Straße 28.](../../images/Marburger%20_321e981a642b3e71589ec0555bd25c3d_original.jpg)
*2025 — Author: Christine Hartwig-Thürmer | License: Christine Hartwig-Thürmer | Description: Street front of the building complex at Marburger Straße 28.*

Forced Labor Camp

Before the Nazi Party came to power, this building housed the Bockenheimer Turngemeinde 1860, a gymnastics club. Here, it had a sports hall, club rooms with a ballroom, as well as apartments, a kindergarten and a large garden.

But from 1 September 1942, the building was rented for 1,900 Reichsmark a month by the auto component producer Alfred Teves KG. The plan was to house 526 forced laborers from the Soviet Union here and deploy them at 17 different companies. Since the Alfred Teves company had overall responsibility for this assembly camp, it also organized, against payment and food ration cards, the guards and provisions for the inmates. However, only six companies made use of the places available, suggesting a striking lack of labor. In December 1942, typhus and typhoid fever broke out among the around 160 inmates, both women and men. The illnesses were supposedly brought into the camp by new arrivals from the Kelsterbach transit camp.

Since the camp was far from fully utilized, the authorities planned to turn it into a “camp for western forced labor” on 1 June 1943. Around 100 French, Belgian and Dutch forced laborers at the Kolben-Seeger company were working in the precision workshops in Adalbertstraße 44-48 but accommodated at Darmstädter Landstraße 380 on the southern edge of the city.

On 31 May 1943, though, the Alfred Teves KG’s legal department wrote to the responsible Deutsche Arbeitsfront (German Labor Front), the Nazi Party’s national labor organization: “Due to further arrivals of Eastern workers, we see ourselves compelled to keep the Bockenheimer Turngemeinde building as a labor camp for Eastern workers.”

Even if the assembly camp still had free capacity, it certainly proved worthwhile for the Alfred Teves company: “The rent amounted to 1,900,-RM /month; in January 1943, 232 [sic! 231] Eastern workers were accommodated in the sports hall: 231 x 0.50 x 30 days = 3,450 RM. With a difference of 1,550,- RM, this camp was profitable, even taking into account expenditure on food.”

Walja M., born in 1923, was a young Catholic Eastern worker. Taken from the camp to the St. Elisabethen Hospital in Ederstraße, she died there  of “cardiac insufficiency and pneumonia“ in August 1944. Her body was cremated and her ashes buried on today’s burial site for victims of Nazi tyranny on Frankfurt’s Main Cemetery. In 1945, the inmates at the Marburger Straße 28 assembly camp even included babies of French parents who worked as forced laborers at the Günther & Co precision tool factory in Voltastraße. As yet, nothing more is known of their fate.
