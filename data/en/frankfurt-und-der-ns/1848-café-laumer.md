# Café Laumer

*Bockenheimer Landstraße 67*

A place for discussions and encounters

A place to talk and meet people
Café Laumer has been around since 1919. Located at Bockenheimer Landstrasse 67 in Frankfurt, it is a meeting place for people of all ages and a piece of the city’s history. In the café’s early years, it was frequented by social philosophers like Theodor W. Adorno, Jürgen Habermas and Max Horkheimer, who came there to discuss sociology and social criticism. They also argued with other people, including philosopher Karl Mannheim, who was a proponent of what was known as critical consciousness. With the opening of the first university in Frankfurt’s Westend district in 1914, the café was al so a popular haunt for professors, who discussed political topics, new books and professional developments there. When the National Socialists seized power in 1933, the government closed down the Institute for Social Research for both political and racial reasons. Jewish philosophers like Adorno and Horkheimer were forced to flee Germany. In 1950, the café once again became a meeting place for intellectuals. In the 1960s, left-wing students used it for their protests, calling it “Café Marx”.
