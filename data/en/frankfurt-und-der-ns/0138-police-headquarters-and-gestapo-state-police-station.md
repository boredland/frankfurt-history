# Police headquarters and Gestapo state police station

*Friedrich-Ebert-Anlage 5-11*

![Photo of the police headquarters and Gestapo state police station](../../images/da657d6e351eafeccf8d27e3dda3a6ce_original.jpg)
*1933 — Author: Hannah Reeck | License: HMF, Ph03225a | Description: Photo of the police headquarters and Gestapo state police station*

Until 2002, the police headquarters were located at Hohenzollernanlage 5-11 (now Friedrich-Ebert-Anlage). In 1933, the police were “gleichgeschaltet”, aligned with Nazi goals. Any police officers who were against the Nazi regime were dismissed – without the right to a pension. Frankfurt’s Police President Steinberg was removed from office directly on 30 January 1933. Criminal Police Councillor Ferdinand Mührdel, head of the political police, also had to fear for his job. During the years of the Weimar Republic, he unwaveringly opposed the Nazis and had received many threats. In April 1933, Mührdel was taken into custody accused of allegedly accepting bribes and other crimes. His show trial in October 1933 ended with a sentence of seven months imprisonment. After he was released, the Gestapo continued to keep him under surveillance, and he was dismissed from his public service position. But the police were not just forcibly aligned with Nazi goals. From 22 February 1933, they also had to work with a new “auxiliary police” force (“Hilfspolizei”) comprising SA, SS and Stahlhelm members who brutally attacked political opponents. In March 1933, Hesse’s regular police force consisted of 2,158 officers compared to 4,594 SA and SS auxiliary police ready to accompany them on patrol. Moreover, in mid-February 1933 Reinhard von Westrem, an SA “Standartenführer” (equivalent to a colonel), was appointed as Frankfurt’s new Police President. There was also a change in leadership on the regional level. Hesse’s Social Democrat interior minister Wilhelm Leuschner was dismissed. From 6 March, he was replaced by Nazi Party member Dr. Heinrich Müller, who then controlled the regional police force in Hesse. This change resulted in a wave of house searches and arrests of political opponents such as Communists, Social Democrats and trade unionists. From 30 April 1933, the Frankfurt Gestapo also initially had its offices in the police headquarters.

 

Frankfurt Gestapo

Established in April 1933, the Gestapo with its Frankfurt representative office was housed in the Frankfurt police headquarters from April 30, 1933 to July 1936 and was responsible for the administrative district of Wiesbaden. It emerged from the political department of the police headquarters and quickly developed into the heart of the Nazi persecution apparatus.
In the years leading up to 1937, it was responsible for the destruction of opposition parties, trade unions and their organizations. It persecuted Jews, unpopular associations, lodges, rebellious church people, homosexuals and non-conformists. In total, it monitored around one million people during this time. Interrogations were carried out brutally using beatings and the “unity stick”. During so-called “aggravated interrogations”, the Gestapo men beat people under the eyes of a doctor - often until they fainted.
In 1936, the office moved to Bürgerstraße 22 (now Wilhelm-Leuscher-Straße).
