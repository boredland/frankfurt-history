General background to the November pogroms of 1938

The pogroms of November 1938 marked a key turning point in the Nazi regime’s antisemitic policies. The event that served as a trigger was the assassination of German diplomat Ernst vom Rath in Paris on 7 November 1938 by 17-year-old Herschel Grynszpan. Grynszpan had been driven to desperation by the deportation of his parents and many other Polish Jews from Germany. The National Socialist regime used the assassination as a pretext for organised violence against Jews which, on the night of 9 November 1938, culminated in the most violent antisemitic outbreak in Nazi Germany up to that point. In a night of violence coordinated by the Gestapo, synagogues were burnt down, Jewish homes and businesses destroyed and some 100 Jewish people murdered. Around 30,000 Jewish men were arrested and sent to concentration camps.

The events of the November pogroms in Frankfurt

In Frankfurt, the pogroms escalated in the early hours of the morning of 10 November 1938. As early as 5 a.m., groups of SA men in civilian clothing were destroying Jewish homes and businesses. Synagogues – including the main synagogue – were torched. More than 3,000 Jewish men from Frankfurt and the vicinity were arrested by the Gestapo and registered before being taken to the Festhalle. Here, they were forced to surrender their valuables and passports and were detained under humiliating conditions. The men were then taken from the Festhalle to the Südbahnhof train station and sent to concentration camps. Some of the prisoners did not survive the journey. Since 1991 there has been a memorial plaque on the rotunda of the Festhalle in remembrance of these events.
