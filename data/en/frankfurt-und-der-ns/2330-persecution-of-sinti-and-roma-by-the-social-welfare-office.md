# Persecution of Sinti and Roma by the Social Welfare Office

*Braubachstraße 18-22*

![The building of the municipal health department also contained the welfare office. Photo: HMF.](../../images/5a3d7d7bd8704bcf59ba3dc8e079ba7c_original.jpg)
*ca. 1936 — Author: Max Göllner | License: HMF, NS1366 | Description: The building of the municipal health department also contained the welfare office. Photo: HMF.*

Social Welfare Office

The Frankfurt social welfare office played a key role in the systematic discrimination and persecution of Sinti and Roma. It was already this office that oversaw the implementation of the municipal resolution to establish a so-called “concentration camp” on Friedberger Landstrasse back in 1928. Sinti and Roma were to be forced to relocate from the inner city to the camp on its outermost periphery. At that point in time, however, there were no instruments for compulsory committal to the camp. By the time the internment camp was established on Dieselstrasse in 1937, however, that had changed. This new camp for Sinti and Roma was set up at the behest of Werner Fischer-Defoy, who headed Frankfurt’s social welfare and health offices from 1933 to 1945. The social welfare office was in charge of the camp’s establishment and furnishings; the police took over its surveillance. This cooperation continued after the camp was relocated to Kruppstrasse. Already early on, the welfare office called for the appointment of Johannes Himmelheber—a police officer known for his brutality—as camp head. Himmelheber was never held accountable for his crimes.

Frankfurt’s social welfare office moreover played a substantial role in the organization of “Operation Work-Shy,” which was carried out in June 1938. Within this framework, people were vilified as “asocial” and deported to concentration camps on the pretext of “preventive crime control.” The social welfare office gathered information about these people and passed it on to the criminal investigation department. The target of placing at least 200 persons in “preventive custody” was willingly exceeded. More than 400 were deported to concentration camps. In retrospect, a police officer assessed “Operation Work-Shy” as “the first major operation against gypsies.”

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
