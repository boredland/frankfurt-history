# Martha Wertheimer und die Kindertransporte

*Guiollettstraße 18*

![Martha Wertheimer arbeitend an einem Schreibtisch.](../../images/22126d301444164d4beab36be2b5c5e1_original.jpg)
*License: © Jüdisches Museum Frankfurt, Signatur: B2002/002-014d | Description: Martha Wertheimer arbeitend an einem Schreibtisch.*

Martha Wertheimer (1890–1942) was a Jewish teacher, journalist and women’s rights activist who managed to save countless children during the Nazi era thanks to her work with the Jugendhilfe organisation.

Martha Wertheimer was born to Jewish parents Julius and Johanna Wertheimer and lived in Frankfurt. After receiving her doctorate in philosophy in 1917, she fought for women’s right to vote. Later she would write for Jewish weekly newspaper Israelitisches Familienblatt about Jewish emigration and educating young people. When the National Socialists came to power, Martha lost her job, her apartment and her freedom. In 1938, she began helping Jewish children to leave Germany, ultimately saving the lives of many children. Martha Wertheimer was unswerving in her efforts. As well as organising transport for the children, she also looked after their visas and gave them lessons. In a letter to her friend Siegfried Guggenheimer, she wrote: “It is hard work [...] but it still means something to be an officer on the sinking ship of Judaism.” She was very clear: “I have no regrets. I have been able to experience life in all its depths and I have known the happiness of being indispensable.” In 1942, Martha and her sister Lydia were sent to the Sobibor extermination camp. Today, her courage and tireless efforts are commemorated by a memorial stone and by a square named after her in Frankfurt: Martha-Wertheimer-Platz.
