# Forced Labour Camp for Sinti and Roma

*Dieselstraße 40*

![Placeholder image](../../images/Hier%20fehlt_dfdc6d631f271e842082099187aa5623_original.jpg)
*License: HMF*

In August 1937, the City of Frankfurt set up an internment camp for Sinti and Roma at this site. It received support in this undertaking from the municipal welfare office. On 18 August, the police brought 55 women, children, and men here. Between January 1938 and May 1941, the number of inmates increased from 122 to 160. Ultimately the camp lodged 300 persons. Initially Sinti and Roma living in caravan parks in Frankfurt were arrested and committed to this camp; later the Gestapo also rounded up numerous persons from their private homes. From 1938 onwards, the camp was also used to lodge families from Darmstadt, Worms, and Mainz. The grounds were surrounded by a high barbed-wire fence and guarded. The camp commander was the policeman Johannes Himmelheber, who threatened and beat the residents or tormented them by forcing them to carry out pointless work. Only those assigned to forced labour were able to leave the camp during the daytime. The living conditions were inhumane: the lodgings consisted of caravans converted in makeshift manner to house three families each. There were only eight toilets and two sources of fresh water in total, and neither electricity nor heating. The people held here had to pay five Reichmarks rent per week for their lodgings. Until 1940, the children were permitted to go to school outside the camp. After that they were no longer admitted to schools. One of the children who lived in the camp was Herbert Adler, who later recalled: “Dieselstrasse—that meant saying goodbye to school friends and the kids from the neighbourhood and living in a caravan instead of a flat. … Roll calls, orders, curfew, forced labour, and the cold. At night-time the barking of the police patrol dogs. Dieselstrasse—that meant, for the first time: fear! And again and again the question asked by children—why?”
 
 Persons held in the camp at Dieselstrasse were constantly being committed to concentration camps. One Sinti woman recalled that fellow inmates were deported to Dachau in 1937. In June 1938, all male Sinti were transported to Buchenwald. Others were probably also deported in May 1940. Throughout the Reich, the major deportations of the Sinti and Roma were organized by the police criminal investigation department.
 
 The camp in Dieselstrasse was cleared in September/October 1942. The Sinti and Roma still living there were moved to a camp in Kruppstrasse. In the spring of 1943 they were deported to the Auschwitz-Birkenau concentration and extermination camp by way of the East Station. One of them was Herbert Adler. He and two of his siblings were the only ones of their family of nine to survive.
