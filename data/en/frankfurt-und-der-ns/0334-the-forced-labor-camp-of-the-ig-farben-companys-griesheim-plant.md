# The Forced Labor Camp of the I.G. Farben Company’s Griesheim Plant

*Stroofstraße 27*

![Photograph of a chemical plant belonging to I.G. Farben-Industrie AG; there was a barracks camp on the factory premises in Stroofstraße in Griesheim](../../images/4776fa2871647976672aaa2f47f17a44_original.jpg)
*1927 — Author: Südwestdeutsche Luftverkehrs AG | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-22672 | Description: Photograph of a chemical plant belonging to I.G. Farben-Industrie AG; there was a barracks camp on the factory premises in Stroofstraße in Griesheim*

In the period under Nazi rule, the I.G. Farben company’s Griesheim plant was the largest employer in the district. The Griesheim Chemical Factory established back in 1856 had been one of the six founding members of the I.G. Farben conglomerate in 1925. In August 1940, the first group of foreign workers—249 Belgians—arrived. Over the subsequent months, they were followed by people from Slovakia, Italy, the Netherlands, the Soviet Union, France, and Poland. French prisoners of war, Italian military internees, and “civilian workers” from different countries were put to work in the factory. Depending on their countries of origin, the Nazis divided them into “Western” and “Eastern workers.” So-called “Eastern workers” were often subjected to harsher treatment than their “Western” counterparts.

Although it is no longer possible to determine the exact total number of forced laborers, a card file containing cards on more than 900 persons as well as photos and memos has come down to us. The workers were initially lodged in existing, factory-owned “welfare buildings” at Stroofstrasse 7 and in various buildings on the factory grounds. In the late 1930s, a new administration building containing a kitchen, a dining hall, washrooms, toilets, and offices was built at the plant’s main gate at Stroofstrasse 27. Beds were also set up on the attic floor of this building. In September 1942, the German Labor Front (DAF) reported that altogether 560 forced laborers were employed in the factory at that time. By April 1943, the number had grown to 663.

Over the course of the year 1942, a large barracks camp was built in the eastern section of the factory grounds at Softgrasses 31. In 1943, all “Eastern workers” previously lodged in other buildings had to move into a new, fence-enclosed “Easterners’ camp” within the barracks camp.

A review of the surviving card file reveals that many forced laborers, particularly those from France, left the Griesheim plant for various reasons: arrest by the Gestapo, failure to return from home leave, or because they joined the Armed SS.

*Author: Sascha Mahl*
