# Memorial Site Großmarkthalle

*Philipp-Holzmann-Weg*

![Photo of the memorial at the Frankfurt wholesale market hall](../../images/d440d389c1a093da0375a32f84361d17_original.jpg)
*n.d. — Author: Norbert Miguletz | Description: Photo of the memorial at the Frankfurt wholesale market hall*

The wholesale market hall was designed by architect Martin Elsaesser in the mid-1920s. It was built to the highest standards, designed for modern trade and the smooth handling of fruit and vegetables: with practical loading ramps, generously dimensioned refrigerated cellars, rail connections with covered tracks and its own signal box.
The Secret State Police (Gestapo) and NSDAP Gauleitung used the building and its functionality for mass deportations of Jews from 1941 onwards. This location was chosen because it was close to the city center and conveniently located between the port railroad and Ostbahnhof. The Gestapo therefore rented the eastern cellar area as a collection point for the deportations. The cellar offered protection from prying eyes and provided makeshift space for hundreds of people. Women, men and children were humiliated and physically abused by the personnel deployed. Robbed of their last possessions, they were finally forced onto the track in front of the hall. The trains of the German Reichsbahn were waiting there for the transports to the ghettos and concentration camps. Right next door, the daily market operations continued. The cruel events did not go unnoticed by those working there.

Not only Gestapo officers and party functionaries were involved in the Grossmarkthalle. "Normal" police officers, finance and railroad officials as well as employees of various municipal offices also took part in the deportations. The city administration and many non-Jewish citizens profited from the property left behind by the deportees. Others moved into apartments and houses from which Jews had been expelled.
According to the design by the KatzKaiser architectural firm, quotes from anti-Semitic persecutees and observers of the deportations depict the events at the memorial site. Embedded in the floor and on wall surfaces, the quotes follow a chronological dramaturgy in their arrangement. Indirect events and emotions are also addressed: the suicides in connection with the deportations, the reactions of the city's population or reflections on the crimes in Frankfurt.

*Author: Jüdisches Museum Frankfurt*
