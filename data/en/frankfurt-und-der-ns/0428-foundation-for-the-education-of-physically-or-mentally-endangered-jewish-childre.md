# Foundation for the education of physically or mentally endangered Jewish children

*Bleichstraße 8*

![Picture postcard with the Stockmann boarding house](../../images/C80382_jpg_26b3b34249d09a66787d4f4f0bea1f7f_original.jpg)
*Unknown — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF, Sammlung Church, C80382 | Description: Picture postcard with the Stockmann boarding house*

From at least 1917, this was the location of a day-care centre for Jewish boys and girls. After the First World War, this was taken over and expanded by a Jewish day-care centre run by the “Stiftung zur Erziehung körperlich oder geistig gefährdeter israelitischer Kinder” (“Foundation to educate Jewish children physically or mentally at risk”). Set up in 1881, the Foundation aimed to help neglected, ill and needy children. Its two-storey building in the back yard comprised teaching and meeting rooms as well as a small gymnasium. The building, which also operated a school, could take 70 children. In February 1939, the Foundation was forced to sell the building at far below the market price to the non-Jewish company E.W. Lechhorn, Fabr. Messwerkzeuge GmbH. The purchase price was paid into an account blocked by the Devisenstelle (Foreign Exchange Control Office), so the Foundation was unable to access it. In 1961, the Jewish Restitution Successor Organization (JRSO) submitted a claim for compensation. The JRSO, designated the official custodian of heirless Jewish property, was active in the American zone from 1948. According to the JRSO, during the November Pogroms in 1938, the caretaker at the time blew up the heating system and destroyed the Foundation's entire furnishings and fittings. However, when various neighbours as well as the building’s new owner testified there had been no such damage. the compensation authorities rejected the JRSO’s claim.
