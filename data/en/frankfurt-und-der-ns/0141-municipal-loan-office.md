# municipal loan office

*An der Hauptwache 127*

![Municipal lending institution](../../images/7ca39895a3f35f502c99a84b93c60391_original.jpg)
*Before 1941 — Author: Otto Emmel | License: HMF, Ph21687 | Description: Municipal lending institution*

From December 1938, those persecuted as Jews were forbidden to buy, pawn or sell independently any art objects or items of gold, platinum, or silver, jewellery, precious stones or pearls worth over 1000 Reichsmark. They could now only conduct such sales legally at the municipal pawnshop (“Städtische Darlehensanstalt”). A few months later, between late February and the end of March 1939, German Jews were compelled to hand in all their items of precious metals, precious stones and pearls at the municipal pawnshops. The only exceptions were personal items such as dentures or wedding rings, a wristwatch or pocket watch, and per person one knife, fork, table and coffee spoon. A few smaller silver items could also be kept providing they did not weigh more than 40 grams each and not more than 200 grams in total. The silver handed in to the municipal pawnshops was bought at a price ten per cent under the world market price. These prices were solely based on the material value and disregarded the art historical worth of the objects, which also included mementoes centuries old and with a religious significance. A total of 12,000 items of silver were forcibly surrendered to the Frankfurt municipal pawnshop. Initially, this silver, weighing over ten tons, was to be melted down. The director of the Stadtgeschichtliches Museum approached Mayor Friedrich Krebs to secure pieces interesting in terms of cultural history and craft work for the city’s museums. Krebs agreed to help and provided the Stadtgeschichtliches Museum and the Museum für Kunsthandwerk with a purchase fund of 50,000 Reichsmarks. Since the municipal pawnshop sold antique silver by weight, the museums profited significantly from the forced surrender of these silver items.
