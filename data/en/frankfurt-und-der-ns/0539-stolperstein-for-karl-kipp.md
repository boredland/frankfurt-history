# Stolperstein for Karl Kipp

*Berliner Straße 32*

Karl Kipp (1902–1942)

Karl Kipp was born in Schwefe near Soest in the state of Westphalia, the youngest of nine children. After attending school, he completed a commercial apprenticeship and then worked as a sales assistant in Soest, Dortmund, and Frankfurt am Main. He lost his job in 1932.

His last address in Frankfurt, registered in 1939, was Landsberggasse 4, a street near the present-day “Little Market Hall.” He presumably lived there as a subtenant. Between 1932 and 1936, he was arrested several times for homosexuality. In addition to charges under Section 175 of the German Criminal Code, he was also convicted for sexual offensiveness and in one case blackmail. In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and was severely tightened in 1935. In 1937, Kipp was committed to the Sachsenhausen, later to the Buchenwald and Flossenbürg concentration camps, where he was categorized as a “professional criminal.” He was released from “preventive custody” and placed under police supervision in Frankfurt. He worked for the Iso-Ge Isoliergesellschaft on Oederweg as an insulation assistant.

On December 8, 1939, Kipp was once again arrested. Now he was charged with attempted sexual harassment. He had also been informed on for consensual contact to men. In 1940, the regional court in Frankfurt sentenced him to two years in prison under Section 175 of the German Criminal Code. Even before the sentence had been served, the criminal investigation department ordered his renewed “preventive detention.” In December 1941, following his imprisonment in the Diez prison, he was sent back to the Flossenbürg concentration camp, this time as a “Section 175 preventive custody inmate” with the inmate number 2810.

Karl Kipp died in Flossenbürg on April 25, 1942 at the age of 39—officially of heart and circulatory failure. His dentures were removed, his corpse incinerated, and the ash scattered in the camp. His belongings were turned over to the Frankfurt criminal investigation department.

 The Stolperstein was initiated by Martin Dill and Heidi Stögbauer of the Frankfurt Stolperstein Initiative, funded by Stefan Diefenbach, and laid in 2019.

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
