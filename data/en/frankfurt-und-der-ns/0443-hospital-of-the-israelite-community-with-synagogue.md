# Hospital of the Israelite community with synagogue

*Gagernstraße 36*

![Picture postcard with aerial view of the hospital of the Israelite community](../../images/C80396_jpg_deac6e7cf666bccd006f7d4f2b643044_original.jpg)
*1931 — Author: unbekannt | License: HMF / Sammlung Church C80396 | Description: Picture postcard with aerial view of the hospital of the Israelite community*

The hospital of the Jewish community was opened here in 1914. It replaced an older building near the zoo. The architect was Franz Roeckle, later an employee under municipal architect Ernst May. The hospital had 200 beds and modern, functional, hygienic furnishings. From 1933 onwards, ever more Jewish patients came here for treatment because, like the Jewish doctors, they were subjected to discrimination in the municipal hospitals. During the November Pogroms of 1938 the Gestapo took money from the hospital and the doctors; it was later returned to them in part. For fear of arrest, the hospital director Dr. Bernhard Rosenthal poisoned himself on 10 November 1938. In April 1939 the Jewish community was forced to sell the hospital to the city of Frankfurt. However, the facility was permitted to stay in operation for a further three years. All other Jewish care facilities had to close by July 1941. Now the hospital also cared for the elderly and persons approaching death. The building moreover housed a prayer room and a small synagogue. Owing to the high demand for beds, the prayer room had to be converted into a dormitory. In September 1941 more than 240 beds were occupied; there were 120 patients and 128 elderly and dying persons. The hospital also set up a soup kitchen for the needy. It was understaffed, however, because many Jewish nurses had to perform forced labour and only few non-Jewish nurses worked there. The facility was closed in the autumn of 1942; the patients and staff were deported. Now the Hospital zum Heiligen Geist used the building.
 
 The hospital complex was partially destroyed by bombing during the war, and after 1945 gradually reconstructed. In the post-war period, survivors from the Terezín concentration camp were cared for here. Now the Jewish community operates a modern centre for the elderly with a synagogue at this site.
