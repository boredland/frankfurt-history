# Gestapo police department

*Lindenstraße 19 und 27*

![Photo of the Secret State Police Office Frankfurt a. M.](../../images/54c8ca1bc0359a68200f60946aacb4dc_original.jpg)
*1947 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-13183 | Description: Photo of the Secret State Police Office Frankfurt a. M.*

The Frankfurt branch of the Secret State Police (Gestapo) had its headquarters here from 1 April 1941 onwards. The building had previously belonged to the Cronstett- und Hynspergische evangelische Stiftung, a ladies’ religious foundation that had been forced to sell it to the Gestapo. In 1942, the headquarters had a staff of 180 employees. In the cellar were three cells for detaining prisoners before and after interrogation. The Gestapo tortured them to get confessions out of them. In late September or early October 1941, some 18 members of the so-called Harlem-Club, which was part of the swing scene, were arrested and interrogated by the Gestapo here. Although most of the young people belonging to the jazz and swing scene were apolitical, they were persecuted by the Gestapo. The members of the “Harlem-Club” were accused of listening to radio stations of enemy countries of the German Reich. That had been prohibited since the beginning of the war in 1939. The ban on broadcasting jazz on the radio in Germany had already been in effect since October 1935.
 The Gestapo released the majority of the “Harlem-Club” members. One of them, however—Franz Kremer—, remained in custody. The 16-year-old was interrogated and tortured daily over a span of several weeks. In later interviews, Kremer recalled that he had often considered confessing to the Gestapo charges despite his innocence to avoid further violence. One typical Gestapo method was to impute young members of the swing scene with petty offences. Many of them were accused of trading in food or other goods subject to rationing during the war.
 The Gestapo are associated with numerous crimes against political opponents, Jews, forced labourers, and concentration camp inmates. However, it is difficult to prove the crimes committed by the Frankfurt Gestapo. Shortly before the end of the war, Gestapo officials burned incriminatory records in the garden. Only the Gestapo card index survived largely intact; it is now in the holdings of the Hessisches Hauptstaatsarchiv in Wiesbaden.
