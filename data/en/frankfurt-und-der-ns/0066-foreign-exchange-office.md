# Foreign Exchange Office

*Bethmannstraße 19*

![Black-and-white drawing of the exterior façade of the Foreign Exchange Office on Bethmannstraße](../../images/f4a9de115b4de8045a20586eef0b452e_original.jpg)
*1919 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 2014-60 | Description: Black-and-white drawing of the exterior façade of the Foreign Exchange Office on Bethmannstraße*

During the Nazi years, the Foreign Exchange Offices (“Devisenstellen”) played a crucial role in helping to register, check and expropriate those persecuted by the Nazi regime. Under measures introduced from 1933, those persecuted as Jews were systematically excluded from society and deprived of their livelihoods. Many of them sought to leave the country. The largest waves of emigration were in 1933, 1935 and 1938. But anyone trying to emigrate faced growing bureaucratic victimisation. By as early as September 1934, emigrants were not allowed to take more than ten Reichsmark in cash with them. As a result, they arrived abroad totally destitute. Their money on accounts in the German Reich could only be exchanged into foreign currency by the Deutsche Golddiskontbank, a process resulting in substantial losses. In 1938, the laws were tightened even further. In April, a new ordinance required those persecuted as Jews to register their entire assets. From May that same year, the Foreign Exchange Offices could impose a “security order” (“Sicherungsanordnung”), freezing cash and share and security accounts. Families were then only allowed to withdraw a small monthly amount from their account, and needed to submit a new application to their Foreign Exchange Office for every single withdrawal. With the shock of the November Pogroms in 1938, many began to prepare to emigrate. To prevent assets and art works leaving the country with their Jewish owners, the Nazi regime imposed punitive taxes such as the “Reich flight tax” (“Reichsfluchtsteuer”). Art works could be classified as “cultural property of national significance” and simply confiscated prior to emigration. Anyone trying to emigrate had to submit a comprehensive list of their removal goods and baggage to the Foreign Exchange Office. Parents also had to submit such lists for their children when they travelled on their own to England on the Kindertransports.
