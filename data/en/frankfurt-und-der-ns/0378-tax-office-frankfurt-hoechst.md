# Tax Office Frankfurt-Höchst

*Hospitalstraße 16a*

![Placeholder image](../../images/Hier%20fehlt_dfdc6d631f271e842082099187aa5623_original.jpg)
*License: HMF*

The Frankfurt-Höchst tax office had been located in a new building at Hospitalstraße 16a since 1935. Like all tax offices, it was involved in the tax discrimination of Jews.

From 1934, they denied charitable status and thus tax advantages to foundations that did not serve the "good of the German national community". After the Nuremberg Race Laws were passed in 1935, all social foundations that did not "exclusively support German national comrades" were subject to full corporation and property taxation.
From 1936, Jews no longer received a child allowance, and from 1938 the child allowance was abolished for them. Expenses for Jews could no longer be deducted. From 1939, Jews were generally classified in the least favorable tax bracket, whose assessment rate was also raised by 12.5 percent. The recognition of pension expenses such as building society savings contracts, health and accident insurance as special expenses was also abolished for Jews. From 1941, Jews as well as Poles had to pay a social equalization levy, which deducted 15 percent of their income in addition to income tax.

Emigrants had to pay a Reich flight tax, which amounted to 25 percent of all taxable assets. The tax authorities were able to order security payments in anticipation of a possible emigration and thus "secure" a quarter of the assets long before emigration.
Jews had to submit a comprehensive declaration of assets in 1938. After the November pogrom, Jews with assets of more than 5,000 Reichsmark had to pay a Jewish property levy of 25 percent.
As the domestic assets of emigrants were confiscated in favour of the Reich after expatriation, the tax offices had to provide official assistance with their documents. After the deportations, the tax offices administered and disposed of the assets left behind by the deportees.
