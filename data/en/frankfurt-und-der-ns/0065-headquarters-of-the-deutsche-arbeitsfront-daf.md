# Headquarters of the Deutsche Arbeitsfront (DAF)

*Wilhelm-Leuschner-Straße 69-77*

![Photo of the headquarters of the Deutsche Arbeitsfront (DAF)](../../images/2dbc6ae8b31383adee828cd1574dcf83_original.jpg)
*1931 — Author: Wolff & Tritschler | License: HMF C35145 | Description: Photo of the headquarters of the Deutsche Arbeitsfront (DAF)*

The trade union hall at Bürgerstrasse 69–77 (now Wilhelm-Leuschner-Strasse 69) was built in 1930/31. The architectural firm Taut & Hoffmann of Berlin had designed it in the Early Modernist style. On 2 May 1933, the SA (Storm Troopers) occupied the building and confiscated the union funds. The union secretary and the employee representatives were arrested and taken to an early concentration camp in a building in Bockenheim commonly known as the “Pearl Factory”. On 10 May 1933, the German Labour Front seized the union assets and hall, which it renamed the “House of Labour”. It now set up its regional Hesse-Nassau headquarters here. During World War II, its “Labour Deployment” department kept track of which companies made use of forced labourers and organized the supervision of the forced labour camps in Frankfurt. Surviving records provide evidence of some 600 addresses where forced labourers were lodged or employed in the city.

 In 1946 the American military administration turned the building over to the Hesse branch of the Free Federation of German Trade Unions. The street was named after Wilhelm Leuschner, a trade union leader and member of the resistance.
