# Traub's synagogue and prayer room

*Börneplatz*

![View of the group of houses at Dominikanerplatz 4-12](../../images/616257f9c5ed55791777a8f3dc336265_original.jpg)
*1939 — Author: Claus Mehs | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S8-3 Nr. 28 | Description: View of the group of houses at Dominikanerplatz 4-12*

The name of this square changed several times over the course of the centuries. From the sixteenth century onwards it was called the Jews’ Market. In 1886 it was renamed Börneplatz (Börne Square) in honour of the Jewish writer Ludwig Börne. In 1935 the National Socialists renamed it Dominikanerplatz (Dominican Square). From 1711 to around 1829, the hospitals of the Jewish men’s and women’s health funds were located in the half-timber houses with the numbers 4, 6, 8, 10, and 12. After the hospitals moved to new quarters at Rechneigrabenstrasse 18–20, the buildings’ interiors were converted into private flats. The first floor of the three-storey building number 6 housed the Traub’sche Synagogue, which had seating capacity for 60 men and 40 women. “Betstuben”, or prayer rooms, were small, simply furnished synagogues. Another such Betstube with seating for 100 men and 60 women was reportedly located at house number 10. It is unclear, however, whether it was already abandoned in the mid-1930s. In 1938 there were still 40 prayer rooms in Frankfurt where the faithful could go to say their prayers near their homes. Owing to conflicting reports on the matter, it has not yet been possible to determine for certain whether anti-Jewish attacks took place at this site during the 1938 November Pogroms. By the end of the war, the row of houses had been destroyed. Five street signs today remind us of the eventful history of the square, which was renamed Börneplatz in 1978.
