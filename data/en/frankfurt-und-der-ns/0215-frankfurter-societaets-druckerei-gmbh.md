# Frankfurter Societäts-Druckerei GmbH

*Große Eschenheimer Straße 31-37*

![Photo of the Frankfurt Societäts-Druckerei GmbH](../../images/296bb0c9964148c5dd36b8ef77428679_original.jpg)
*ca. 1940 — Author: Christian | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-8334 | Description: Photo of the Frankfurt Societäts-Druckerei GmbH*

The Frankfurter Societäts-Druckerei printing company was based at this site. It had been founded by Leopold Sonnemann in 1860. His grandsons Kurt und Heinrich Simon enthusiastically carried on their grandfather's lifework. It was under their oversight that the richly traditional printing enterprise and its flagship publication, the "Frankfurter Zeitung" newspaper, came to international fame. Heinrich Simon directed the newspaper's editorial conference; Kurt Simon, a doctor of law, managed the comemrcial side of the business. In the summer of 1929, the latter resigned from the management and joined the company's supervisory board. In addition to being a wealthy publisher and businessman, he was also known as an art connoisseur and coin collector. After Adolf Hiler's accession to power on 30 January 1933, Kurt Simon's life changed dramatically. From 1933 onwards, he was persecuted as a Jew and a so-called non-Aryan although he was a Protestant. In the spring of 1933 he received a summons from the Reich Chancellery's "Department for the Aryanization of Business Enterprises" in Berlin. The officials demanded that he give up his interests in the "Frankfurter Zeitung". He was told that, if he refused, the newspaper would be discontinued and all employees dismissed. Even before the year 1933 was out, immense political pressure ultimately forced the Simon family to give up their shares in the "Frankfurter Zeitung" and the Frankfurter Societäts-Druckerei. Kurt Simon lost all his posts as well as his livelihood as a result. His professional and social world had been destroyed. His colleagues on the supervisory board, shocked by his fate, promised him their full loyalty. In 1943 the Nazis had the "Frankfurt Zeitung" shut down. 
 
 Kurt Simon lived in New York but did not manage to gain a foothold in the U.S., either personally or financially. No sooner had the war ended than he contacted his old associates. He invested every ounce of strength in getting the "Frankfurter Zeitung" back on the newspaper stands. There was no doubt in his mind that Europe needed a liberal-democratic voice again. He also made every effort to obtain compensation. His auditor estimated that, between 1934 and 1948, through the loss of his posts, he had suffered damages in the amounts of 754,000 Reichsmarks. In August 1957 he was awarded 40,000 DM — the pre-determined maximum amount for "financial and professional damages due to persecution". The sum was far below the income he would have earned had he not been forced to resign from his posts and emigrate. Just weeks after receiving the first instalment, Kurt Simon died in New York after a long illness.
