# Forced camp Friedberger Landstraße

*Friedberger Landstraße (Heiligenstock)*

![Cover of the Frankfurt municipal authority file on the “concentration camp on Friedberger Landstraße”](../../images/8a70ee5860723f6a337412293a2b1cde_original.jpg)
*1929-1930 — License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte Frankfurt | Description: Cover of the Frankfurt municipal authority file on the “concentration camp on Friedberger Landstraße”*

From 1929 to 1935, there was a so-called “concentration camp” for Sinti and Roma on Friedberger Landstrasse behind the present day Heiligenstock Park Cemetery on Frankfurt’s border to Bad Vilbel. It was not a concentration camp in the later National Socialist sense but is referred to by this term in municipal administration records of 1929. More specifically, the camp was a site under municipal authority in which all Sinti and Roma living in mobile homes in Frankfurt were to be “concentrated.”

The camp’s establishment went back to a resolution by the Frankfurt city council assembly of December 1928. The city wanted to remove Sinti and Roma who lived in mobile homes or used them for mobile trade from the townscape. In exchange, it offered them an intentionally unattractive site on the outermost periphery. The Frankfurt social welfare office was in charge of implementing the plans for the internment camp. At the time, there was not yet any legal basis for compulsory internment. The city’s only means of applying pressure was to terminate leases or cut welfare benefits. The latter measure hardly had the desired effect, however, because almost none of the persons in question depended on such benefits.

The “concentration camp” on Friedberger Landstrasse was an initial step in the systematic persecution of Sinti and Roma and can therefore be seen as part of their systematic persecution under National Socialism.

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
