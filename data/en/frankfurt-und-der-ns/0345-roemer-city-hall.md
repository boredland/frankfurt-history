# Römer City Hall

*Römerberg 23*

Römer City Hall

Even before 1933, advocates of right-wing positions were on the city council. But after the Nazi Party became the strongest faction in the city council in 1933, democratic councillors found themselves under extreme pressure. Many Communist Party and Social Democratic Party members were arrested and taken to concentration camps set up by the SA and SS in Frankfurt. These early concentration camps aimed at destroying political opposition to the Nazis. Mayor Ludwig Landmann, who was Jewish, was forced to resign with immediate effect. Deputy Mayor Karl Schlosser (SPD) was first relieved of his duties and then arrested. Friedrich Krebs, a member of the Nazi Party, was then appointed mayor. At the end of 1933, Frankfurt’s city council assembly was dissolved. The actions of the local authorities were now determined by racism and antisemitism. The Nazi regime’s “Law for the Restoration of the Professional Civil Service” from 7 April 1933 provided for the blanket dismissal of all those defined as Jews or categorised as politically objectionable. But even before it came into force, Mayor Krebs had removed Jewish staff and civil servants from their positions in Frankfurt. A total of 880 city authority employees lost their jobs, a group not only including the Nazi Party’s political opponents but also Jehovah’s Witnesses. The city of Frankfurt looted the assets of Jewish foundations, confiscated libraries and art works, and expropriated Jewish families and the Jewish community. In this process, a large amount of real estate passed to the city authorities. They then also rented the empty buildings to the SA and SS which used them to set up torture sites as well as labour and concentration camps. In addition, during the November Pogroms in 1938, the city authorities provided the Gestapo with the Festhalle arena and the Großmarkthalle covered market as collection points for deportations. After Frankfurt was liberated on 29 March 1945, the US Military Government banned the Nazi Party and its organisations. It also removed Nazi Party members and supporters from responsible positions in the administration, economy and public life. Through a denazification process, the Allies sought to permanently prevent Nazism having any further influence on German society. Streets renamed by the Nazi administrations were given their former or new names, and Nazi symbols and memorials were removed. Efforts were also made to establish who had been a member of a Nazi Party organisation and how far they had been involved in the Nazi system and its crimes. To avoid this legal vetting process, a not insignificant number of Nazis went underground.
