# "wild" concentration camp

*Ginnheimer Landstraße 40-42*

![Photo of an early concentration camp in a former pearl factory](../../images/84ddc4f5b2eb1eb6c9160d8c966a677a_original.jpg)
*ca.1910 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 1998-20027 | Description: Photo of an early concentration camp in a former pearl factory*

From April 1933, the SA (Sturmabteilung), the paramilitary wing of the Nazi Party (NSDAP), took over a site known locally as the “Perlenfabrik”, named after the factory producing glass beads here until 1903. From 1923 to January 1933, the reconstructed site was home to a residential institution for young offenders. On 24 April 1933, the Frankfurt Magistrat, the municipal authorities, approved the application of the SA Hesse-Nassau South sub-group to sequester the house and grounds to train SA commanders. Shortly afterwards, the SA Standarten (regiments) 63 and 81 moved into the building and set up a concentration camp there, the first in Southern Hesse. From around June 1933, Adolf Heinz Beckerle, head of the SA sub-group, lived in the building. Such early concentration camps aimed at destroying political opposition to the Nazis. The inmates here were trade unionists, Social Democrats and Communists. They had to work outside every day on the land, under constant SA surveillance, and were brutally humiliated, interrogated and tortured – often for weeks at a time. In the hope of extracting confessions through terror and mortal fear, the SA also transported some inmates into the forest and forced them to dig their own graves. The “Perlenfabrik” concentration camp closed in summer 1933. 
 The SA continued to use the building until Frankfurt’s liberation by US forces on 29 March 1945.
