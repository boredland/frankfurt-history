# Frankfurt south train station

*Diesterwegplatz*

![South station](../../images/a9a92efabb3694a9bc680f28c1ab1a8e_original.jpg)
*ca. 1925 — Author: unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7A Nr. 2012-149 | Description: South station*

Busses left the festival hall over and over again, taking groups of Jewish men to the South Station at Washingtonplatz 51–53 (now Diesterwegplatz). Frankfurters primed for violence awaited them there with shouts of: “Beat the Jews to death; cut off their necks!” as one eyewitness reported. Extreme acts of violence already took place here at South Station. Altogether 534 Jewish men were transported from South Station to the Dachau concentration camp, and 2,621 to the Buchenwald concentration camp by way of Weimar. According to another eyewitness report in the holdings of the Wiener Library in London, they were met with violence on arriving in Weimar: “We had to fall into line in the underpass, faces to the wall, and were … abused with jabs and blows. After the transport had been counted, we were driven with hoarse screams … up the stairs in the greatest possible haste to busses standing there.”
 
 At the Buchenwald concentration camp, the conditions were disastrous—even compared to other camps—, causing 61 Frankfurt Jews to die. The youngest was just twenty years old. Most of the men were released but had to commit to leaving Germany immediately. What is more, many of them suffered afterwards from the physical as well as emotional consequences of their brutal imprisonment.
 
 In 1943, the Gestapo deported some 100 Sinti and Roma to the Auschwitz extermination and concentration camp from South Station.
