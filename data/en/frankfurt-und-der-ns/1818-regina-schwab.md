# Regina Schwab

*When lines are crossed!*

![](../../images/a2c7c38b9688813fda74f8986c1efd8c_original.jpg)

The life and agonising death of Regina Schwab: Despite her escape and the abandonment of her living environment, she became a victim of the Holocaust.

Regina Schwab (born Goldberger, 22.03.1878-21.05.1943) lived at Mousonstraße 10. Together with her husband Siegmund Schwab, the Nuremberg-born Jewish merchant ran the company ‘Thalmann und Schwab’, which specialised in the trade and distribution of beverages and foodstuffs. After the death of her husband, who was also Jewish, on 24th of August 1926, she became the sole owner of the business. Due to the increasing marginalisation and discrimination of Jews during the Nazi era, she was forced to dissolve the company and emigrate in 1939. However, the business had already been severely damaged by anti-Semitic riots some time earlier.

Regina Schwab herself moved to the Netherlands, while other family members sought refuge mainly in the USA (e.g. New York, Chicago). In the course of her emigration, she had to document her entire household. The foreign exchange offices, which accused her of fraud, confiscated large parts of her assets and belongings. She had to build a new life without money. A banker named Breslau paid the ‘Reichsfluchtsteuer’ of RM 200,000-300,000, which was used to exploit fugitives once again. Many of her documents were destroyed by her lawyer in order to prevent further investigations by the foreign exchange offices and the Gestapo.

On the 20th of January 1939, Regina Schwab moved in temporarily with her sister-in-law Bella Schwab, who lived in Dinxperlo (Netherlands). In the course of the Nazi occupation of the Netherlands, Regina Schwab followed the order to register as Jewish. As a result, she was deported to the Dutch concentration camp Westerbork on 9th of April 1943. 
On 18th of May 1943, she was deported to Sobibor in Poland, where she was murdered three days later.

Her last residence was Stationsstraat 408, also in Dinxperlo. Today there is a ‘Stolperstein’ to remember her. Her heirs applied for compensation. These were partially approved. Both the Schwab family and the Goldberger family lost several family members as a result of the Holocaust.

*Author: Benedikt Bockholt, Emilia Dewitz,  Linus Hornberger,  Ashley Kaminski*
