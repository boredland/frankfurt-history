# Stolen place

*Mörikestraße 3*

This house belonged to the lawyer Dr. Robert Maximilian Cahn and his wife Mathilde (née Rpthbarth). The couple had two children: a son, Franz Robert, born in 1920, and a daughter, Mathilde Katharina, born in 1925. In 1927, the couple had a spacious two-storey house built for their growing family at Mörikestrasse 3 in Dornbusch. It had a music room with a piano, phonograph, and three celli, a study with a library, a winter garden, and rooms for the children. As a holder of the Iron Cross II. Class, Dr. Cahn was spared professional discrimination even after the anti-Jewish "Law on Admission to the Legal Profesion" went into effect in April 1933. Initially he was also permitted to keep his authorization to practise law at the regional appeal court in Frankfurt. In fact, in March he was awarded the Honour Cross for Front-Line Veterans for his service in World War I. 
 
 When Robert Cahn was dismissed from his post as an authorized signatory for the Frankfurter Privat Telefon-Gesellschaft (PRITEG) on 8 September 1938, he had worked there for twenty-five years. He was disbarred not long afterwards. Owing to the "Decree on the Use of Jewish Assets" of 3 December 1938, the couple was forced to sell their house, cancel their life insurance, and liquidate other investments. 
 They moved in with Mathilde's parents. Their money was in a blocked account at a licenced foreign exchange bank overseen by the Frankfurt foreign exchange control office. The Cahns were accordingly unable to dispose freely to their assets. Shortly before the beginning of World War II, they managed to send their fourteen-year-old daughter to England on a Kindertransport organized by the Jewish Social Welfare organization. Her older brother Robert had already been living there since 1935. Robert and Mathilde Cahn were subjected to increasing pressure from the foreign exchange control office: now they were permitted to withdraw only 300 Reichsmark per month — too little to live on — from their account. On 22 November 1941 they were both deported. The transport was destined for Riga. Because the ghetto there was overcrowded, however, the train was rerouted to Kaunas in Lithuania. Accordning to reports by the Gestapo and the commander of the security police and security service in Lithuania, the deportees were shot to death or buried alive by slodiers of Task Force 3 on 25 November 1941, one day after their arrival at Fort IX. The Cahn children survived the Holocaust in England. Not a single memento of their parental home remained to them.
