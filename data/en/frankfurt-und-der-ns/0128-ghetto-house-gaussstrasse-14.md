# Ghetto House Gaußstraße 14

*Gaußstraße 14*

![Photo of a ghetto house in Gaußstraße 14](../../images/1404b1e2361b27c6fd4da79dfa9a3bca_original.jpg)
*2010 — Author: Martin Starl | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7C Nr. 2010-158 | Description: Photo of a ghetto house in Gaußstraße 14*

The multiple family dwelling in Gaußstrasse 14 was built around 1911 and from 1920 onwards belonged to Julius Wertheimer, a Jewish businessman from Amsterdam. There was a six-room flat on each floor. 
 
 The Mannheimer, Neuhaus, and Wald families were among those who lived here. They were likewise businesspeople and members of Frankfurt’s middle class. The anti-Jewish campaigns and laws imposed by the National Socialists had severe consequences for their commercial pursuits and their personal lives. After the Nazis called for the boycott of Jewish businesses on 1 April 1933, they suffered substantial losses in sales. The wholesaler Salomon Wald was forced to move his office to the little attic flat on the fourth floor and store his lining fabrics there as well. After the enactment of the Nuremberg Laws on 15 September 1935, the ostracism of Jews increased significantly. The Wald family was torn apart in 1938. In July of that year, Salomon Wald and his son were sentenced to prison for allegedly “listening to enemy radio broadcasts”. Salomon’s wife Rosa, a Polish Jew, was deported in October.
 The brothers Justin and Siegbert Neuhaus gave up their flat in 1936 to save money for their emigration to North America. Justin Neuhaus lodged with his friends the Mannheimers on the first floor. He managed to flee to Amsterdam with his family in 1939. Once the war began, it was almost impossible to leave the country.
 
 New tenants, the May family, moved into the building in 1936. They had left Schlüchtern to protect themselves from Nazi hostilities and abuse. Many Jews came to Frankfurt from the surrounding region for this reason.
 
 After the November Pogroms of 1938, nearly all of the families at Gaußstrasse 14 took in lodgers to supplement their income. Tax increases, compulsory charges, and other oppressive requirements placed a heavy burden on the families. The residents were not allowed to leave the building between 8.00 pm and 6.00 am, were permitted to shop only in certain groceries, and had to turn in their radios.
 
 From 1938 onwards, Jewish men could be called up for forced labour. Erich Mannheimer was initially put to work in a gardening business, then in a clay- and brickworks. In 1941 the persecution measures increased yet again. Now Jews were required to wear a star in public to identify themselves.
 
 The families at Gaußstrasse 14 had no choice but to move closer together. More and more of Frankfurt’s Jewish citizens had to leave their own flats and move into ghetto buildings. Gaußstrasse 14 was one of 300 ghetto buildings in Frankfurt! Jewish victims of persecution had to live at extremely close quarters in these buildings. Heating was also a problem, because starting in 1941 they were permitted to purchase coal only after the “Aryans’” demand had been met. The deportations began in October 1941. The residents gradually disappeared—until December 1943, when the last of them were deported.
