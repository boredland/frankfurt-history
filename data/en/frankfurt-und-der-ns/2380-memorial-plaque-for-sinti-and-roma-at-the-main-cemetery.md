# Memorial plaque for Sinti and Roma at the main cemetery

*Eckenheimer Landstraße 194*

![Memorial plaque for Sinti and Roma at the main cemetery](../../images/0eae53f17ab1cefd692473dac2e22f96_original.jpg)
*2013 — Author: Daniel Ebert | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte Frankfurt | Description: Memorial plaque for Sinti and Roma at the main cemetery*

In the 1970s and ’80s, protests by the Sinti and Roma civil rights movement heightened public awareness of their concerns in the Federal Republic of Germany. Again and again, the protestors called attention to the persecution of Sinti and Roma under National Socialism and the group’s continued persecution in the post-war period. On the local and national level alike, they fought for the commensurate public remembrance of the genocide of around 500,000 Sinti and Roma.

In Frankfurt, the dedication of the first plaque commemorating the Sinti and Roma under National Socialism at the city’s main cemetery represented a significant milestone in the struggle for recognition of the injustices suffered. Initiated by the Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen (Hesse State Association of German Sinti and Roma), the dedication was carried out in two steps. Within the framework of a memorial service carried out on August 2, 1991 (European Holocaust Memorial Day for Sinti and Roma), the plaque was unveiled in Frankfurt’s Saint Paul’s Church, a space used for exhibitions and special local events. Hans Eichel, a Social Democrat and minister president of Hesse at the time, gave an address. Two years later, on March 3, 1993, the dedication of the plaque at the city’s main cemetery took place. The inscription reads: “In commemoration of the 500,000 Sinti and Roma who became victims of genocide under National Socialism.”

It was stonecutters of the “Sinti Workshop” in Albersweiler, Rhineland-Palatinate—a project of that state’s Sinti and Roma association—who produced the sandstone plaque. Today it is located at the entrance to the section of the main cemetery dedicated to commemorating “Victims of the Violence of 1933–1945.” Opened on November 15, 1959, this section comprises some 1,500 gravesites of victims of concentration camps, prisons, and “euthanasia” crimes as well as prisoners of war and forced laborers. Since its inauguration, commemorative plaques for various victim groups have been installed there.

*Author: Verband Deutscher Sinti und Roma, Landesverband Hessen*
