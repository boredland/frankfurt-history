# Adlerwerke

*Kleyerstraße 17*

![The main building of Adlerwerke on Kleyerstraße.](../../images/a0a6bc8be95bd2d1ac588e0274191b95_original.jpg)
*Author: Wolff & Tritschler | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte, Frankfurt Best. S7Wo Nr. 2531 | Description: The main building of Adlerwerke on Kleyerstraße.*

Monument to Industrial Culture and Place of Commemoration

Before 1945, the Frankfurt “Adlerwerke” was one of the largest manufacturers of motorized vehicles in the German Reich. It was already producing war vehicles and precision parts for weapons and ammunition during World War I. In the Nazi period, the company purchased four parcels of land from Jewish owners far below market value in order to expand production. Persons subjected to persecution for being Jewish had no choice but to sell their property; many were also robbed. The National Socialists referred to this appropriation, which was nearly always unlawful, as “Aryanization.” The military build-up for the war of conquest meant an increasing demand for armaments production. As the German laborers were in active duty at the front, the Adlerwerke made increasing use of forced laborers and prisoners of war from 1942 onward. In August 1944, a subcamp of the Natzweiler concentration camp in Alsace—the main camp in the region—was set up in the Adlerwerke factory building and given the code name “Katzbach.”

After World War II, the same managers remained in charge of the Adlerwerke, whose chief product was now office machines. The company was later sold to the Olivetti group. The factory was closed in 1994 and converted to serve as an office building. The works council had publicized the history of the “Katzbach” concentration camp in 1990. These activities, but also the need to represent the interests of the workforce during the process of the factory’s liquidation, led to the formation of the association “Living and Working in Gallus and Griesheim.” Along with other interested parties in Frankfurt, the association campaigned for many years for the payment of compensation to the former forced laborers. The Adlerwerke Historical Site opened in 2022, the hard-earned result of dedicated efforts by members of the civil society.
