# Forced labor camp for Sinti and Roma

*Kruppstraße 100-102*

![Placeholder image](../../images/Hier%20fehlt_dfdc6d631f271e842082099187aa5623_original.jpg)
*License: HMF*

Kruppstraße 14 (today: Kruppstraße 100-102) had been a site for caravans of Sinti and Roma since the early 1930s. In spring 1942, after the evacuation of the forced camp for Sinti and Roma at Dieselstraße 40, a new forced camp was set up here. The site was surrounded by a barbed wire fence. Police officers guarded the facility, which was brutally run by police chief Johannes Himmelheber.
Some of the camp's inmates, including children, had to perform forced labor. At that time, there were 179 women, children and men living in Dieselstraße, who were then taken to Kruppstraße. In March 1943, the Gestapo deported hundreds of people via Ostbahnhof to the Auschwitz concentration and extermination camp. The majority of the deportees were murdered there, as were numerous families.

Friedrich L. was also housed in Kruppstraße. He was sent to the camp in 1942, after he had already been forced to work for several years following his arrest in 1938 at the age of five. Although he was still a child, he had to work in building construction and civil engineering and endured severe abuse on several occasions. Most of his family were murdered in Auschwitz-Birkenau.

When US troops liberated the camp in March 1945, some people were still imprisoned here. For survivors of the concentration camps, it served as a point of contact to find relatives, and today the Hessen-Center is located nearby.

The head of the Himmelheber camp stood trial in 1948 and was acquitted on appeal. The victims did not receive any compensation under the Federal Compensation Act until 1987.
