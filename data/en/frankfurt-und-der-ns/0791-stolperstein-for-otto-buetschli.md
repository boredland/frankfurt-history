# Stolperstein for Otto Bütschli

*Körnerstraße 16*

Otto Bütschli (1907–1941)

Otto Ernst Bütschli was born in Frankfurt. He had three sisters and lived with his family at Cordierstrasse 14 in Frankfurt’s Gallus district. In 1939, he moved into a room at Körnerstrasse 16 in the Westend district and lived there as a subtenant. Although he had been confirmed a Protestant, he referred to himself as faithless. He attended the Musterschule in Frankfurt until eleventh grade, also a school in Bad Brückenau. In 1924, he completed a commercial apprenticeship at the Dr. M. Albersheim cosmetics company in Frankfurt. After that he was unemployed for a time—like many during the economic crisis. Later he took on various odd jobs, carried out practical training in the Ziegler bookshop from 1933 to 1935, and in 1936 took a position as an office worker for the Rhein-Main-Neckar Farmers’ Cooperative.

In July 1939, Otto Bütschli was arrested in the framework of a major police operation against homosexual men in Frankfurt. He was initially placed in pre-trial detention and lost his job. Although he had no previous convictions, the Frankfurt regional court sentenced him to extremely harsh punishment—a 20-month prison term—for consensual sexual relations with adult men under Sections 175 and 175a of the German Criminal Code. In force from 1871 to 1994, Section 175 criminalized sexual acts between men and had been severely tightened in 1935.

Bütschli initially served his sentence in the Preungesheim prison in Frankfurt. In November 1939, he was transferred to the Rodgau prison camp near Dieburg, where many Section 175 convicts were assigned to forced labor. There he severely injured his right hand, and in August 1940 he was returned to Preungesheim. The plea for clemency submitted by himself and his mother was rejected. He had to serve his sentence in full—until March 11, 1941. Even then, however, he was not released. According to a directive in force since 1940, men who had been sentenced for homosexual contacts to several partners were not released after their prison terms but put in “preventive custody.” The Frankfurt criminal investigation department also ordered “preventive custody” for Bütschli—without his having had the right to know about it beforehand. On March 11, 1941, he was taken directly from the prison to the Sachsenhausen concentration camp. Eight months later, on November 3, 1941, Otto Bütschli died in the Gross-Rosen concentration camp at the age of 34. “Gastritis with circulatory insufficiency” was given as the official cause of death.

The Stolperstein was initiated by Martin Dill and Heidi Stögbauer of the Frankfurt Stolperstein Initiative, funded by Ralf Puchas, and laid in 2019.

 

Stolpersteine

A Stolperstein—literally “stumbling stone”—is a 10 x 10 x 10-cm concrete block with a brass plate mounted on the upper side. The brass plate is inscribed with the name of the person and brief information about their biography and persecution during the Nazi period. Learn more about the Initiative Stolpersteine Frankfurt: https://stolpersteine-frankfurt.de/en
