# Institute for Social Research (destroyed)

*Senckenberganlage 31*

![Street view of the institute building with entrance area emphasised by a portico with two columns.](../../images/30dcbc948c1ea2f374e173281ccb72b3_original.jpg)
*around 1925 — Author: Unbekannt | License: Institut für Stadtgeschichte Frankfurt am Main | Description: Street view of the institute building with entrance area emphasised by a portico with two columns.*

Founded in 1923, the Institute for Social Research was initially housed in the Senckenberg Museum before its own institute building was inaugurated in 1924. With its cubic shape and flat roof, it gave the impression of solidity and asceticism. Siegfried Kracauer described it as having an “earnest, almost fortress-like character”. The location of the practice rooms, study cells, staircases, and the book depository was made recognisable by different window formats. This symbolic transparency was intended to emphasise the objectivity and independence of the institute, which became the nucleus of the Frankfurt School and its Critical Theory. It characterised the city’s leftist and liberal atmosphere, thus contributing to the successful development of the Neues Frankfurt. After its destruction in the Second World War, it was replaced by a new building diagonally across in 1951 (Senckenberganlage 26).

Construction period: 1924

Architect: Franz Roeckle
