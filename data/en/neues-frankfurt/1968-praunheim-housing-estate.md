# Praunheim housing estate

*Am Ebelfeld 259*

![](../../images/f6ee550138a0689403da6acc77000ad1_original.jpg)

Located to the west of the Praunheim town centre, the housing estate was part of the Nidda valley project and served as a testing ground for new construction methods. The development, divided into three phases, extends in an east-west direction along the Nidda river. The so-called Frankfurt assembly method with pumice concrete slabs was used in the second and third construction units. In the third unit, small flats along Ludwig-Landmann-Straße (formerly Hindenburgallee) were equipped with the fold-up “Frankfurt bed”. Between the rows of flats, there were strictly organised tenant gardens that embodied the ideal of standardised self-sufficiency. Hans Leistikow’s colour scheme combined the striking white of the housing estate with colourful accents on doors and windows, as well as with deliberately highlighted building elements in red and blue. Another special feature of the housing estate can be found in the building located at Am Ebelfeld 263-277, which was designed by Anton Brenner, with access balconies and micro-apartments (around 50 square metres) for families. Floor plans that were arranged in staggered storeys allow for rooms of different heights. In the cross-section, this results in a honeycomb-like structure. In 1930, the church of Christ the King (Christ-König-Kirche), designed by Martin Weber, was built at Damaschkeanger 156.

Construction management: City of Frankfurt am Main

Construction period: 1927–1929

Architects: Ernst May, Herbert Boehm, Wolfgang Bangert (planning); Ernst May, Eugen Kaufmann, Margarete Schütte-Lihotzky (architectural adaptation)

Horticultural design: Leberecht Migge

Further reading: Heike Risse: Frühe Moderne in Frankfurt am Main 1920–1933. Frankfurt am Main 1984, p. 262-271.

DW Dreysse: May-Siedlungen. Architekturführer durch zehn Siedlungen des Neuen Frankfurt 1926-1930. 4th ed., Köln 2019, p. 8-13.
