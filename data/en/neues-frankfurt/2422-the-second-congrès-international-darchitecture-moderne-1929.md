# The second Congrès International d’Architecture Moderne, 1929

*Ludwig Erhard Anlage 1, 60327 Frankfurt am Main*

![](../../images/2aebff7ee20a214d48f6f69cb593b5a4_original.jpg)

In 1928, CIAM (Congrès International d’Architecture Moderne) was founded in La Sarraz, Switzerland, with the aim of bringing together the international avant-garde of architecture. At the end of 1929, Frankfurt hosted the second congress – at the invitation of Ernst May, who had already attended the founding meeting.

From 24 to 26 October, eminent architects, including Walter Gropius, Le Corbusier, and Victor Bourgeois, met at the Gesellschaftshaus Palmengarten to discuss the topic of “the apartment for the breadline”. Against the backdrop of the economic crisis and housing shortages, the question was how to make living space available not as a privilege but as a common good. Floor plan designs, high-rise and low-rise construction, new materials, as well as the social responsibility of architecture were all up for debate.

For an accompanying exhibition, the Frankfurt building department sent questionnaires to 18 countries in order to collect standardised floor plans for small apartments. The plans were standardised, anonymised, and displayed on around a hundred panels at the Werkbundhaus. Under the direction of Mart Stam, who was also responsible for the Hellerhof housing estate, the exhibition provided an overview of the state of international housing research, attracting over 3,000 visitors and receiving widespread attention in the press and radio. It was subsequently presented in several European cities.

The monthly magazine “Das Neue Frankfurt” devoted an entire issue to the topic of the congress in its November edition. Thanks to high attendance and international resonance, the congress was considered a success, even though May would have wished for binding resolutions.
