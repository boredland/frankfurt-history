# Leinwandhaus

*Weckmarkt 17*

Reiffensteins Frankfurt<br />(1820-1893)

![](../../images/f02fb3c10523eb4d30795951dae487f9_original.png)

The Leinwandhaus – the Canvas House – was built in the late 14th century on the site of three houses in the old Jewish quarter. For a time from 1419, the Leinwandhaus functioned as a prison. However, even by the later 15th century, it had become a popular place for private festivities. Reiffenstein sketched it with its four corner towers, protected walkway and battlements.

The Leinwandhaus was named after the merchants trading there with canvas, an important source of income for the city well into the 18th century. The merchants had to measure their canvas on the iron “elle” yardstick on the façade. This was a compulsory practice as the length of an “elle” varied in other cities. In Frankfurt, for instance, an “elle” was 54.73 centimetres, but in Nuremberg it was 65.65 centimetres.

The interior of the Leinwandhaus burnt out in the Second World War. The building was only reconstructed in the 1980s. Since 2008, it has been home to Caricatura Museum, dedicated to comic art.
