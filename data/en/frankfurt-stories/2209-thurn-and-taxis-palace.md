# Thurn and Taxis Palace

*Große Eschenheimer Straße 10*

Frankfurt places of mobility

![Johann Michael Eben (1716–1761), Thurn und Taxis Palace, 19th century, HMF.C01274, public domain](../../images/57ccdf7e9b89ae83805b283f7ff4467d_original.png)
*18th century — Author: Johann Michael Eben | License: Gemeinfrei; HMF, Foto: Ursula Seitz Gray | Description: Johann Michael Eben (1716–1761), Thurn und Taxis Palace, 19th century, HMF.C01274, public domain*

The Thurn und Taxis Palace was built until 1739 by Robert de Cotte on behalf of the family of the same name. The magnificent Baroque building represented the wealth and power of the family, who originally came from Italy.

*Author: Sarah Roller*

At the end of the 15th century, they were commissioned by the Holy Roman Emperor to establish an organized postal service. Since the late Middle Ages, people in Europe had been using the postal service for communication and travel. The Imperial Postal Service provided routes, timetables, carriages, and stations. The postal service made it possible to travel from Frankfurt to the central cities of Europe. However, tickets were so expensive that only a few could afford to use the stagecoach.

*Author: Sarah Roller*

The “Fahrpost”, which was used for travel, was housed in the Thurn und Taxis Palace until 1841. The atmosphere and hustle and bustle of the arriving and departing stagecoaches can best be compared today to a large train station. The family itself did not live in this building for long; they left the city only a few years after its completion. Until its destruction in 1944, the palace was used repeatedly by the postal service. In 2004, reconstruction began on this site. Since 1841, the Fahrpost had been located behind the palace in the “Rotes Haus” (red house) on the Zeil. Today, a large shopping center stands there.

*Author: Sarah Roller*
