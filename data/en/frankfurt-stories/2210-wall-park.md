# Wall Park

*Bockenheimer Anlage*

Frankfurt places of mobility

![Matthäus Merian d. Ä. (1593-1650), Updated edition of the large city map of Frankfurt with ramparts, Frankfurt around 1761, HMF.C01215, public domain](../../images/02b37e244b30b52527cea901ed0d38a7_original.png)
*Around 1761 — Author: Matthäus Merian d. Ä. | License: Gemeinfrei, HMF | Description: Matthäus Merian d. Ä. (1593-1650), Updated edition of the large city map of Frankfurt with ramparts, Frankfurt around 1761, HMF.C01215, public domain*

From 1804 onwards, Frankfurt underwent a rapid transformation from a medieval city to an open urban landscape. The old fortifications were removed as they had proven useless. Starting in 1807, city gardener Sebastian Rinz redesigned the former bastions into an English park, which was created between 1804 and 1812 as Frankfurt's first public park and was particularly inviting for strolling between Eschenheimer and Bockenheimer Warte. However, the pond that is located here today was only created as a fire-fighting water reservoir during World War II.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

The Kurhäuschen was opened in 1817 in the middle of the Bockenheimer Anlage. Until 1875, promenaders could buy mineral water and milk here.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

The citizens financed the demolition of the fortress ring by purchasing the land and participating in the construction work. The plots of land could only be built on towards the city side, and gardens had to be laid out on the outskirts, which blended in perfectly with the public green space. The rules for development were laid down in the so-called Wallservitut. The municipal law is still in force today, so that the wall parks still form a green ring around the city center.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

Walking developed into a cultural technique and established itself as a bourgeois practice. As part of bourgeois distinction in the 18th and 19th centuries, walking in urban areas took on a new meaning and was upgraded. Walking offered many social classes greater freedom of movement. Women in particular were able to expand their radius.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*
