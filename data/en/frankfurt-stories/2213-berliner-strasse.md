# Berliner Straße

*Frankfurt places of mobility*

![Kurt Röhrig (1912-2007), An der Paulskirche (today: Berliner Straße), Frankfurt after 1952, HMF.Ph16641](../../images/914df86b89b7bf37ff6e20f9686b40e5_original.png)
*After 1952 — Author: Kurt Röhrig | License: HMF, roebild | Description: Kurt Röhrig (1912-2007), An der Paulskirche (today: Berliner Straße), Frankfurt after 1952, HMF.Ph16641*

The rise in mass motorization in the post-war period changed society. From the mid-1950s onwards, the automobile became a symbol of economic growth, and more and more people were able to fulfill their dream of owning their own vehicle. Europe's historic city centers were not prepared for the rapid increase in motor vehicle traffic. Frankfurt was particularly affected by this; at the end of the 1950s, the city had the highest car density in the Federal Republic of Germany. As a result of the 1962 traffic master plan, new road connections have been created and lanes have been widened to accommodate the predicted increase in car traffic.

*Author: Ilyas Chhima, Sarah Roller*

Berliner Straße, built in 1952/1953, followed the original street layout only to a limited extent and otherwise ran across the old buildings. This new east-west connection, 750 meters long, was built between the Schauspielhaus and the Dominican monastery. The street with a width of 18 meters allowed for two lanes to be created. Berliner Straße was a central traffic artery, especially for inner-city traffic. The dense network of asphalt roads since the mid-20th century seals the ground and brings exhaust fumes, noise, and space-consuming cars into the city.

*Author: Ilyas Chhima, Sarah Roller*
