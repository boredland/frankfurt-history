# Frankfurt Central Station

*Frankfurt places of mobility*

![Carl Hertel (1832–1906), Frankfurt Central Station with undeveloped surroundings, Frankfurt, 1896, HMF.C29184, public domain](../../images/84721ba4a4ce59e094b6a605bb5c2fb2_original.png)
*1896 — Author: Carl Hertel | License: Gemeinfrei, HMF | Description: Carl Hertel (1832–1906), Frankfurt Central Station with undeveloped surroundings, Frankfurt, 1896, HMF.C29184, public domain*

Shortly after the first German railroad between Nuremberg and Fürth opened in 1835, Frankfurt already began planning its own line. The Taunus-Bahn opened in 1839, with its first connection running between Frankfurt and Höchst. In the following years, the Main-Neckar and Main-Weser stations were built on the western edge of the city, connecting Frankfurt with Heidelberg and Kassel. The three adjacent stations were located about 600 meters before today's Central Station, where the Bahnhofs- and Gallusviertel are located.

*Author: Sarah Roller*

The success of the railroad soon pushed the early stations to their capacity limits. As a result, large main stations were built after 1871, especially in the big cities. In Frankfurt, the Central Station opened in 1888, and the old western stations made way for the growing city. The side of the Central Station's facade facing the city was decorated with sculptures. The platform hall was an ultra-modern structure made of iron and glass, with three naves and 18 tracks. When it opened, Frankfurt Central Station was the largest station in Europe. The building impressed new arrivals and Frankfurt residents alike as the new “city gate.”

*Author: Sarah Roller*

The railroad revolutionized the mobility of the population. Previously, travel was limited, and only a few could afford not to walk. In Frankfurt, five million people used the new Central Station in its opening year. Twenty years later, this number had increased more than tenfold. Most travelers were found in third and fourth class. The railroad thus became the first means of mass transportation.

*Author: Sarah Roller*
