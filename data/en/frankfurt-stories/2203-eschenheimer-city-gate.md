# Eschenheimer City Gate

*Frankfurt places of mobility*

![Georg Braun (1541/42–1622)/Franz Hogenberg (1535–1590), Excerpt showing the Eschenheimer Tower from the city map of Frankfurt am Main, 1574, HMF.C06902, public domain](../../images/db0527f0cfd4ff84d42ec0718e4a864d_original.png)
*1574 — Author: Georg Braun; Franz Hogenberg | License: Gemeinfrei; HMF, Foto: Horst Ziegenfusz | Description: Georg Braun (1541/42–1622)/Franz Hogenberg (1535–1590), Excerpt showing the Eschenheimer Tower from the city map of Frankfurt am Main, 1574, HMF.C06902, public domain*

The Eschenheimer Tower and City Gate were completed in 1428 and formed part of the northern section of Frankfurt's fortifications. The gate provided merchants with access to the important “Hoher Straße,” a trade route from Cologne to Leipzig. With the demolition of the medieval city walls, it lost its function as a defensive tower. Until the early 19th century, the Eschenheimer Gate and the other city gates were the primary means of entering and leaving the city. They were locked in the evening.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

In der Vormärzzeit wurde der Wunsch der Bürger*innen nach mehr Freiheit auch im öffentlichen Raum immer größer. In der Folge wehrte sich die Bevölkerung zunehmend gegen die einschränkenden Regulierungen durch die Stadtregierung. Besonders die seit Mitte des 18. Jahrhunderts geltende städtische Anordnung, nach 21 Uhr an den Stadttoren eine Gebühr für den Einlass zu zahlen, löste so lautstarke Proteste aus, dass sie wieder zurückgenommen werden musste. Die 1831 stattfindenden Proteste gingen als „Sperrbatzenkrawall“ in die Geschichte ein.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

Today, the Eschenheimer City Gate stands as a relic of medieval Frankfurt, seemingly lost amid busy traffic routes.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

Jan Kamensky's “Visual Utopia” shows that there is another way. The Hamburg-based artist combined the past, present, and his idea of an utopian future using the example of the Eschenheimer Tower. His goal is not to depict “the” utopia, but to change our viewing habits. By distancing ourselves from what we are used to, our view of the present is to be sharpened. This “Visual Utopia” allows us to recognize that the world could be different.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*
