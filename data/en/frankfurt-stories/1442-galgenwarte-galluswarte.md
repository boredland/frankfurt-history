# Galgenwarte / Galluswarte

*Mainzer Landstraße*

Reiffensteins Frankfurt<br />(1820-1893)

![Carl Theodor Reiffenstein, Galgenwarte, view from the west, 1856/1857, watercolored pencil drawing, HMF RS0100](../../images/b23c4ace37e0a196f52b00189e761487_original.jpg)
*1856/1857 — Author: Carl Theodor Reiffenstein | License: HMF | Description: Carl Theodor Reiffenstein, Galgenwarte, view from the west, 1856/1857, watercolored pencil drawing, HMF RS0100*

In the 14th and 15th centuries, a raised border defence, known as a landwehr, was added to the city’s fortifications. The landwehr border was established some distance in advance of the city walls. The raised border had a total of five watch-towers set on the main roads, guarding the city in case of attack.

The Gallow’s watch tower (today’s Galluswarte tower on the eponymous square) was named after its site – a former gallows field. Just like the three other surviving watch towers, it was a high tower set in a walled courtyard. The courtyard had stables, sheds, a dwelling house and a well. In 1880, Reiffenstein described the former peace of this place, lost with the growth of the railways.

When the city’s fortifications were demolished, the watch towers were left standing. Today, the Gallow’s tower – now Galluswarte – stands at the centre of busy traffic junction on the Mainzer Landstrasse.

*Author: Carina Koch*
