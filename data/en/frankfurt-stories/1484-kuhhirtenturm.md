# Kuhhirtenturm

*Große Rittergasse 118*

Reiffensteins Frankfurt<br />(1820-1893)

![](../../images/d96625530f32d9a439412532a5d3ac1c_original.png)

The Kuhhirtenturm – Cowherds’ Tower – was a fortified tower in Sachsenhausen’s city wall. It was built in 1390 on the southern bank of the River Main. Since the tower stood next to Paradise Lane, it was also known as the Paradise Gate until the 19th century. Due to the tower’s size, it was also nicknamed the “Elephant”.

Its three massive floors were faced with a blue lava rock known as Bockenheimer natural stone. A half-timbered room at the top was for a guard who could sound the alarm in case of fire.

In 1878, Reiffenstein wrote about the demolition of the city fortifications at the tower: “The entire area is being changed entirely”. Only afterwards did Sachsenhausen cowherds actually live in the tower.

Badly damaged in the Second World War, the tower was later rebuilt. Since 2011, it has been home to an exhibition on the life and work of the composer Paul Hindemith (1895-1963) who lived in the tower from 1923 to 1927.
