# Zeil

*Frankfurt places of mobility*

![Carl Friedrich Mylius (1827–1916), View of the Zeil with the tracks of the horse-drawn railroad, around 1880, HMF.Ph19777, public domain](../../images/76b2d318d75d6b753f0c2caafa1cd50c_original.png)
*Around 1880 — Author: Carl Friedrich Mylius | License: Gemeinfrei, HMF | Description: Carl Friedrich Mylius (1827–1916), View of the Zeil with the tracks of the horse-drawn railroad, around 1880, HMF.Ph19777, public domain*

The Zeil has been part of the city since 1333 and served as a cattle market for a long time. The name probably derives from the first row of houses built there in the 14th century. With the construction of increasingly magnificent houses for the bourgeoisie, the Zeil became a boulevard. From the 19th century onwards, it increasingly transformed from a residential street into a shopping street. In 1881, the Zeil was extended to become the “new Zeil” with the construction of a new passageway from Konstablerwache to Friedberger Anlage.

*Author: Sarah Roller*

The Zeil as a pedestrian zone – most Frankfurt residents cannot imagine it any other way. But it was not until the 1970s that Frankfurt's shopping street was closed to car and streetcar traffic. The conversion of the Zeil into a pedestrian zone had been under discussion since 1963. There was considerable resistance among the population. Many feared rent increases for shops and traffic chaos in the surrounding streets. Before it was closed, the Zeil was a busy street and, together with Berliner Straße, was one of the most important east-west connections for cars in the city center. The danger to shoppers, the exhaust fumes, and the noise were arguments in favor of converting the Zeil into a pedestrian zone.

*Author: Sarah Roller*

Starting in 1973, the Zeil was initially closed on a trial basis and car traffic was diverted via the Cityring. The closure was planned while the underground and suburban railways were being built under the Zeil. In 1978, after more than 100 years of rail transport, the streetcars ran through the Zeil for the last time. The Zeil became a construction site in the 1980s for the construction of the local transport tunnels, with the pedestrian zone being implemented using temporary measures. On May 28, 1983, the city officially opened the pedestrian zone. Soon after its introduction, it became clear that fears of traffic chaos and unprofitable businesses were largely unfounded.

*Author: Sarah Roller*
