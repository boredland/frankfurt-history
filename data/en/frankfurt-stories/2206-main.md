# Main

*Mainkai*

Frankfurt places of mobility

![Uwe Dettmar, Excavated remains of the Staufer harbor, around 2012, HMF](../../images/3a484f47cc6540f53010f9285c0958b4_original.png)
*2012 — Author: Uwe Dettmar | License: HMF, Foto: Uwe Dettmar | Description: Uwe Dettmar, Excavated remains of the Staufer harbor, around 2012, HMF*

The Main River is the oldest and was for a long time the most important “road” to and from Frankfurt. The oldest remains of a port were found during construction work in 2012 at the site of the Historical Museum and date back to the Staufer period.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

The market ship between Frankfurt and Mainz was the earliest regular transport link from Frankfurt. It transported people, animals, and goods since 1105. Upstream, the ship was hauled by horses in the ford or on the riverbank. The Main and its Frankfurt ports were central transshipment and trading centers for goods and commodities of all kinds. The city's tax office used to be located where the Historical Museum stands today. The Rententurm still exists today. Arriving ships unloaded and loaded their goods here and paid customs duties.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

From 1828, the paddle steamer “Stadt Frankfurt” commuted between Frankfurt and Mainz as a passenger ship. However, there were many technical problems. Before it was straightened, the Main was too shallow in many places – the stagecoach was faster on this route. In 1832, the steamer was sold to Cologne.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*

Hardly any other street has sparked as much controversy in recent years as the nördliche Uferstraße. The Mainkai is closed to through traffic during events. Since 2019, it has been transformed into a car-free zone with many non-commercial offerings for extended periods of time.

*Author: Nina Gorgus, Sarah Roller*
